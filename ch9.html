Visualization Logic: The top diamond captures the "OR, not AND" structure of authority — express and apparent authority are independent paths to the same destination (principal bound), and neither needs the other to exist. The lower diamond is where the chapter's cited case (Pacific National Leasing v. An agent loudly insisting "don't worry, I'm authorized to sign this" creates ZERO apparent authority on its own — the representation has to trace back to something the principal said, did, or permitted (even passively, by appointing the agent into a role where having that authority would seem natural). The bottom box captures the consequence that actually punishes a rogue agent: if neither authority type exists, the THIRD PARTY isn't left empty-handed — the agent who falsely held themselves out becomes personally bound to honor the deal instead of the principal.

2. Imputed Knowledge — When the Agent's Knowledge Becomes the Principal's

This diagram isolates a rule that's easy to forget exists at all: a principal can be legally deemed to "know" something they never personally learned, simply because their agent knew it.

Visualization Logic: The two channel boxes matter because they're the ONLY two routes the chapter recognizes for imputed knowledge — knowledge an agent picks up entirely outside both of these channels (purely personal, off-duty knowledge with no connection to formal notice or the agent's actual job duties) would NOT automatically transfer to the principal. The "Deemed" box at the bottom is the practical sting of this doctrine: a principal cannot defend a claim by genuinely, honestly saying "I personally never knew that" if their agent knew it through proper notice or through the agent's normal duties — the law simply doesn't care that the knowledge never physically reached the principal's own awareness, it treats the agent's knowledge as legally equivalent to the principal's own.

Visualization Logic: The direction diamond is the single most important thing to check when reading any indemnity clause, because the two branches produce nearly opposite real-world effects despite looking grammatically similar on the page. The standard direction is almost redundant with what common law already provides — each party already bears responsibility for their own negligence, so this version of the clause mostly just makes that pre-existing rule explicit and adds remedies like covering legal costs. The flipped direction is structurally different and much riskier: it turns the indemnitor into something like an insurance policy for the OTHER side's mistakes, which is precisely why some American courts have refused to enforce such clauses on public policy grounds — the worry being that if you know someone else will pay for YOUR negligence, you have less incentive to be careful in the first place.

A short but high-value rule connects directly to diagram 3: indemnity clauses are a one-way ratchet in terms of what they can do to liability that already exists at law.

Visualization Logic: This asymmetry is worth holding as a standalone rule because it's counterintuitive in one specific direction — people often assume a contractual clause can cut both ways (expand OR limit liability) with equal ease, but indemnity clauses default to ONE-DIRECTIONAL ratcheting. They readily ADD exposure (an indemnity not limited to "contractor negligence" can sweep in design errors too, expanding what the contractor is on the hook for), but they do NOT subtract from what already exists unless the clause goes out of its way to say so explicitly — many standard forms (like CCDC 2's GC 1.3.1) even include language reinforcing that the contract's remedies are "in addition to" rather than a replacement for existing legal rights.

Visualization Logic: The two branches under "Quote" represent the contractor's fork in the road, and the diagram is built to show that the SAFEST path for a contractor is the reservation branch — but only if the owner actually agrees to accept it. The cumulative-impact rationale (100 small unreserved changes potentially adding up to 10%+ of contract value) is exactly why both sides care so much about which of these paths gets used.

The pricing-method difference at the bottom (lump sum vs cost-plus) isn't incidental — it follows logically from the timing: you can only commit to a firm lump sum once you actually know and agree on the price, whereas a directive that starts work before pricing is settled almost has to default to cost-plus, since there's no other way to compensate fairly for work whose final scope and price aren't yet pinned down.

Visualization Logic: The "SoundsGood" diamond is placed deliberately to mirror the trap a reader falls into on first hearing the facts — 13 visits against a contracted 10 LOOKS like over-performance, and that's exactly the wrong intuition this diagram is built to correct. The real legal test only emerges once you check WHEN those visits happened: clustering reviews at the very beginning (before meaningful work existed to inspect) and the very end (after walls were already closed, hiding everything) means the reviews largely missed the actual WINDOW during which problems could have been caught and corrected. The RiskFactors branch adds a second dimension — the court didn't just fault generic timing, it specifically pointed to known risk signals (missing as-built drawings, known aggressive/corrosive water conditions) that should have triggered HEIGHTENED vigilance, and faulted the engineer for instead leaning on the owner's and contractor's own staff rather than independently confirming the work. The BudgetNote at the bottom is a separate, standalone principle worth remembering on its own: professionals can't defend inadequate reviews by pointing to what they were paid for — the professional standard of care is owed regardless of contract economics, meaning if the appropriate fee isn't available, the right move is to decline the engagement or absorb the extra review cost personally, not to quietly under-deliver.
