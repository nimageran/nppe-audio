The 5 Elements of a Contract
Click to flip
The Rule: To be legally binding, a contract MUST have all five: 1. Offer 2. Acceptance 3. Consideration 4. Capacity 5. Lawful Purpose.

The Catch: If even one is missing, the contract simply does not exist.
O-A-C-C-L
Offer vs. Invitation to Treat
Click to flip
The Story: You see an ad selling a generator for $10. You walk in and say "I accept!"

The Rule: They do not have to sell it to you. Advertisements, catalogs, and store displays are NOT legal offers. They are "Invitations to Treat" (requests for YOU to make an offer).
Ads Are Not Offers
Revoking an Offer
Click to flip
The Story: A contractor says "My quote is good for 30 days." Two days later, he changes his mind and cancels it.

The Rule: This is perfectly legal. An offer can be revoked ANYTIME before acceptance, even if they promised to keep it open.
Promises Are Cheap
The Option Contract
Click to flip
The Catch: How do you legally force someone to keep an offer open?

The Rule: You must buy an "Option." If you pay them $1 to keep the offer open for 30 days, it creates a mini-contract. Now they cannot revoke it.
Options Cost Money
The Mirror Image Rule
Click to flip
The Story: Someone offers to sell you a truck for $5k. You say "I accept, but wash it first."

The Rule: You did not accept. You destroyed their offer and created a Counter-Offer. Acceptance MUST perfectly mirror the original offer.
Change a Word, Kill the Offer
The Postal Acceptance Rule
Click to flip
The Trap: You mail a signed contract on Tuesday. It arrives on Thursday.

The Rule: The contract became legally binding the exact moment it was dropped in the mailbox on Tuesday, regardless of when it is received.
The Mailbox Trap
What is "Consideration"?
Click to flip
The Basics: The "price" paid for a promise. It is the exchange of value (money, goods, or doing something you didn't have to do).

The Rule: A "Gratuitous Promise" (promising to do something for free) is completely unenforceable in court because it lacks consideration.
No Exchange = No Contract
The "Peppercorn" Theory
Click to flip
The Story: You sell a $1 Million factory to your friend for $1.

The Rule: The court will enforce this contract. Consideration only has to exist; it does NOT have to be fair. The court does not protect you from making a bad deal.
Courts Do Not Protect Fools
Equitable Estoppel
Click to flip
The Trap: You tell a contractor, "Don't worry about the deadline, take an extra week for free." Later, you try to sue them for being late.

The Rule: The court will "estop" (block) you from enforcing your strict contract rights because you made a promise they relied on, and it would be unfair to change your mind.
You Cannot Go Back On Your Word
Capacity (Minors & Drunks)
Click to flip
The Rule: Minors, intoxicated people, and the mentally incompetent lack the capacity to contract.

The Catch: If a minor signs a contract, it is voidable by the minor, but strictly binding on the adult.
Protects the Weak
Statute of Frauds
Click to flip
The Basics: Verbal contracts are generally 100% legal.

The Exception: The Statute of Frauds says certain contracts MUST be in writing to be enforced. This includes buying land and guaranteeing someone else's debt.
Land & Guarantees = Ink Required
Privity of Contract
Click to flip
The Story: Owner A hires General Contractor B. Contractor B hires Subcontractor C.

The Rule: Owner A CANNOT sue Subcontractor C for breach of contract, because they never signed a contract together. Only parties to a contract can sue on it.
Strangers Cannot Sue
Void vs. Voidable
Click to flip
Void: The contract never legally existed (e.g., a contract to commit a crime). It is dead immediately.

Voidable: The contract is valid, but one party has the option to cancel it (e.g., a contract signed because of a lie or signed by a minor).
Void = Dead | Voidable = Optional
