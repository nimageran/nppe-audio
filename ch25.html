Visualization Logic: The structure here is deliberately messy because the REAL system is messy — there's no single national securities regulator in Canada, which is precisely why the "multiple rules can govern a single transaction" problem exists at all. The CSA box is positioned as a coordination layer, not a top-down authority, which matters for a subtle reason: a National Instrument has no independent legal force in a province that hasn't adopted it, yet the bottom box shows it still matters practically, because courts treat it as the baseline of "what a reasonably careful professional would have done" even where it was never formally legislated — the same "minimum practice standard" logic from Chapter 13's codes-and-standards material applies here directly.

Visualization Logic: The diamond labeled "Is disclosure now complete?" is placed deliberately right after the moment most people would assume the job is done — the chapter explicitly opens chapter question 1 with exactly this misconception, and the answer is a hard no. Once a company crosses into "approved and selling," the obligation doesn't switch off, it switches MODE — from a one-time comprehensive document to an ongoing stream of updates anytime something material changes. The four boxes under "Continuing" aren't four separate optional tasks; they're four different TRIGGERS for the same underlying duty (keep the market accurately informed), which is why an engineer or geoscientist involved in any one of these — financial statements, press releases, material change disclosures — needs to understand that their technical sign-off responsibility doesn't end when the prospectus is approved.

Visualization Logic: The diagram splits into two halves that test two different things and are easy to conflate. The top half (Gate → Pass) is a credentials check — relevant experience PLUS good professional standing, both required, and notice "relevant" is doing real work in G1: a geoscientist with 5 years of unrelated experience doesn't qualify, the experience has to match the specific subject matter of THIS project and report. The bottom half is about ONGOING CONDUCT once qualified — inspect personally (no relying entirely on someone else's fieldwork), sign and seal (personal professional accountability), sign consent (an explicit no-misrepresentation promise), and act independently (not as an advocate for the company's preferred narrative). The "Who" box on the side exists to correct a common misreading: even though the QP does all the technical work, the company's OFFICERS AND DIRECTORS are the ones legally responsible for choosing a properly qualified person and for using that person's output correctly — accountability for the SYSTEM sits above the technical contributor.

Visualization Logic: The reason this is drawn as a matrix rather than a list is that the SAME piece of work (one technical report) can simultaneously trigger multiple independent liability routes, and each route has a DIFFERENT relationship to disclaimers — which is the detail most likely to be tested. The common law route (misrepresentation or negligent preparation) treats the National Instruments as evidence of the minimum standard, not as a rule that itself creates liability — a subtle distinction that matters because it means even in a jurisdiction that never formally adopted NI 43-101, a court can still use it to judge whether the work was negligent. The statutory route is structurally different and strictly worse for the professional from a defence standpoint: it's a freestanding cause of action created directly by securities legislation, completely independent of common law negligence, and the chapter is explicit that this kind of liability CANNOT be disclaimed away under any circumstances — making disclaimers essentially useless as a defence anywhere in this chapter's territory, not just weak.

Visualization Logic: The first diamond is the obvious gate everyone expects — confidential information cannot be shared with or acted on by anyone outside the obligation, full stop, whether directly (telling teammates) or indirectly (a spouse trading on your behalf). The second diamond is the one people get wrong, and it's exactly why this diagram exists: becoming public does NOT mean the insider trading rules switch off completely — it shifts the test from "is this shared at all" to "are you ADDING anything beyond what was actually released." Confirming a fact that's already public (a positive test result happened) is fine; layering on extra emphasis, qualitative spin, or non-verbal signals about information that ISN'T yet public — even while technically discussing something "already disclosed" — crosses straight back into illegal territory. The practical habit this should build: after a public release, mentally rehearse exactly what words were used in the official disclosure, and never go beyond that wording, no matter how harmless an extra adjective might feel.
