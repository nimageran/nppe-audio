8 PIPEDA Obligations for Organizations8 items

1
Protect personal information
2

3
Obtain CONSENT prior to collection
4
Collect only REQUIRED information
5
Limit USE to the purpose for which it was collected
6
Ensure information is ACCURATE
7
Ensure ADEQUATE SAFEGUARDS are in place
8
Allow individuals easy ACCESS to their information and the org's privacy policy
Scope: PIPEDA is FEDERAL and governs private-sector organizations' collection/use/disclosure of personal information. Privacy legislation does NOT apply to information about CORPORATIONS — only individuals. PIPEDA does NOT apply to collection for domestic, journalistic, artistic, or literary purposes.

Enforcement: The PIPEDA Privacy Commissioner decides complaints, can impose fines, and can conduct compliance AUDITS. PIPEDA also creates a RIGHT OF ACTION for individuals to sue an organization for breach of its PIPEDA obligations.

2 Categories of Provincial Privacy Legislation2 items
▾
1
"Privacy Act" Type — Breach as a TORT— creates a private right to sue for violation of privacy; typically brief statutes (e.g. Saskatchewan's Privacy Act: only 11 sections — "It is a tort, actionable without proof of damage, for a person wilfully and without a claim of right, to violate the privacy of another.")
2
"PIPA" Type — Breach as a QUASI-CRIMINAL Offence— Personal Information Protection Act-style statutes, similar in structure to PIPEDA, can carry serious penalties
All provinces/territories have privacy legislation covering collection, access, and correction of personal information, each with a designated COMMISSIONER or OMBUDSPERSON for complaints. Some provinces (e.g. BC) have BOTH a Privacy Act AND a PIPA.
BC PIPA case (Cruz Ventures / Wild Coyote Club): Demanding customer information NOT NECESSARY for business operations — and collecting/retaining it — was found to BREACH PIPA, even without further misuse.
4
"Reasonable Expectation of Privacy" Testkey principle
▾
Provincial courts often resolve privacy disputes by asking whether the complainant had a REASONABLE EXPECTATION OF PRIVACY in the circumstances. Example (Richardson v. Davis Wire): An employer's use of a hidden camera in a lunchroom shared with other employees was found NOT to violate privacy — the employee had no reasonable expectation of privacy in that shared space.

Anti-Spam Legislation (CASL) — Key Rules4 items
▾
1
Express or implied CONSENT required— to send commercial electronic messages
2
Content requirements— must include sufficient info to allow the recipient to readily CONTACT the sender
3
Prohibits causing ANOTHER person's computer to send messages
4
Exceptions— messages between friends/family relations; product recall notices; existing employment relationships; and certain other matters
Penalties are severe: Up to $1 MILLION for individuals and up to $10 MILLION for corporations. Take extreme care before sending unsolicited electronic messages to parties with NO existing relationship — get legal advice if there's any doubt.
