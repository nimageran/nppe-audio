Chapter 19 — Delay and Impact Claims
Lists, types & categories only
🔍
1
3 Core Definitions3 items
▾
1
Delay— an event that causes extended time to complete all or part of a project
2
Impact / Disruption— effect of an event that detrimentally affects a project but MAY OR MAY NOT extend completion time
3
Acceleration— increase in labour/equipment that SHORTENS completion time or mitigates the effects of an impact/delay
2
2 Types of Project Schedules2 items
▾
1
Bar Chart— each activity gets a start/end date. Simple, but does NOT show interrelationships/dependencies between activities.
2
Network Diagram (Critical Path Method / CPM)— links activities to show dependencies (e.g. excavation must finish before piles can be poured); identifies the most efficient sequence
Float = time contingency in an activity (can be delayed without delaying final completion). Critical Path = the series of activities with ZERO float — any delay to these delays the whole project.
3
3 Types of Delay3 items
▾
1
Compensable Delay— one party is CONTRACTUALLY entitled to recover damages from the delaying party (e.g. CCDC 2 GC 6.5.1/6.5.2 for owner-caused delays)
2
Excusable Delay— not attributable to any party's fault; entitles a TIME EXTENSION; may or may not also be compensable (e.g. force majeure)
3
Contractor-Caused Delay— caused by the contractor or those it's responsible for; almost ALWAYS non-excusable AND non-compensable
Critical path NOT required under CCDC 2: CCDC 2 does not distinguish between critical-path and non-critical-path delays for compensation purposes — compensation can apply even to a delay that doesn't push out the completion date (though entitlement to a TIME EXTENSION for non-critical-path delays is less clear, since extensions must be "reasonable").
Notice requirement: CCDC 2 requires notice within 10 working days of a delay's commencement to claim a TIME EXTENSION. However, this notice bar does NOT remove the owner's separate obligation to REIMBURSE COSTS — entitlement to extension and to reimbursement are separate rights.
4
Concurrent Delays — Key Principle1 rule
▾
Concurrent delays = two or more independent delays (compensable, excusable, and/or contractor-caused) occur simultaneously and affect the same activities. Apportioning responsibility becomes difficult/impossible. Result: absent a contrary clause, NEITHER party can recover damages from the other for the period of concurrency — courts are reluctant to award contractor compensation OR enforce owner liquidated damages during concurrency, because each party can argue lack of causation ("but for" my fault, the delay would have happened anyway due to the other party).
5
No-Damages-for-Delay Clauses — 3 Rules of Interpretation3 items
▾
1
Strict construction against the drafter— words exempting a party from liability for its OWN fault are construed strictly against that party
2
Duty to facilitate— a party for whom work is being done has a duty to FACILITATE that work; the clause cannot become an obstruction to the other party's work
3
No party "at the mercy" of another— a contract won't be read to let the owner delay the contractor at will without recourse
These clauses do NOT prevent a contractor from seeking a TIME EXTENSION — only damages. Reasonable/well-scoped clauses (e.g. CCDC 2's force majeure exclusion, GC 6.5.3) are generally ENFORCEABLE. Broader clauses attempting to exclude even INTENTIONAL owner-caused delays are the most likely to be struck down — courts require very clear language before depriving a contractor of damages for owner-caused delay.
6
2 Types of Acceleration Claims2 items
▾
1
Directed Acceleration— owner ACKNOWLEDGES a delay occurred and instructs the contractor to take steps to meet the original completion date. Easy to recognize.
2
Constructive Acceleration— owner does NOT acknowledge any delay, REFUSES a valid extension request, and insists on the original completion date — forcing the contractor to accelerate without admission. May entitle the contractor to compensation for acceleration costs.
Acceleration is generally achieved via overtime/additional labour, which can REDUCE efficiency — so acceleration costs can sometimes exceed delay costs.
7
7 Heads of Damage for Delay Claims (Kelson case)7 items
▾
1
Additional labour costs (supervisors and field labourers)
2
Travel and related expenses
3
Costs from lost productivity and loss of profit
4
Additional site and head office overhead— extended overhead is one of the hardest to prove; head office overhead requires formulas
5
Costs of extending warranties and obtaining additional bonding
6
Loss of interest on holdback
7
Costs of paying subcontractors
Loss of profit on OTHER projects = consequential damages, usually too REMOTE to recover — unless proven not too speculative AND reasonably foreseeable by the owner at the time of breach.
8
Documentation Required to Prove a Delay Claimkey items
▾
1
Project schedules— bid schedule (ideally CPM), regularly updated → final "as-built" schedule. If missing, reconstruct from diaries, correspondence, and photographs.
2
Project records— transmittals (deliveries), labour records, correspondence, diaries, meeting minutes, photographs, phone records, invoices
3
Timely written notice to the other party— per contract requirements (e.g. CCDC 2 GC 6.5.4: 10 working days); failure to comply can BAR the claim
4
Productivity / job cost reports— needed for loss-of-productivity claims; must show productivity on UNAFFECTED portions for comparison; must be recorded at frequent, regular intervals to be useful
Claims consultants: Can help analyze and present delay/impact/acceleration claims, but expertise must MATCH the technical subject matter (e.g. earth-moving expert for earth-moving claims) to be admissible/credible. Get legal advice BEFORE a consultant prepares a report — to preserve privilege and direct the work appropriately.
9
2 Approaches to Valuing Delay Claims2 items
▾
1
Total Cost Approach— difference between EXPECTED cost (original estimate) and ACTUAL cost. Claimant argues the gap is due to owner breaches. HIGH RISK: owner need only show SOME of the cost increase came from other causes (contractor error, excusable non-compensable delays) to have the WHOLE claim rejected. Rarely succeeds in Canada — only where individual causes truly cannot be isolated and the claimant bears no responsibility for the increase.
2
Measured Mile Approach— uses an UNAFFECTED portion of the contract as a productivity "yardstick" to compare against the affected portion. Claimant must still prove the breaches and link them to the productivity loss. Can also be used DEFENSIVELY by an owner (showing poor productivity even on unaffected work). Has been used successfully in Canadian courts.
