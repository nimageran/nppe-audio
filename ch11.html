1. Risk Allocation by Payment Method

The chapter's core organizing idea is that payment method ISN'T just about how money flows — it's actually a risk allocation device. This diagram lines up the three core payment-based contract types on a single spectrum to show who absorbs cost risk in each.

Visualization Logic: Reading these three side by side rather than as separate definitions reveals the real pattern: as you move from fixed-price to cost-plus, the RISK OF UNKNOWN COSTS systematically shifts from contractor to owner, while the contractor's INCENTIVE to be efficient moves in the opposite direction. Fixed-price gives the contractor maximum incentive to control costs (they keep the difference) but also maximum exposure if they estimated wrong. Cost-plus removes that exposure entirely, but the chapter is blunt about the tradeoff — a contractor "has no incentive to save money for the owner, other than the prospect of repeat business and a sense of ethics," and may even be incentivized to INFLATE costs if the fee is percentage-based. Unit price sits in the middle because it isolates ONE specific kind of uncertainty (quantity) while still pricing each unit firmly, which is exactly why it's the standard choice for projects like road-building where total volume is uncertain but unit cost is predictable.

Visualization Logic: The diagram is split top-to-bottom into "what naive owners assume" versus "what sophisticated owners understand," because the chapter frames this exact gap as the single biggest practical risk in fixed-price contracting — not the contract TYPE itself, but the MISUNDERSTANDING of what it actually guarantees. The bottom "Choice" diamond captures a genuinely counterintuitive point: forcing a contractor to price every unknowable risk into a fixed number isn't automatically the prudent move, because contractors will reasonably pad pricing for true unknowns (like subsurface conditions), and that padding can exceed the ACTUAL eventual cost of the risk materializing. A sophisticated owner sometimes does better by accepting some risk directly rather than paying a stranger to absorb uncertainty they can't accurately price either.

Visualization Logic: Comparing Option A and Option B side by side, even though they look financially IDENTICAL to the owner on a month-to-month invoice, is exactly how this trap stays hidden until the project ends — the owner's monthly cash outflow doesn't change, but the END STATE is completely different: in one scenario nobody owns anything extra, in the other the contractor walks away with a physical asset the owner essentially paid for. This is the textbook definition of "transfer pricing" risk in a cost-plus context, and the diagram's final box is the real lesson: this isn't really a flaw of cost-plus contracting as a concept, it's a flaw of cost-plus contracting WITHOUT active cost oversight — the fix isn't avoiding cost-plus entirely, it's building in contractual cost-control mechanisms (audit rights, pre-approval for purchases, defined "cost" terms) from the start.

Visualization Logic: The diamond in the middle is the entire concept of "hybrid" made concrete — below the target, the contract behaves like a cost-plus arrangement with a profit-sharing twist (both parties want costs low because both share in the savings); above the target, it behaves exactly like a fixed-price contract (contractor eats the overage, owner's exposure is capped). This explains precisely why the chapter says GMP contracts give owners "the primary advantage of a fixed-price contract" — that's only true ABOVE the target line; below it, the dynamics are genuinely different. The audit-right box at the bottom is the detail that completes the picture: even though GMP looks fixed-price-like from the cap's perspective, the owner still needs cost-plus-style oversight tools (the right to audit cost claims) specifically to verify the contractor isn't manipulating reported costs to inflate its claimed share of "savings."

Visualization Logic: The two "significantly" branches deliberately show that BOTH directions of quantity change create cost problems, just different ones — this is the part of unit pricing people most often get wrong by assuming only a quantity INCREASE could possibly hurt a contractor. A quantity decrease is just as real a risk because fixed costs like mobilization don't shrink proportionally — fewer units means each remaining unit has to absorb a bigger slice of those fixed costs, effectively eating into the contractor's margin even though the per-unit PRICE never changed. The chapter's separate warning about the arena seating example connects directly to this same diagram from the OTHER side: a contractor who inflates a unit price (anticipating they'll need to ADD units) can get burned if the owner instead chooses to DELETE units at that same inflated price, so unit pricing has to be set honestly in BOTH directions, not strategically padded assuming only one direction of change will occur.

The bottom resolution box reflects the chapter's actual answer to its own chapter question on this exact topic: this conflict doesn't make the hybrid model illegal or automatically improper, it just means the owner needs to go in with eyes open and explicitly accept that tradeoff, rather than assuming they're getting the same objective oversight a pure CM arrangement would provide.

Visualization Logic: The diagram deliberately refuses to declare either party simply "right," because the chapter itself doesn't — it explicitly criticizes BOTH extreme positions. The client's "guarantor" expectation fails because true comprehensive guarantee-level inspection is cost-prohibitive and arguably impossible to deliver perfectly even with unlimited budget. But the professional's opposite extreme — claiming sole discretion over review level AND limiting responsibility only to defects actually noticed — is flagged as equally unreasonable, since it would let a professional effectively define away their own liability. The "Middle" box is the chapter's actual prescription, and it doubles as practical drafting advice: this expectation gap is foreseeable and common enough that it should be explicitly negotiated and written into the contract BEFORE signing, rather than left to be litigated after a dispute arises over what "periodic review" or "general conformance" was actually supposed to mean.

The myth box at the bottom is included as its own separate diagram element because it's explicitly called out as a common misconception, and it tests cleanly as a standalone fact: a liquidated damages clause needs no symmetrical bonus clause to remain valid, the "fairness" of including one is a business choice, not a legal requirement.

Visualization Logic: The chain structure makes a point that's easy to lose in prose: passing stage 1 (the clause clearly covers this scenario) is necessary but absolutely not sufficient — a perfectly clear, perfectly applicable clause can still be struck down at stage 2 or stage 3 for reasons that have nothing to do with whether the wording covers the situation. Stage 1 connects directly to the B.G. Linton principle (a clause drafted clearly enough leaves "no doubt about its meaning" and gets full effect, but ambiguity gets construed against whoever drafted it — the contra proferentem rule). Stages 2 and 3 are where the Coles v. Clarenville case actually lives: that clause may well have clearly covered the situation (stage 1 pass), but the court still found ways to avoid enforcing it given the circumstances of a fundamental breach depriving the plaintiff of substantially the whole benefit of the contract — illustrating that clear drafting alone is not a guarantee of enforceability.

Visualization Logic: The "Barred" box is placed right at the very first gate deliberately — it's the most consequential and most commonly overlooked failure point in this whole sequence, since a party can have a completely valid underlying claim and still lose simply by missing a notice deadline, before the substance of the dispute is ever even discussed. The chapter's rationale for strict notice timing matters here too: the other party wants the CHANCE to remedy or mitigate the issue early, rather than being informed of a claim only after the relevant remedial window has closed. The arbitration box at the end carries a separate, important warning from the chapter: a MANDATORY arbitration clause can force a multi-party dispute into a SINGLE forum with only the two original contracting parties, even when the real dispute genuinely involves other project participants — which is exactly why the chapter says many lawyers actively dislike mandatory arbitration clauses despite arbitration remaining freely available by mutual agreement at any time, mandatory or not.
