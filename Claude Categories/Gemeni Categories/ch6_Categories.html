Chapter 6: Contracts

1. The Five Essential Elements of a Contract
  • Offer and Acceptance: One party must make a clear offer, and the other must give unequivocal acceptance. (An agreement to agree is not a contract).
  • Mutual Intent: Both parties must explicitly intend for the agreement to be legally binding.
  • Consideration: Something of value must be exchanged by both sides (money, services, or a promise). A one-sided free promise is not a contract.
  • Capacity: Both parties must be legally capable of understanding the deal (no minors, no extreme intoxication, no mental incompetence).
  • Lawful Purpose: The objective of the contract cannot be illegal or against public policy.
2. Legal Status of Agreements
  • Valid: Contains all five essential elements and is fully enforceable in court.
  • Void: Missing an essential element (like lawful purpose). The law treats it as if it never existed.
  • Voidable: A contract where one party has the legal option to either enforce it or cancel it (e.g., if a party was coerced or lacked capacity).
3. The Statute of Frauds (Contracts that MUST be in Writing)
  • Contracts dealing with the purchase, sale, or transfer of land.
  • Contracts involving a guarantee (a promise to pay someone else's debt if they default, like a Surety in Chapter 17).
  • Contracts that cannot possibly be performed within one year.
4. Pre-Contractual Documents
  • Letter of Intent (LOI): A document expressing the desire to form a contract in the future. Generally not legally binding unless it accidentally contains all five essential elements.
  • Memorandum of Understanding (MOU): Similar to an LOI, outlines a framework for future cooperation.
5. The "Battle of the Forms"
  • Occurs when two companies exchange standard pre-printed forms (like a Purchase Order and an Invoice) with conflicting terms.
  • General Rule: The "last shot" wins. The party who sends the final form containing their terms before the work actually begins is generally the one whose contract rules apply.
