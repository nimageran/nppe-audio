Chapter 8: Tort Law

1. The Three Categories of Torts
  • Intentional Torts: Deliberately hurting someone or their property (e.g., fraud, trespass, defamation).
  • Unintentional Torts (Negligence): Carelessly hurting someone by failing to meet the standard of practice. (This is the most critical tort for engineers).
  • Strict Liability Torts: You are held liable even if you were not careless and did not intend to cause harm (e.g., Rylands v. Fletcher - letting toxic waste escape).
2. The Four Mandatory Elements of Negligence
  • 1. Duty of Care: The defendant owed a legal obligation to protect the plaintiff from foreseeable harm. (You do not owe a duty to everyone on earth, only to those your work directly impacts).
  • 2. Breach of Duty: The defendant's work fell below the standard of a reasonable, competent professional in that specific field.
  • 3. Causation: The breach directly caused the damage. (The "But-For" test: But for the engineer's mistake, would the building have collapsed?)
  • 4. Damages: The plaintiff suffered an actual, measurable physical or financial loss. (You cannot sue for a "near miss").
3. Special Categories of Liability
  • Vicarious Liability: An employer is legally and financially responsible for the negligence committed by their employees while on the job.
  • Joint and Several Liability: When multiple parties (e.g., the architect and the engineer) are both at fault. The plaintiff can sue just one of them for 100% of the money, and leave it to the defendants to fight amongst themselves to split the bill.
  • Fiduciary Duty: A special, elevated relationship of absolute trust (e.g., a doctor to a patient, or a corporate director to a company). You must put the other party's interests completely ahead of your own.
4. Defences to Negligence
  • Contributory Negligence: The plaintiff was also careless and contributed to their own injury. The court splits the damages (e.g., Engineer is 70% at fault, Client is 30% at fault).
  • Voluntary Assumption of Risk: The plaintiff fully knew the extreme danger and legally signed away their right to sue before doing the activity.
  • Limitation Periods: The plaintiff waited too long to sue. (Usually, you have 2 years from the day you discovered the damage, and an absolute maximum drop-dead limit of 15 years from the day the work was completed).
5. Types of Negligence Claims
  • Negligent Misrepresentation: Giving careless advice or bad data that causes someone to lose money (e.g., a bad geotechnical report).
  • Product Liability: A manufacturer carelessly designing or building a dangerous product that hurts the end-user.
