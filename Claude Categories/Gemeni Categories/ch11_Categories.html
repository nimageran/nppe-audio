Chapter 11: Specific Contracts and Clauses

1. Project Delivery Methods (Shifting the Risk)
  • Design-Bid-Build: The traditional model. The owner hires an engineer to design it, then tenders it to a contractor to build it. The owner assumes the liability if the design blueprints are flawed.
  • Design-Build: The owner hires a single company to do both the design and the construction. The contractor assumes the total design risk.
  • Construction Management: The owner hires a manager to oversee the project schedule and trades. The manager can act purely as an agent (Owner holds financial risk) or "at-risk" (Manager holds financial risk).
  • Public-Private Partnerships (P3): A private consortium designs, builds, finances, and maintains a massive public asset (like a hospital or bridge) for decades, leasing it back to the government.
2. Payment and Pricing Models
  • Fixed Price (Stipulated Sum): One lump sum for the entire job. The contractor eats the cost if materials suddenly get more expensive. (High risk for the contractor).
  • Cost Plus: The owner pays for all actual materials and labor used, plus a fee or percentage for the contractor's profit. (High risk for the owner).
  • Unit Price: Paid per item (e.g., $100 per cubic meter of concrete). Perfect for roadwork or earthmoving where the exact quantities are impossible to know before digging starts.
  • Target Price: A Cost Plus contract, but with a maximum budget limit. If it comes in under budget, the contractor gets a bonus. If it goes over, there is a penalty.
3. Standard Form Contracts
  • CCDC (Canadian Construction Documents Committee): Creates the standard, neutral templates used across Canada (e.g., CCDC 2 is the standard Stipulated Price Contract).
  • CCA (Canadian Construction Association): Creates standard templates primarily for subcontracts.
  • FIDIC: Creates standard international templates for global engineering projects.
4. Anatomy of the Contract Documents
  • General Conditions: The standard boilerplate rules that apply to almost every project (e.g., standard insurance requirements, general delay rules).
  • Supplementary Conditions: Custom, project-specific edits that legally override the General Conditions.
  • Specifications & Drawings: The exact technical requirements defining what is being built.
