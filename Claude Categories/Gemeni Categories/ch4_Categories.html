Chapter 4: Property Law

1. The Two Main Classes of Property
  • Real Property: Land and everything permanently attached to it (buildings, mineral rights beneath the surface).
  • Personal Property: Everything else. All property that is not land.
2. Types of Personal Property
  • Tangible (Chattels): Physical objects you can touch (e.g., a laptop, an excavator, a book).
  • Intangible: Property with no physical form but representing legal rights or value (e.g., shares in a company, intellectual property, software code).
3. Types of Intellectual Property (Intangible)
  • Patents: Protects new, useful, and unobvious inventions/processes. Lasts for 20 years. Must be registered.
  • Copyrights: Protects the expression of an idea (books, software code, CAD drawings), not the idea itself. Exists automatically upon creation. Lasts life of the author plus 50-70 years.
  • Trademarks: Protects brands, logos, or slogans used to distinguish goods or services. Can last indefinitely if renewed.
  • Industrial Designs: Protects the visual appearance or shape of a mass-produced product (e.g., the shape of a Coca-Cola bottle).
  • Trade Secrets: Protects valuable business information (e.g., a recipe or a client list). Never expires, but only protected as long as you can keep it a secret.
4. Types of Real Property Ownership
  • Fee Simple: The absolute highest level of ownership you can have. You own the land essentially forever, subject only to the Crown's underlying rights.
  • Leasehold: You have the exclusive right to occupy and use the land for a specific, temporary period of time (e.g., renting an office space).
5. Concurrent Ownership (Co-Ownership)
  • Joint Tenancy: Both parties own the whole property. Includes the Right of Survivorship (if one owner dies, their share automatically goes to the surviving owner, bypassing their will).
  • Tenancy in Common: Each party owns a specific percentage (e.g., 50/50 or 70/30). No right of survivorship (if you die, your share goes to your heirs through your will).
6. Rights of Others in Your Land (Encumbrances)
  • Mortgages: A bank registers a charge against your land as security for a loan.
  • Easements: The legal right for someone else to use a portion of your land for a specific purpose (e.g., a utility company running a power line across your property).
  • Restrictive Covenants: A legally binding rule registered on the title that limits what you can do with your land (e.g., "You cannot build a house taller than two stories").
  • Builders' Liens: A charge registered against your land by unpaid contractors to prevent you from selling it until they are paid.
