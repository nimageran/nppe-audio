Chapter 3: Ethical Considerations

1. The Four Ethical Theories
  • Utilitarianism (John Stuart Mill): The right action is the one that creates the maximum benefit for the maximum number of people. (Focuses on the outcome).
  • Duty Ethics (Immanuel Kant): There are universal rules everyone must follow. An action is right if it follows your duty, regardless of the outcome. (Focuses on the action itself).
  • Rights Ethics (John Locke): Every human has basic, fundamental rights (like life and liberty). An action is wrong if it violates someone's fundamental rights.
  • Virtue Ethics (Aristotle): Focuses on personal character. The right action is finding the middle ground (the "golden mean") between two extremes.
2. Hierarchy of Professional Duties (In Exact Order of Importance)
  • 1. Duty to Society: The safety, health, and welfare of the public is always number one. It overrides everything else.
  • 2. Duty to Employer: You must act as a faithful agent and keep their secrets.
  • 3. Duty to Clients: You must be fair and honest with them.
  • 4. Duty to Colleagues: You must act with courtesy and goodwill toward other professionals.
  • 5. Duty to Employees/Subordinates: You must treat them fairly and recognize their work.
  • 6. Duty to the Profession: You must maintain the dignity and prestige of the engineering field.
  • 7. Duty to Oneself: You must ensure you are paid fairly and work in a safe environment.
3. Types of Conflicts of Interest
  • Actual: You are currently in a situation where your personal interests are actively clashing with your duty to your employer or client.
  • Potential: You are not clashing yet, but you are in a situation where a conflict will likely happen soon.
  • Perceived (or Apparent): You might not actually be doing anything wrong, but to an outside observer, it looks like you are compromised. (This is just as bad as an actual conflict).
