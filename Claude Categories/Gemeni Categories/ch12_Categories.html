Chapter 12: Human Resources Law

1. Employee vs. Independent Contractor Tests
  • The Control Test: Who decides the working hours, methods, and supervision? If the company controls how the work is done, you are an employee.
  • The Economic Reality Test: Who owns the tools? Who takes on the financial risk of loss or the chance of profit? Contractors bring their own tools and risk their own money.
  • The Organization Test: How integrated is the worker into the company? If their work is a core, daily function of the business, they are likely an employee.
2. Types of Employment Dismissal
  • Just Cause: Firing an employee immediately without severance due to severe misconduct (e.g., stealing, violence, extreme insubordination).
  • Without Cause (Reasonable Notice): Firing an employee for non-disciplinary reasons (e.g., downsizing). Requires giving them fair notice or pay in lieu of notice.
  • Wrongful Dismissal: The employee sues because the employer fired them without cause and failed to provide adequate notice or severance pay.
  • Constructive Dismissal: The employer fundamentally changes the employee's job without their consent (e.g., slashing their salary by 30% or demoting them heavily) forcing the employee to quit. The law treats this as a firing.
3. Human Rights and Discrimination
  • Prohibited Grounds: It is strictly illegal to fire, refuse to hire, or mistreat someone based on race, religion, age, sex, sexual orientation, or physical/mental disability.
  • Duty to Accommodate: Employers must adjust the workplace to support an employee's protected grounds (e.g., building a wheelchair ramp or allowing religious holidays) up to the point of Undue Hardship.
  • Bona Fide Occupational Requirement (BFOR): The only legal exception to discrimination. If a job strictly requires a specific physical ability (e.g., perfect vision for an airline pilot), it is legal to discriminate based on that specific necessity.
4. Occupational Health and Safety (OH&S) Core Rights
  • Right to Know: Workers must be informed about workplace hazards and trained on handling them (e.g., WHMIS for chemicals).
  • Right to Refuse: Workers have the absolute legal right to refuse unsafe work without fear of getting fired.
  • Right to Participate: Workers have the right to be involved in health and safety committees.
