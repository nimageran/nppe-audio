Chapter 5: Business Organizations

1. The Three Main Forms of Business
  • Sole Proprietorship: An individual carrying on business for profit. You and the business are the exact same legal entity. Absolute personal liability.
  • Partnership: Two or more people carrying on business together for profit. Joint liability.
  • Corporation: A separate, distinct legal entity created by statute. The owners have limited liability.
2. Types of Partnerships
  • General Partnership: All partners share the management and share 100% of the liability. If your partner makes a catastrophic error, the client can seize your personal house to pay the debt.
  • Limited Partnership (LP): Requires at least one General Partner (who runs the business and takes all the risk) and Limited Partners (silent investors who only risk the money they invested).
  • Limited Liability Partnership (LLP): Used strictly by professionals (like lawyers and accountants). You are protected from the negligence of your other partners, but still liable for your own negligence.
3. The Structure of a Corporation
  • Shareholders: The owners of the company. They vote to elect the directors. They have limited liability (they only lose the value of their shares).
  • Directors: The governing mind of the company. They make high-level policies, declare dividends, and appoint the officers.
  • Officers: The day-to-day management (CEO, President, VP). They execute the directors' policies.
4. Piercing the Corporate Veil (Exceptions to Limited Liability)
  • Fraud: Setting up a fake corporation just to steal money or avoid a specific debt.
  • Taxes: Directors can be held personally liable if the company fails to pay employee income tax deductions.
  • Environment: Directors and officers can go to prison personally for gross environmental negligence.
5. Types of Corporate Finance
  • Equity (Shares): Selling ownership in the company. No obligation to pay it back, but dilutes control.
  • Debt (Bonds/Loans): Borrowing money. The company keeps full ownership, but must pay the money back with interest, regardless of profit.
6. Public vs. Private Corporations
  • Private Corporation: Shares are not sold to the public. Usually restricted to a small number of shareholders (e.g., founders and family).
  • Public Corporation: Shares are traded openly on the stock market. Subject to massive, strict securities regulations (as covered in Chapter 25).
