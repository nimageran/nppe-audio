Chapter 10: Getting to a Contract (Tendering)

1. Types of Procurement Processes
  • Call for Tenders: A strict, formal process where the buyer knows exactly what they want and simply needs the lowest price.
  • Request for Proposals (RFP): The buyer has a problem but isn't sure how to fix it. They invite contractors to submit creative solutions and prices.
  • Request for Qualifications (RFQ): A filtering process. Used to evaluate the skills of contractors to create a short-list before asking for actual prices.
  • Expression of Interest (EOI): A very early step just to see which companies might be interested in a future project.
2. The Two-Contract Tendering System (The Ron Engineering Rule)
  • Contract A (The Bidding Contract): Created the exact second a contractor submits a valid bid. It dictates the rules of the contest.
  • Contract B (The Construction Contract): Created only when the owner officially accepts the winning bid and signs the deal. It dictates the actual building process.
3. The Owner's Strict Legal Duties (During Contract A)
  • Duty to Reject Non-Compliant Bids: If a bid is missing a required signature or a deposit, the owner MUST throw it out. Accepting it is a breach of Contract A.
  • Duty of Fairness: All bidders must be treated equally. No secret preferences or hidden rules.
  • Duty to Disclose: The owner must openly share all relevant information (e.g., bad soil conditions). You cannot hide facts to trick contractors into giving cheaper prices.
4. The Contractor's Strict Legal Duties (During Contract A)
  • Bid Irrevocability: Once the tender deadline passes, the bid is locked. The contractor cannot cancel their bid or change their price.
  • No Bid Shopping: A general contractor cannot take a subcontractor's confidential price and show it to another subcontractor to force them to lower their price.
5. The Privilege Clause
  • What it says: "The lowest or any bid will not necessarily be accepted."
  • What it does: It allows the owner to pick the second or third-lowest bidder, provided they have a legitimate, disclosed reason (like a better safety record or more experience).
  • What it DOES NOT do: It does not give the owner permission to run an unfair contest, use hidden criteria, or accept a non-compliant bid.
