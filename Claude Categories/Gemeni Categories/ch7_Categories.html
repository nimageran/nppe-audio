Chapter 7: Contract Interpretation, Discharge, and Breach

1. Rules of Contract Interpretation
  • Plain Meaning Rule: Courts read the contract literally. They will not rewrite a bad deal for you if the words are clear.
  • Parol Evidence Rule: If a contract is fully written and signed, you cannot introduce prior verbal promises to contradict the written words. (Get it in writing, or it doesn't exist).
  • Contra Proferentem: If a clause is highly ambiguous and can be read two ways, the court will interpret it against the party who drafted the contract.
2. Ways a Contract is Discharged (Ended)
  • 1. Performance: Both parties do exactly what they promised. The contract naturally ends.
  • 2. Mutual Agreement: Both parties agree to rip up the contract and walk away.
  • 3. Frustration: An unforeseeable, extreme event (like a factory burning down or a war) makes it physically or legally impossible to perform the work. (Note: It becoming more expensive does NOT equal frustration).
  • 4. Breach: One party fails to perform, killing the contract and triggering a lawsuit.
3. Types of Contract Breaches
  • Minor Breach: A failure of a non-essential term. The innocent party can sue for damages, but they MUST still finish their side of the contract.
  • Fundamental Breach: A failure so severe it destroys the entire purpose of the contract. The innocent party can immediately stop working and sue.
  • Repudiation (Anticipatory Breach): One party explicitly tells the other, before the deadline, that they will not or cannot finish the job. The innocent party can sue immediately; they do not have to wait for the deadline to pass.
4. Remedies for Breach of Contract
  • Damages (Money): The default remedy. The court forces the breaching party to pay enough money to put the innocent party in the financial position they would have been in if the contract was performed properly.
  • Specific Performance: The court legally forces the breaching party to physically complete the promise. (Very rare, usually only used for unique land sales).
  • Injunction: The court orders a party to stop doing something that breaches the contract (e.g., stopping an engineer from sharing a trade secret).
  • Quantum Meruit: "As much as is deserved." If there is no valid contract price, the court orders payment for the fair market value of the work already done.
5. Damages Limitations
  • Mitigation: The innocent party cannot sit back and let damages pile up. They have a strict legal duty to actively minimize their own losses (e.g., if a supplier doesn't deliver steel, you must try to buy steel from someone else immediately).
  • Remoteness: You can only sue for damages that were reasonably foreseeable at the time the contract was signed. You cannot sue for bizarre, unpredictable chain-reaction losses.
