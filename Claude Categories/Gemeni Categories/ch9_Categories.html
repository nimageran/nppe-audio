Chapter 9: Common Issues in Contract and Tort

1. Concurrent Liability
  • The Concept: A plaintiff can sue a defendant in both Contract and Tort at the exact same time for the exact same mistake.
  • The Benefit: This allows the plaintiff to choose whichever legal pathway offers the best financial payout or the longest time limit to sue.
  • The Defense: To stop this, engineers use specific contract clauses (Limitation of Liability) to explicitly state that all liability is restricted only to the contract, legally killing the tort claim.
2. Limitation Periods (The Ticking Clock)
  • Basic / Discoverability Period: The standard clock. You generally have 2 years to file a lawsuit starting from the exact day you discovered (or reasonably should have discovered) the damage.
  • Ultimate Period: The absolute drop-dead limit. Usually 15 years starting from the exact day the engineering work was completed. Even if you discover the damage 16 years later, your right to sue is permanently dead.
3. Joint and Several Liability
  • The Concept: When multiple parties (e.g., an engineer, an architect, and a contractor) all contribute to a failure.
  • The "Deep Pockets" Rule: The plaintiff does not have to collect 33% of the money from each of the three parties. The plaintiff can sue the richest party (the one with the deepest pockets) for 100% of the damages.
  • The Aftermath: It is then the legal responsibility of that rich party to hunt down the other at-fault parties and sue them to get their share of the money back.
4. Vicarious Liability
  • The Concept: An employer is strictly, financially responsible for the negligent acts of their employees, provided the employee was acting within the scope of their normal job duties.
  • The Exception: If an employee goes completely rogue and commits a crime or an act entirely unrelated to their job, the employer is generally not vicariously liable.
5. Indemnities and Guarantees
  • Indemnity: A promise to protect someone and pay for their financial losses if they get sued by a third party (e.g., "If my crane drops a beam on a pedestrian and they sue you, I promise to pay your legal bills").
  • Guarantee: A promise to step in and pay someone's debt if they fail to pay it themselves. (Requires the Statute of Frauds—must be in writing).
