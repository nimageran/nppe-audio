The #1 Purpose of the Association
Click to flip
The Catch: The provincial association (like EGM) is NOT a labor union. They do not care about your salary or protecting your job.

The Rule: Their only legal purpose is to protect the public from bad engineers.
Protect the Public FIRST
Who regulates engineering?
Click to flip
The Rule: It is regulated provincially, not federally. The provincial government passes a law giving engineers the privilege of Self-Regulation.

The Catch: If the profession fails to protect the public, the government can take this privilege away.
Self-Regulated by Province
Right to Title vs.
Scope of Practice
Click to flip
Right to Title: Only registered members can legally call themselves "Engineer" or "P.Eng.".

Exclusive Scope of Practice: Only registered members can legally DO engineering work. Both the name and the job are protected by law.
Protects the Name AND the Job
Discipline vs. Enforcement
Click to flip
Discipline: Punishing registered members who break the rules (e.g., unethical behavior, incompetence).

Enforcement: Going after fake engineers (non-members) who illegally use the title or practice without a license.
Discipline = Members | Enforcement = Fakes
The Hierarchy of Rules
Click to flip
1. The Act: Passed by the government. The highest authority.
2. Regulations: Rules for running the association, approved by the government.
3. Bylaws: Day-to-day administrative rules voted on by the engineers themselves (the lowest authority).
Act > Regulations > Bylaws
What does your Professional Seal (Stamp) mean?
Click to flip
The Rule: Applying your stamp means you personally prepared, supervised, or thoroughly reviewed the work.

The Catch: It means you are taking full legal and professional responsibility for that document. Never stamp something you did not check.
Stamp = Total Responsibility
Certificates of Authorization
(C of A)
Click to flip
The Rule: A company cannot be a "P.Eng." Only humans get that title.

The Catch: If a company wants to offer engineering services to the public, the business itself must hold a Certificate of Authorization (or Permit to Practice) to operate legally.
Humans get P.Eng. | Companies get C of A
Can you practice in another province?
Click to flip
The Rule: Your license stops at the provincial border. If you move, you must apply to the new province's association.

The Catch: "Mobility agreements" make transferring your license fast, but it is never automatic. You must register locally.
License Stays at the Border
What can the Discipline Committee do to you?
Click to flip
The Rule: They can fine you, suspend you, force you to take classes, or permanently strip your P.Eng. license.

The Catch: They CANNOT send you to jail. Only the real Canadian court system can put you in prison.
They Take Your License, Not Your Freedom
Incompetence
Click to flip
The Story: You take on a bridge design job, but you only know how to design HVAC systems.

The Rule: Practicing outside your specific area of training and expertise is legally considered "incompetence" and is grounds for losing your license.
Stick to What You Know
Continuing Professional Development (CPD)
Click to flip
The Rule: Getting your P.Eng. is not the finish line. To keep the public safe, you are legally required to keep your skills sharp.

The Catch: You must track and report your ongoing education (CPD hours) every year, or you will lose your license.
Learn or Lose It
The Code of Ethics
Click to flip
The Catch: The Code of Ethics is not just a list of polite suggestions.

The Rule: It is a strict legal requirement built directly into the provincial Regulations. Breaking the Code is professional misconduct and will trigger a discipline hearing.
Ethics = The Law
