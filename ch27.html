Visualization Logic: The split into "Solves" versus "Does Not Solve" is the entire point of this diagram — a governing law clause is necessary but routinely OVERESTIMATED in how much protection it actually provides. It locks down which law and which court resolve a dispute BETWEEN the two contracting parties, but it has zero power over independent regulatory regimes (employment standards, tax authorities, safety regulators, privacy commissioners) that apply based on WHERE the work physically happens or where data travels, regardless of what the contract says. The bottom branch captures the chapter's separate, compounding problem: even setting aside contract law entirely, a foreign court — especially in the US — can simply decide it has jurisdiction over an online dispute just because its own residents were affected, which means a business's risk exposure isn't fully controllable through drafting alone.

Visualization Logic: The threshold diamond is placed first and given the most visual weight because it's the gate most people get wrong about online speech — there's a strong intuition that anything posted publicly and read by many people must be more serious than a private remark, but the actual legal test never changes based on audience size, only on whether the REPUTATIONAL injury itself is significant. The written/oral split underneath only matters once you're already past that gate, and it's really just inherited terminology (libel/slander) being mapped onto internet-specific formats — a blog post is libel, a recorded video rant is slander, but both still have to clear the same significance threshold first. The "Treatment" box at the bottom answers a question that might otherwise seem unresolved: courts don't invent a new framework for websites, they simply borrow the existing broadcast-media treatment and apply it directly.

Visualization Logic: Splitting this into two completely separate national branches is the only way to make the gap impossible to miss — the DMCA is a US-specific statutory safe harbour, and the chapter is explicit that nothing equivalent exists in Canada, meaning a Canadian rights holder cannot rely on a formal notice-and-takedown statutory process the way an American one might. That gap is exactly why the "Protect" box at the bottom matters so much more in the Canadian context — without a statutory ISP framework to lean on, the practical defence becomes almost entirely about being LITIGATION-READY in advance: registering the copyright before any dispute arises (to prove publication date), having licensing paperwork in order, and using technical markers that make ownership easier to prove in court, since court action is described as "the only real recourse" once Canadian infringement actually occurs.

4. Cybersquatting — Two Legal Theories, One Act

Cybersquatting isn't punished under just one legal theory — courts have found that the SAME single act simultaneously satisfies two separate causes of action. This diagram shows both theories branching off the one act.

Visualization Logic: The diagram is built around a single point worth dwelling on: courts didn't have to choose just one legal label for cybersquatting, and the chapter explicitly states both theories apply. The trademark theory focuses on CONSUMER CONFUSION — the risk that internet users encountering the domain might think they're dealing with the real trademark owner. The misrepresentation theory focuses on something subtly different — a FALSE IMPLICATION that the real trademark owner has somehow blessed or endorsed this domain's use, even if no actual consumer confusion about WHO owns the domain occurs. Having both theories available gives a trademark owner two independent paths to a remedy, which matters practically if one theory faces a weaker fact pattern (e.g., if confusion is hard to prove) — the other theory remains available as a backup.

5. Electronic Contract Formation — The Agent Substitution

An "I Agree" button click feels less formal than a signature on paper, but courts have mapped it onto an existing legal concept rather than inventing something new. This diagram shows that substitution directly.

Visualization Logic: Placing the traditional and electronic paths side by side, converging on the same "Substitution" diamond, makes the legal reasoning transparent — courts didn't need a brand-new doctrine for online contracts, they simply extended AGENCY LAW (already covered in Chapter 9) to say that software performing the offering function counts as an agent the same way a human salesperson or representative would. This is precisely why "I Agree" clickwrap agreements are enforceable in Canada despite involving no human interaction at the moment of formation — the legal fiction of "software as agent" carries all the same weight as if a person had made the offer directly, and the click itself is simply ordinary acceptance under standard offer-and-acceptance principles from Chapter 6.

6. Electronic Delivery and Receipt Timing

Electronic communications need their own timing rule because there's no physical envelope being handed over. This diagram shows the two distinct moments the law cares about, and where party autonomy can override the default.

Visualization Logic: The key distinction this diagram protects is that DELIVERY and RECEIPT are two genuinely different moments, not synonyms — delivery is defined entirely from the SENDER's side (the moment it leaves their control), while receipt is defined entirely from the RECEIVER's side (the moment they're capable of accessing it), and there can be a real time gap between the two if a server delay or network issue intervenes. This distinction matters anywhere a contract's timing affects rights — like whether an acceptance arrived before a deadline, or whether a notice was properly given. The override diamond at the end is a reminder that none of this is mandatory law in the sense of being unchangeable — parties remain free to write their own timing definitions into a contract, and where they've done so, that custom definition displaces the statutory default entirely.

Visualization Logic: The three boxes under "Concerns" deliberately separate three DIFFERENT failure modes that get casually lumped together as "is this person legit" — identity (are they who they claim), capacity (can they legally enter a binding agreement at all — e.g., not a minor, not under some legal incapacity), and authority (even if this individual is real and capable, can THEY bind the company they say they represent). The layered structure (technological measures, THEN traditional backups on top) reflects the chapter's actual recommendation that companies don't rely on just one method — pairing a cryptographic signature with an old-fashioned phone call confirmation closes gaps that either method alone would miss. The final diamond is an honest acknowledgment baked into the chapter's own text: even with every reasonable layer in place, residual risk can never be reduced to zero, which is exactly why insurance and contractual risk allocation close out the diagram as the last line of defence for whatever verification simply cannot catch.

Visualization Logic: Rather than memorizing eight disconnected risk facts as a flat list, grouping them into four underlying THEMES gives you a reusable mental model: any new email-related problem you encounter on the exam likely falls into one of these same four buckets, even if it's phrased differently than the textbook's exact wording. Carelessness is about the SENDER's own poor judgment in the moment of writing. Loss of control is about what happens to the message ONCE SENT — it can spread, misfire, or vanish in ways neither party controls. Authenticity is the unique risk that the email you're looking at might not actually be genuine. Permanence is the theme that surprises people most: email doesn't behave like a private conversation that fades, it behaves more like a permanent, recoverable, and exposed record — which is precisely why professionals are advised elsewhere in the broader course material to treat every email as though it could appear, unaltered, in a courtroom years later. The single arrow converging all eight risks into one "Policy" box reinforces that a CSP isn't optional boilerplate — it's the direct, deliberate response to this entire risk landscape, not a generic IT formality.
