1. The Five Pillars of Enforceability

A contract is an enforceable voluntary agreement that sets out the rights, responsibilities, and liabilities of the parties involved[cite: 1]. Its real purpose is to allocate risk and obligations[cite: 1]. For a court to uphold it, five strict elements must be present[cite: 1].

Visualization Logic: This map shows the foundational requirements of any contract[cite: 1].

  • Privity of Contract: Only parties strictly within this structure (privy to the contract) can enforce it[cite: 1]. Even if a contract was made specifically to benefit a third party, that third party generally cannot enforce it in Canada, unlike in the United States[cite: 1].
  • Form: A contract does not always need to be in writing (except for specific cases like land sales); oral contracts are fully enforceable, though much harder to prove in court[cite: 1].

2. The Negotiation Dance (Offer & Acceptance)

Contract formation requires an offer capable of being accepted, and an unequivocal acceptance of that offer[cite: 1]. However, the rules of negotiation dictate exactly when an offer is alive or dead[cite: 1].

Visualization Logic: This sequence outlines how negotiations legally function[cite: 1].

  • Invitation to Treat vs. Offer: A general request for offers is an "invitation to treat" and cannot be accepted[cite: 1]. However, in large-scale procurement, an invitation for bids is treated legally as an offer in Canada[cite: 1].
  • The Counter-Offer Trap: If an offeree responds to an offer by asking for different terms, they are making a counter-offer[cite: 1]. This automatically terminates the original offer; the offeree cannot change their mind and accept the original offer later unless the offeror revives it[cite: 1].
  • Revocation: An offeror can revoke their offer at any time before it is accepted, but the revocation must be communicated to the offeree[cite: 1]. Bids in a formal tendering process are an exception and are typically irrevocable for 30 to 60 days[cite: 1].

3. The Consideration Trap (Amendments)

Consideration means something of value, no matter how small, has been given or promised by each party[cite: 1]. The most common legal trap engineers face is attempting to amend a contract without adding fresh consideration[cite: 1].

Visualization Logic: This diagrams the ruling from cases like Smith v. Dawson and Modular Windows[cite: 1].

  • The Core Rule: You cannot demand extra money to perform a task you are already legally bound to perform[cite: 1]. The promise to simply not abandon the contract is not valid consideration[cite: 1].
  • The Exception: A promise made without consideration is considered a gift and will not be enforced by courts, unless the promise is made in a written document and a formal seal (like a corporate seal) is applied to it, which the courts recognize as a substitute for consideration[cite: 1].

4. The Contract Killers (Voidability)

Sometimes a contract is formed perfectly, but a hidden flaw allows one party to void the contract and walk away legally[cite: 1]. The courts will only void a contract under very rare, specific circumstances[cite: 1].

Visualization Logic: This charts the exact conditions required to escape a contract[cite: 1].

  • Mistake: To escape via mistake, the misunderstanding must be significant (material), made by both parties (mutual), and must have existed at the exact time the contract was formed[cite: 1].
  • Misrepresentation: A false statement that induces someone to sign[cite: 1]. If an owner has critical subsurface soil information that contradicts bid assumptions and hides it, this is misrepresentation by omission[cite: 1].
  • Duress: Illegitimate pressure or threats[cite: 1]. The courts look at whether the coerced party protested, had any alternative legal remedies, or took steps to avoid the contract later (from the Pao On case)[cite: 1].
  • Frustration: Also known as impossibility[cite: 1]. An unforeseen event makes performance literally impossible[cite: 1]. Note that most modern contracts use a "force majeure" clause to pre-allocate these exact risks, making frustration very difficult to prove[cite: 1].

5. Quantum Meruit & Waiver

When the strict rules of a contract break down due to casual behavior or fundamental changes, the courts step in with equitable remedies to ensure fairness[cite: 1].

Visualization Logic: This highlights how courts fix broken contractual realities[cite: 1].

  • Waiver and Estoppel: If a contract says all changes must be in writing, but the owner casually approves oral changes for months, they have "waived" their right to enforce that clause[cite: 1]. They are "estopped" from suddenly refusing to pay for an oral change later[cite: 1].
  • Quantum Meruit: Translates to "the amount it is worth"[cite: 1]. If an owner forces a contractor to change their work method (a constructive change) but refuses to acknowledge it as an official change order, the contract price no longer applies[cite: 1]. The court will use Quantum Meruit to calculate a fair payment based on the actual value of the work performed, including a reasonable profit[cite: 1].
