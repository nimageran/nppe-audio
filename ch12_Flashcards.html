What is a "Tort"?
Click to flip
The Basics: You make a mistake that physically hurts someone or costs them money.

The Catch: You can be sued for a tort even if you never signed a contract with the person you hurt.
Mistake = Lawsuit (No Contract Needed)
What are the two main types of Torts?
Click to flip
1. Intentional: You meant to do it (like lying on purpose or parking equipment on a neighbor's land).

2. Unintentional: It was an accident (like making a math error on a drawing). This is called "Negligence."
On Purpose vs. By Accident
The "Snail in the Bottle" Case
(Donoghue v. Stevenson)
Click to flip
The Story: A woman found a dead snail in her drink and sued the factory.

The Rule: The court said the factory must protect the final user, even without a direct contract. This invented modern Negligence law.
Protect the Final User
How do you prove "Negligence"?
Click to flip
The 4 Steps: A person suing you must prove you had a Duty to protect them, you Breached that duty, they suffered a Loss, and your mistake Caused it. If they cannot prove all 4, they lose.
DBLC: Duty, Breach, Loss, Causation
Who do you owe a "Duty of Care" to?
Click to flip
The Rule: You must protect anyone you can reasonably guess might be hurt by your actions. If you design a building, you owe a duty to the future people walking inside it, not just the owner.
Protect Your "Neighbour"
What is the "Standard of Care"?
Click to flip
The Rule: You do not have to be perfect. You just need to be as good as a normal, average engineer.

The Catch: The court judges you based on the rules that existed when you did the work, not years later.
Average Competence, Not Perfection
What if you call yourself an "Expert"?
Click to flip
The Catch: If you tell the public you are a "specialist," the court will judge you harder. You will be compared to other top experts, not just average engineers.
Claim Expert = Judged as Expert
What is "Proximate Cause"?
Click to flip
The "But For" Test: Ask yourself: "But for my mistake, would the building still have fallen?" If it would have fallen anyway, your mistake did not trigger the damage, so you are not liable.
Did YOUR mistake pull the trigger?
How does the court calculate damages?
Click to flip
The Goal: Put the victim back where they started. The court will make you pay either the Cost of Repair (fixing the physical mistake) OR the Diminution (the amount of value the property lost).
Fix It OR Pay the Lost Value
Can you sue just for losing money?
(Pure Economic Loss)
Click to flip
The Rule: Usually, no. If nothing physically broke and nobody got hurt, you cannot sue for just lost money.

Exception: You CAN sue if the mistake creates a real danger to health and safety, or if it involves bad advice.
Must Have Damage OR Danger
Can an injured construction worker sue the engineer?
Click to flip
The Shield: Usually, no. "Workers' Compensation" laws stop injured workers from suing their boss or the project engineers. They get a payout from a government fund instead.
Workers' Comp Blocks Lawsuits
What is "Negligent Misrepresentation"?
Click to flip
The Story: You accidentally give bad professional advice. If a client reasonably trusts your advice, uses it, and loses money, they can sue you. It is an honest mistake that causes financial ruin.
Bad Advice + Trust = Liability
What is "Fraudulent Misrepresentation"?
Click to flip
The Story: You lie on purpose, or you do not care if what you say is true, to trick a client into signing a contract.

The Result: The client can cancel the contract AND sue you for money damages.
Lie on Purpose = Cancel + Damages
What is "Innocent Misrepresentation"?
Click to flip
The Story: You give false information, but it was a complete, honest mistake with no carelessness at all.

The Result: The client can cancel the contract, but they CANNOT sue you for extra money.
Honest Mistake = Cancel Only (No Money)
Can staying silent be a lie?
(Misrepresentation by Omission)
Click to flip
The Rule: Yes. If the law says you must share a report (like finding bad soil) and you stay quiet to keep the deal going, the court treats your silence exactly the same as a spoken lie.
Hiding Facts is Fraud
What is an "Entire Agreement" Clause?
Click to flip
The Shield: A line in a contract that says: "Anything we talked about before signing doesn't matter unless it's written in this paper." It is meant to stop people from suing over verbal promises.
If it is not in writing, it does not exist
What is a "Fiduciary Duty"?
Click to flip
The Rule: It is a special, extremely high level of trust where you must put a vulnerable client's needs completely above your own.

The Catch: Regular engineering contracts usually do NOT require this.
Total, Unselfish Loyalty
What counts as "Trespass" in construction?
Click to flip
The Story: Crossing property lines without asking. This includes putting rock anchors deep underground or swinging a crane in the air over a neighbor's yard.

The Catch: You are liable even if you cause zero damage.
Crossing the Line = Strict Liability
What is a "Nuisance"?
Click to flip
The Story: Ruining your neighbor's peace. If you bring something dangerous to your site (like a water tank or heavy pile-driving vibrations) and it escapes to their land to cause damage, you are strictly liable.
The Escaping Mischief
What is your "Duty to Warn"?
Click to flip
The Rule: If you see something dangerous on a site that could hurt someone, you MUST speak up. Even if safety is not your specific job or in your contract, you must warn the owner or authorities.
See Danger, Speak Up
How does "Product Liability" work in Canada?
Click to flip
The Rule: Unlike the US, if a product hurts a Canadian, they must prove the factory was negligent.

The Catch: Once the user proves the product was defective, the burden shifts, and the factory is forced to prove they weren't careless.
Prove Defect, Shift Blame
What is a "Learned Intermediary"?
Click to flip
The Story: You are the technical middleman (the engineer) standing between the factory and the public.

The Rule: You have a legal duty to read the factory's warnings and explain the technical risks to the consumer.
The Technical Middleman
Do "Limitation Clauses" protect you from everyone?
Click to flip
The Shield: You can write a contract that limits how much you can be sued for.

The Catch: This ONLY stops the person who signed it. A stranger walking down the street can still sue you for everything you have.
Contracts Do Not Block Strangers
Is following the "Code" always a safe defense?
Click to flip
The Rule: Normally, yes.

The Exception: If you know the minimum code is outdated and a design is actually dangerous, you cannot hide behind the code. Minimum codes do not excuse ignoring obvious risks.
Minimum Code ≠ Maximum Safety
