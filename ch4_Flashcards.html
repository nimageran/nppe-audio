Real Property vs. Personal Property
Click to flip
The Basics: "Real Property" is land and anything permanently attached to it (like a building).

The Rule: "Personal Property" (also called Chattels) is anything movable, like a truck, a laptop, or an unbolted machine.
Real = Dirt | Personal = Movable
Chattels vs. Fixtures
Click to flip
The Story: You bring a heavy generator (a chattel) into a factory and bolt it permanently to the concrete foundation.

The Rule: It is now a "Fixture." It has legally become part of the Real Property and transfers to the new owner if the building is sold.
Bolted Down = Real Estate
Fee Simple
Click to flip
The Concept: The absolute highest, most complete level of land ownership you can hold in Canada.

The Catch: It is not perfect. The Crown (government) still has the right to expropriate it for public use, like building a highway.
The Best Ownership Possible
Joint Tenancy vs.
Tenancy in Common
Click to flip
Joint Tenancy: Co-owners have a "Right of Survivorship." If you die, your partner automatically gets your half. You cannot put it in a will.

Tenancy in Common: You own a distinct share. If you die, your share goes to your heirs, not your partner.
Joint = Survivor Takes All
Easements
Click to flip
The Story: Manitoba Hydro needs to run a power line across your farmland.

The Rule: An easement is a legal right to use someone else's land for a specific, limited purpose. It stays attached to the land even if the land is sold.
Legal Right to Cross Land
Restrictive Covenants
Click to flip
The Story: You buy a lot in a subdivision, and the deed says you cannot build a fence taller than 4 feet.

The Rule: A condition attached to the land that restricts what the owner can do. It must be negative (restricting action) to be valid.
Rules Tied to the Dirt
Patents
Click to flip
The Target: Protects new, useful, and non-obvious inventions (like a new gear mechanism).

The Deal: The government gives you a 20-year monopoly to make money off it, but in exchange, you MUST publish the design to the public.
Inventions: 20 Years + Full Disclosure
Copyright
Click to flip
The Target: Protects the physical expression of an idea (like engineering drawings, written reports, or software code), not the idea itself.

The Rule: It is automatic the moment you write it down. Lasts for the life of the author plus 50 (or 70) years.
Protects Expression, Not Ideas
Who owns the Copyright?
Click to flip
The Story: You write a brilliant software script while sitting at your desk at your engineering firm.

The Rule: Your employer automatically owns the copyright to anything you create during the course of your employment, unless your contract says otherwise.
Created at Work = Boss Owns It
Moral Rights (Copyright)
Click to flip
The Story: You design a beautiful bridge. The client buys the copyright but decides to paint it hot pink with polka dots, ruining your reputation.

The Catch: The creator always keeps "Moral Rights" to prevent the work from being distorted, even after selling the copyright, unless explicitly waived.
You Can Stop Them Ruining Your Art
Trademarks
Click to flip
The Target: Protects words, symbols, logos, or designs used to identify a brand (like the Nike swoosh or "Coca-Cola").

The Goal: To protect the public from being confused or tricked by knock-offs.
Protects Brands and Logos
Industrial Designs
Click to flip
The Target: Protects the visual appearance, shape, or pattern of an object.

The Catch: It ONLY protects how it looks, not how it works. (e.g., The cool shape of a sports car hood, but not the engine underneath it).
Protects the Look, Not the Function
Trade Secrets
Click to flip
The Story: The Coca-Cola recipe or Google's search algorithm.

The Rule: You do NOT register it with the government. It is only protected by NDAs and strict security. It lasts forever, but if someone legally reverse-engineers it, you lose your protection completely.
No Registration, Just Secrecy
Software Protection
Click to flip
The Rule: Source code is protected by Copyright (like a book). However, if the software controls a physical process or hardware in a novel way, that process might be protected by a Patent.
Code = Copyright | Process = Patent
