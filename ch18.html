CHAPTER 18: DEEP CONSTRUCTION LIENS VISUALIZATION

1. The Security Gap & The Lien Solution

In construction, payment follows performance[cite: 1]. You cannot repossess installed drywall or paint if you don't get paid[cite: 1]. The Lien act creates a legal bridge, allowing unpaid workers and suppliers to bypass the contractual chain and attack the owner's property directly[cite: 1].

Visualization Logic: This demonstrates why liens exist and how they bypass privity of contract[cite: 1].

  • The Security Gap: A supplier only has a contract with the subcontractor[cite: 1]. If the subcontractor goes bankrupt, suing them for "breach of contract" is a hollow remedy because they have no assets[cite: 1].
  • The Lien: The statute creates a right to register a claim directly against the title of the real property that was improved by the supplier's materials[cite: 1]. This makes the property difficult to sell or mortgage, forcing the owner to deal with the unpaid supplier's claim[cite: 1].
  • Federal Exception: You cannot file a lien against the title of a federally owned property, though trust provisions may still apply[cite: 1].

2. The Lien Lifecycle & Substitute Security

A lien is just a claim until it is proven in court[cite: 1]. There are strict, unforgiving deadlines (limitation periods) that govern how a lien survives[cite: 1].

Owner can now sell/mortgage land. C->>R: 3. "Perfects" the Lien (Starts Lawsuit + Lis Pendens) Note over C,O: Lawsuit now targets the Substitute Security,
not the land itself.

Visualization Logic: This sequence shows the procedural hurdles of keeping a lien alive[cite: 1].

  • Filing the Lien: Must be filed within a strict limitation period (e.g., days after substantial completion or last day of work)[cite: 1]. Missing this deadline destroys the lien right, though the right to sue for breach of contract remains[cite: 1].
  • Substitute Security: An owner does not want their property frozen[cite: 1]. They can pay the value of the lien (plus security for costs) into court using cash, a letter of credit, or a lien bond[cite: 1]. This clears the property title, and the lien simply transfers onto this new pile of money[cite: 1].
  • Perfecting the Lien: To keep the lien alive, the claimant must actually start a lawsuit and often file a "lis pendens" (certificate of pending litigation) within a specified time[cite: 1]. If they fail, the lien is dismissed[cite: 1].

3. The Statutory Trust Waterfall

Money paid on a construction project is legally protected as it flows downward[cite: 1]. Using project money for anything other than paying your direct workers and suppliers is a severe legal offense[cite: 1].

graph TD Owner[Owner] -->|Pays $100k| GC[General Contractor] GC -->|Fails to pay Subs| Bad[Buys New Truck / Pays Bank Loan] GC -.->|Must pay first| Subs[Subcontractors / Workers] Bad --> Breach((BREACH OF TRUST)) Breach --> Conseq1[Directors & Officers Personally Liable] Breach --> Conseq2[Quasi-Criminal Penalties / Jail] style Owner fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0284c7 style GC fill:#1e293b,color:#fff style Subs fill:#dcfce7,stroke:#16a34a style Bad fill:#fee2e2,stroke:#dc2626 style Breach fill:#991b1b,color:#fff,stroke-width:3px style Conseq1 fill:#f8fafc,stroke:#94a3b8 style Conseq2 fill:#f8fafc,stroke:#94a3b8

Visualization Logic: This illustrates the lethal danger of co-mingling funds[cite: 1].

  • The Trust: As soon as the owner pays the General Contractor, those funds are impressed with a statutory trust[cite: 1]. The GC is the trustee, and the subs are beneficiaries[cite: 1]. The GC must pay the beneficiaries before taking any profit or paying overhead[cite: 1].
  • The Penalty: Breaching this trust can trigger quasi-criminal penalties (fines or jail) and pierce the corporate veil, making directors and officers personally liable to repay the stolen funds[cite: 1].
  • The Mackenzie Redi-Mix Exception: If a GC pays a Subcontractor in full, the GC's trust obligation to that specific branch is discharged[cite: 1]. The GC does not become a guarantor if that fully-paid Sub then steals the money and fails to pay their own suppliers[cite: 1].

4. Holdback Mechanics & Pro-Rata Math

A holdback is a percentage of the contract (typically 10%) kept out of the hands of the contractor until the end of the project to serve as a reserve fund for potential liens[cite: 1].

graph TD Owner[Owner] -->|Retains 10% from GC| Holdback1[Owner's Holdback Fund: $100,000] GC[General Contractor] -->|Retains 10% from Sub| Holdback2[GC's Holdback Fund: $10,000] Holdback2 -->|Sub defaults owing $50,000| Claimants[Supplier 1 & Supplier 2] Claimants -.->|Liens total $50k, but fund is only $10k| Math[PRO-RATA SHARING] Math -->|Supplier 1 gets 20% of their claim| S1[Supplier 1: Paid $5,000] Math -->|Supplier 2 gets 20% of their claim| S2[Supplier 2: Paid $5,000] style Owner fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0284c7 style GC fill:#f1f5f9,stroke:#64748b style Holdback1 fill:#fef3c7,stroke:#d97706,stroke-width:2px style Holdback2 fill:#fef3c7,stroke:#d97706,stroke-width:2px style Claimants fill:#fee2e2,stroke:#dc2626 style Math fill:#1e293b,color:#fff style S1 fill:#dcfce7,stroke:#16a34a style S2 fill:#dcfce7,stroke:#16a34a

Visualization Logic: This diagrams the math of Case Study 18.3[cite: 1].

  • Maximum Exposure: A party who correctly retains the statutory holdback has their maximum liability capped at that holdback amount[cite: 1]. If the GC holds back $10,000 from a Sub, and the Sub's unpaid suppliers file $50,000 in liens, the GC only has to pay the $10,000 into court to clear the liens[cite: 1].
  • Pro-Rata Sharing: Because the $10,000 fund is too small to cover the $50,000 debt, the suppliers share the available money proportionally[cite: 1]. Here, the fund is 20% of the total debt, so each supplier gets 20% of their respective claim[cite: 1].
  • Single vs. Multiple: In a Multiple Holdback system (BC, ON, SK), everyone holds back 10% from the party below them[cite: 1]. In a Single Holdback system (AB), only the Owner holds back money from the GC[cite: 1].

5. The General Contractor's Defensive Shield

A General Contractor can do everything right and still be forced to pay twice for the same work if a subcontractor goes bankrupt and leaves unpaid suppliers[cite: 1]. The GC must deploy active defenses[cite: 1].

Right of Set-Off] D1 -.->|Shifts payment risk to the Surety| Safe1[GC is protected] D2 -.->|Sub swears under oath everyone is paid| Safe2[Sub faces criminal perjury if lying] D3 -.->|Allows GC to deduct costs to beat CRA priority| Safe3[Protects Holdback from Tax Agency] style Threat fill:#fee2e2,stroke:#dc2626,stroke-width:2px style Shield fill:#1e293b,color:#fff style D1 fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0284c7 style D2 fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0284c7 style D3 fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0284c7 style Safe1 fill:#dcfce7,stroke:#16a34a style Safe2 fill:#dcfce7,stroke:#16a34a style Safe3 fill:#dcfce7,stroke:#16a34a

Visualization Logic: This outlines how a GC prevents paying for the same work twice[cite: 1].

  • Bonds: Requiring the sub to post a Labour and Material (L&M) Payment Bond means the bonding company (Surety) steps in to pay the unpaid suppliers, saving the GC[cite: 1].
  • Statutory Declarations: The GC requires a sworn legal document from the sub before releasing the monthly check, declaring that all lower-tier workers and suppliers have been paid[cite: 1]. Lying on this triggers criminal perjury charges[cite: 1].
  • Set-Off vs. CRA: If a sub goes bankrupt owing taxes, the CRA will issue a "Requirement to Pay" demanding the owner/GC send the sub's money to the government[cite: 1]. The PCL Constructors case proved that if the contract contains a "Right of Set-Off," the GC can use that money to pay the unpaid suppliers instead of sending it to the CRA[cite: 1].
