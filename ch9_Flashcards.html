The Law of Agency
Click to flip
The Story: A client (Principal) hires an engineer (Agent) to negotiate with a contractor (Third Party).

The Rule: The Agent is just a middleman. Any contract the Agent signs binds the Principal directly to the Third Party. The Agent steps completely out of the legal picture.
The Middleman Binds the Boss
Actual Authority
Click to flip
The Basics: The legal power an employer specifically gives to an employee.

The Rule: It can be written (a contract saying "You can buy up to $50k in steel") or implied (the normal duties an engineer in your position would normally handle).
What You Are Actually Allowed To Do
Apparent Authority
Click to flip
The Trap: Your boss secretly tells you NOT to buy steel. But you wear the company hardhat and order steel from a supplier anyway.

The Rule: The company MUST pay the supplier. The supplier had a reasonable right to believe you had authority. (Your boss can fire you later, but the contract stands).
Looking Like a Boss = Binding the Boss
Indemnification Clauses
(Hold Harmless)
Click to flip
The Story: An owner says, "If I get sued because your crane dropped a beam, you pay my lawyers."

The Rule: A contractual agreement where one party legally promises to absorb the financial losses or legal fees of the other party.
You Pay My Lawsuits
Change Orders
Click to flip
The Story: The owner decides to add a third floor to a two-story building after construction starts.

The Rule: A Change Order is literally a mini-amendment to the contract. It is only valid if both sides agree on the new scope, the new price, and the new schedule.
Agreed Change to the Contract
Change Directive
Click to flip
The Story: The owner demands the new floor be built TODAY, but you cannot agree on a price yet.

The Rule: A legal command forcing the contractor to do the work immediately so the project doesn't stall. The exact payment amount will be fought over and decided later.
Do It Now, Argue Price Later
"Pay-When-Paid" Clauses
Click to flip
The Trap: A General Contractor (GC) tells a Subcontractor, "I'll pay you when the Owner pays me." The Owner goes bankrupt.

The Rule: Courts treat this as a timing delay, NOT an escape hatch. The GC must still pay the Subcontractor eventually, even if the GC never gets paid.
Just a Delay, Not an Excuse
"Pay-If-Paid" Clauses
Click to flip
The Exception: The GC uses extremely strict, brutal language saying the Subcontractor takes 100% of the risk of the Owner going bankrupt.

The Rule: Courts hate this, but if the language is perfectly clear, they will enforce it. If the Owner doesn't pay, the Sub gets nothing.
Strict Words Transfer the Risk
Unjust Enrichment
Click to flip
The Story: A contractor paves your driveway by accident. You watch them do it from your window, say nothing, and get a free driveway.

The Rule: The court will force you to pay for it. You cannot knowingly and unfairly benefit from someone else's labor at their expense.
No Free Rides
Waiver
Click to flip
The Story: A contract says "All extras must be in writing." The owner asks for 5 verbal changes and pays for them. On the 6th, he refuses because it wasn't in writing.

The Rule: By ignoring the rule 5 times, the owner "waived" his right to suddenly enforce it. He must pay.
Ignoring the Rule Kills the Rule
