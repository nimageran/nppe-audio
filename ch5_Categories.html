4 Principal Forms of Business Organization4 items
▾
1
Sole Proprietorship— individual carrying on business; unlimited personal liability
2
Partnership— group pooling resources; jointly liable for all debts
3
Corporation— separate legal person; owned by shareholders; liability limited to corporate assets
4
Joint Venture— two or more parties act together but retain separate legal status

3 Types of Partnerships3 items
▾
1
General Partnership— all partners have unlimited joint liability for all debts; each can enter contracts on behalf of all
2
Limited Partnership— at least one general partner (unlimited liability, runs business) + at least one limited partner (liable only for their financial contribution). Limited partners cannot actively participate or lose limited status.
3
Limited Liability Partnership (LLP)— each partner's liability limited to their own acts only; other partners' personal assets not at risk. Must carry prescribed level of liability insurance.

3 Roles in a Corporation3 items
▾
1
Shareholders— own the corporation proportionally; vote to elect directors and authorize major changes; do not run the business
2
Directors— direct the business; elected by shareholders; elect officers; must be individuals (not corporations)
3
Officers— president, vice-president, corporate secretary; provide closer operational direction; elected by directors; must be individuals

2 Types of Corporations2 items
▾
1
Private (Closely Held) Corporation— shares held by small group; cannot offer shares to public; financial records not public (but names/addresses of directors/officers/shareholders must be available)
2
Public Corporation— shares publicly traded on a stock exchange; stringent legislative rules; accounting records filed and publicly accessible

10
Fiduciary Duties — Who Owes Them to Whom2 items
▾
1
Partners → Other Partners— cannot compete, must act in best interest of partnership, declare conflicts of interest. Cannot be negated by partnership agreement.
2
Officers & Directors → The Corporation— NOT to shareholders. Must act honestly, in good faith, be loyal, declare conflicts. Only the corporation can sue for breach (or minority shareholders via derivative action).

Insider Trading — 2 Key Distinctions2 items
▾
1
Lawful Insider Trading— trading shares while connected to a corporation IS allowed as long as no non-public privileged information is used
2
Unlawful Insider Trading— using or sharing privileged non-public information to trade. Includes stock tipping. Both civil AND criminal penalties. Applies to commodities markets too.
