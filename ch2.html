Visualization Logic: The critical distinction this diagram protects is that these are TWO SEPARATE rights, not one combined package — a profession with only title protection (like interior design) controls who gets to use a fancy label, but cannot stop an unlicensed person from actually DOING the work itself. Engineering, architecture, and geoscience are unusual specifically because they hold BOTH pillars together, meaning unauthorized practice is illegal regardless of what title the person uses or doesn't use — someone performing engineering work while carefully avoiding the word "engineer" is still breaching scope of practice, even though they haven't breached right to title. The PEI/Yukon exception for geoscience is worth remembering as a specific, testable fact: it shows the two pillars genuinely CAN be split apart even within these three core professions, not just as a hypothetical possibility but as an actual real-world example sitting right in the chapter's own text.

The Violation branch at the bottom is a standalone warning worth remembering separately: jumping the gun and using "engineer" before completing this progression isn't just an informal faux pas, it's MISREPRESENTATION that can trigger formal enforcement action plus separate civil exposure — the title itself is a legally guarded asset, not just a professional courtesy.

Visualization Logic: The Circular branch is the heart of this diagram because it captures something the chapter states plainly but is easy to gloss over: the Engineers Canada definition, despite being the OFFICIAL, governing definition of the entire profession, openly acknowledges its own circularity — defining engineering practice by reference to "engineering principles" is somewhat tautological, since you'd need to already know what counts as an engineering principle to apply the definition in the first place. This isn't presented as a drafting failure, though — the chapter frames it as reflecting the genuine, inherent DIFFICULTY of precisely bounding any profession, which is exactly why ambiguous edge cases (is THIS specific activity engineering or not?) end up being resolved by courts on a case-by-case basis rather than through a perfectly precise statutory checklist. The tradeoff implied in the Specific branch deserves equal attention: a highly detailed, itemized definition trades away that flexibility — it's clearer in obvious cases but can fail to anticipate genuinely new activities or technologies that don't map cleanly onto the pre-written list, creating its own different kind of gap.

Visualization Logic: This diagram exists because the chapter explicitly tells you WHY this exclusion exists — to "retain the distinction between the practice of engineering and the practice of natural science" — and that purpose only makes sense once you see it as a deliberate boundary-drawing mechanism rather than a random technical footnote. Without this carve-out, the generic engineering definition from diagram 3 (any act requiring "application of engineering principles" concerning safety/welfare) could theoretically be read broadly enough to swallow up legitimate scientific work performed by chemists, physicists, or mathematicians who have zero engineering credentials. The Q1 diamond's qualification path is worth noting carefully — it's not JUST about holding the right degree; "equivalent combination of education, training and experience" is explicitly included too, and the supervision branch (AlsoExcluded) extends the carve-out even further to people who DON'T personally hold the qualification but are working directly under someone who does, similar in spirit to how engineering interns work under licensed P.Engs.

Visualization Logic: The dotted line from Education feeding into the Exam diamond is the key relationship this diagram is built to show — these three components aren't fully independent, the EDUCATION component directly determines what the EXAM component will actually require. A candidate from an accredited program essentially gets a "lighter" exam load (law and ethics only), on the theory that the accreditation process has already verified their technical competence through the degree itself. A candidate from a non-accredited program hasn't had that technical verification built in elsewhere, so the regulatory body compensates by adding technical examinations on top of the standard law/ethics requirement. The architecture and US-engineering comparisons mentioned elsewhere in the chapter (NCARB's nine exams, NCEES in the US) are worth holding as separate reference points — they show this same three-component skeleton (experience + education + exam) repeating across different professions and countries, just with different specific exam bodies and structures layered on top of the same underlying framework.

Visualization Logic: The WrongAssumption box is placed deliberately to catch the intuitive-but-incorrect answer many people would give to this exact scenario — it FEELS reasonable that your "home province" registration should be the one that matters, but the actual rule tracks something completely different: WHERE the practice is actually happening, regardless of where the professional's office or home base sits. This means design work done remotely from Saskatchewan FOR a Yukon project still counts as practising in the Yukon, and on-site inspection work in Ontario counts as practising in Ontario — three separate locations of actual practice, three separate registration requirements, even though it's all one continuous project for one client. The Reciprocity branch exists to soften what would otherwise sound like a genuinely heavy administrative burden — these agreements don't ELIMINATE the multi-jurisdiction requirement, they make SATISFYING it easier by allowing credentials earned in one jurisdiction to count toward registration in another, extending in some cases (per the IntlExamples box) well beyond Canada's own provincial borders into specific reciprocal relationships with other countries.

Visualization Logic: The WhoTarget diamond is the single fact that cleanly separates these two terms, and it directly answers the chapter's own chapter question 8 (is a disciplinary hearing a criminal proceeding — no, it's quasi-criminal). Discipline is internal-facing — it's the profession holding its OWN registered members accountable for incompetence or ethics breaches, essentially the regulatory body policing people who voluntarily joined and agreed to its rules. Enforcement is external-facing — it's the regulatory body asserting its legal monopoly against OUTSIDERS who never joined at all, whether by stealing the protected title or by performing protected work without a licence. Both share the same "quasi-criminal" classification and procedural seriousness, which is worth remembering as a single combined fact: NEITHER process is literally a criminal proceeding (no criminal record results, the Crown isn't prosecuting), but BOTH are treated with comparable rigour and strict standards of proof specifically because the practical stakes — someone's professional livelihood — are genuinely severe.

Visualization Logic: The three C-boxes at the top matter because they show sealing isn't a single, narrow claim — it can legitimately represent ANY of three different relationships to a document (personal authorship, supervision, or review/checking), but it must represent AT LEAST ONE of them truthfully. The Gap box captures the chapter's central warning about seal misuse: the practical danger isn't usually outright fraud, it's a professional sealing something out of habit, convenience, or external pressure WITHOUT having actually done the preparing or checking the seal is supposed to certify — turning a routine administrative act into a false professional representation. The letter-of-assurance branch is connected to this same diagram because it shares the same underlying logic but raises the stakes further: these letters are often extremely broad certifications (the whole building matches the plans), and the chapter's practical advice is to make that assessment BEFORE accepting the project at all — discovering partway through that you're unwilling to sign the assurance document the client needs is a much worse position than declining the engagement upfront.
