4 Terms Implied Into Every Employment Contract4 items
▾
1
Duty to Mitigate— most often applied where the employer has improperly terminated the relationship; employee must seek suitable alternate employment
2
Employee's Duty of Fidelity (Loyalty)— employees are agents of the employer; includes not disclosing/using confidential information

Employer's Duty to Give Adequate Notice or Pay Severance— severance = salary + benefits that would accrue during the notice period

4 Attributes of a Trade Secret4 items
▾
1
Used in a particular industry
2
Not widely known in that industry
3
Has value BECAUSE it is not widely known
4
Company makes reasonable efforts to keep it secret
Trade secrets aren't only "secret formulas" — day-to-day operational info (e.g. profit margin formulas, client lists not derivable from public sources) can qualify. A client list generated from a phone book is NOT confidential; one that's difficult to recreate likely IS.
3
Confidential Information vs Skill/Experience2 items · Monarch Messenger
▾
1
Confidential / "Special or Peculiar" Information— CANNOT be used after termination for the employee's own benefit. Obligation begins DAY ONE and survives termination. Re-creating it from memory is still actionable. Profits from breach may be disgorged via a constructive trust.
2
Skill and General Knowledge— employee is FREE to use this for their own benefit or a new employer's, even if acquired during the prior job
Example: Mary can take her geological-mapping software SKILLS to a new employer, but cannot disclose that her old employer's confidential plan was to search for diamonds in Nunavut specifically.
4
Restrictive Covenants — 2-Part Enforceability Test2 items
▾
1
Reasonableness— scope (time period, geographic area, area of business) must be no broader than necessary to protect the employer's legitimate interest. E.g. 15 years/same field/same province = likely unenforceable; 1 year/same city/narrow practice area = more likely enforceable. No "bright line" — depends on facts (e.g. role/duration of specialized work).
2
Clarity— ambiguous terms render the WHOLE covenant unenforceable (Shafron v. KRG — "Metropolitan City of Vancouver" had no legal definition). Applies to ANY ambiguous element, not just geography.
Terra Engineering v. Stewart: A 20-month, 150km covenant for one of only ~15 materials engineers in BC was struck down — (1) total prohibition on practising the profession exceeded what was needed to protect the employer's interest, and (2) the geographic radius effectively excluded most major population centres. Public interest strongly disfavours keeping skilled people out of the market.
5
2 Categories of Termination2 items
▾
1
For Cause (Just Cause / Sufficient Cause)— employee committed a FUNDAMENTAL breach destroying the employer/employee trust; no notice or severance required
2
Without Cause— employer must give REASONABLE NOTICE or pay severance in lieu
No layoff right at common law: Unlike unionized settings (Ch.20), there is NO right of layoff or recall at common law — "laying off" with a promise to "call back" is generally just dismissal without cause (the draftsman example).
6
6 Guidelines for "Just Cause"6 items
▾
1
Breach serious enough to destroy employer–employee trust → may be just cause
2
Dishonesty and theft— usually just cause
3
Minor/isolated breaches (e.g. tardiness)— NOT just cause alone, but repeated incidents WITH escalating warnings can cumulatively become just cause
4
Gross incompetence or insubordination— may constitute just cause in some circumstances. BUT: refusing demeaning, unsafe, or out-of-scope work is NOT insubordination (e.g. P.Eng. refusing to compromise a safety factor)
5
Isolated intoxication incidents— not usually just cause without prior warnings
6
Lack of business / downturn— NOT just cause
Richardson v. Davis Wire: Sleeping on the job (without prior warnings) was NOT just cause, but LYING about it when confronted WAS just cause.

Factors Affecting "Reasonable Notice"key factors
▾
1
Age of the employee
2
Length of service
3
Type/seniority of position held
4
External factors— e.g. whether the employee was enticed away from a prior secure job
No precise formula — courts use ranges from similar cases. Example: junior estimator (1 yr) ≈ 1 month notice; senior executive (20 yrs) ≈ 18–24 months. Wallace principle: bad faith in the MANNER of dismissal can extend the notice period (employer owes a duty of good faith/fair dealing in HOW termination is carried out).

Employment Standards Legislation — Key Areaskey items
▾
1
Minimum wage— varies by province; MOST PROFESSIONALS (architects, engineers, geoscientists) are typically EXCLUDED (e.g. BC, NB, Ontario)
2
Hours of work / overtime— e.g. overtime triggers after 7.5 hrs/day or 38 hrs/week
3
Minimum vacation pay and holiday pay
4
Minimum notice of termination— usually MUCH LOWER than common law (e.g. 3 weeks statutory vs 6 months common law)
5
Maternity and parental leave
Why use the statute despite lower amounts? Enforcement is FREE — file a complaint and the government pursues the employer, vs the cost of suing at common law. Can be more cost-effective for smaller claims (e.g. junior employee owed only a few weeks).
10
Human Rights — Prohibited vs NOT Prohibited Grounds2 categories
▾
1
PROHIBITED Grounds— religion, ethnic origin, skin colour, age, disability, gender (general categories — full lists vary by province)
2
NOT Prohibited — "Employability Skills"— education, experience, length of service. Employers MAY discriminate on these (e.g. pay/hire based on higher education is fine).
Disability — special rule: Discrimination based on disability is only permitted where the disability genuinely affects ability to perform the work AND no reasonable accommodation is possible (e.g. modified equipment/furniture). If the job genuinely requires high physical ability (e.g. firefighter carrying a person), discrimination on that basis is allowed.
Harassment: Everyone has a right to a workplace free of physical/sexual/psychological harassment. Employers aware of harassment MUST act immediately — even minor incidents — or face vicarious liability.
