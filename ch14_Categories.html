4 Methods of Dispute Resolution — Voluntary vs Involuntary4 items
▾
1
Negotiation— purely voluntary; forcing it is futile
2
Litigation— voluntary for plaintiff (no recovery if not pursued), INVOLUNTARY for defendant (judgment awarded if non-participant)
3
Arbitration— voluntary at contract negotiation stage (parties choose whether to include the clause); but once a MANDATORY arbitration clause exists, it can be forced on the other party. Always available even without a clause/contract.
4
Mediation— can be agreed before or after dispute arises, but is NON-BINDING; a party can always refuse to settle

6 Types of Pleadings6 items
▾
1
Statement of Claim— commences litigation; identifies parties, allegations, and relief sought (in BC: "Notice of Civil Claim"; in US: "Complaint")
2
Statement of Defence— sets out legal basis for denying the claim; failure to deny an allegation may = admission (typically 14–21 days to file)
3
Reply— plaintiff's response to statement of defence; rarely used
4
Demand for Particulars
5
Interrogatories— written questions answered under oath
6
Motions
Key point: Pleadings are the ONLY documents the judge sees prior to trial commencement. A statement of claim that fails to set out a cause of action (e.g. alleges a duty but not a breach) will be struck — though this rarely happens.
3
Counterclaim vs Third-Party Claim2 items
▾
1
Counterclaim— a claim the DEFENDANT asserts against the PLAINTIFF. No rule limiting it to the size of the original claim — can be much larger.
2
Third-Party Claim— pleading by the DEFENDANT against a THIRD PARTY not originally part of the suit
Why include third parties in the main action: If heard separately, the third party is NOT bound by findings of fact from the main action — risk of inconsistent results, different witnesses/evidence, and the defendant may have to fight a second battle later.

4 Discovery Procedures4 items
▾
1
Examination for Discovery— oral cross-examination of a representative of the opposing party before trial; transcript can be used to IMPEACH a witness whose trial testimony differs
2
Interrogatories— written questions answered under oath; sent shortly after pleadings to identify knowledgeable witnesses
3
Inspection of Property— on-site inspection, often with litigant, lawyer, and expert witness
4
Discovery of Documents— ongoing identification of all RELEVANT documents (test: does it tend to prove or disprove a fact in dispute), except privileged documents
Email warning: Emails are a "gold mine" in litigation because written informally. Treat every email as you would a signed and sealed report.

3 Advantages of Negotiated Settlements3 items
▾
1
Parties maintain control over the procedure
2
Much less costly than litigation and arbitration
3
Eliminates uncertainty once settlement reached
Privilege: If negotiation fails, everything discussed is privileged — cannot be used as evidence later. Same applies to mediation.

3 Requirements for Mediation to Succeed3 items
▾
1
Genuine desire by both parties to reach settlement
2
Each party represented by someone with authority to conclude a settlement
3
Mediator has the trust of all parties
Unlike a judge/arbitrator, a mediator MAY meet privately with each party and must keep confidences shared in those meetings.

Expert Witness — Key Rules3 items
▾
1
Must remain neutral and unbiased— role is to assist the COURT, not to act as a "hired gun" for the party that retained them
2
Cannot charge a contingent fee— a fee contingent on outcome creates a conflict between client interest and public interest; disclosure of such a fee likely renders the opinion INADMISSIBLE (real case: 3-month suspension for an engineer who did this)
3
Must present the most credible theory while acknowledging other plausible explanations— presenting an unlikely theory as equally acceptable is unprofessional conduct
Opinion evidence is normally inadmissible — expert opinion is a recognized EXCEPTION for technical matters beyond a layperson's knowledge. Standard-of-care testimony is judged on balance of probabilities (more likely than not), not "beyond a reasonable doubt."
