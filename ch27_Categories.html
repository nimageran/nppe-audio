Jurisdiction (Conflict of Laws) — Key Challenges2 items
▾
1
Contracts must specify GOVERNING LAW and COURTS— e.g. a contract signed in BC and Washington, with work done in India for use in Sweden — must clearly state which jurisdiction's laws/courts apply. But this alone may not resolve LOCAL law issues (employment, taxation, safety, privacy) that can still apply to the work performed.
2
Borderless nature of the Internet → multiple jurisdictions may claim authority— courts (especially US courts) may take jurisdiction simply because a wrongful activity affected residents in their jurisdiction. A business may face risk from unknown laws/court systems applying to its online activity.

Defamation Online — Key Rules3 items
▾
1
Defamation = communication that injures another's reputation— includes SLANDER (oral) and LIBEL (written); definitions vary by jurisdiction
2
Merely unflattering/annoying/embarrassing ≠ actionable— the communication must injure the party in a SIGNIFICANT way, not just hurt feelings
3
Website communications treated like radio/TV broadcasts— defamatory posts/emails can result in libel or slander actions; negligent advice posted online can support a MISREPRESENTATION claim, same as any other medium

Copyright Online — ISP Liability & Protection3 items
▾
1
US: ISPs liable only if they ACTIVELY ENGAGE/PARTICIPATE in infringement— Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA) protects ISPs for activities BEYOND their knowledge/control
2
CANADA has NO equivalent legislation to the DMCA
3
Courts remain the only real recourse— copyright holders should: register the copyright (proves publication), have licensing agreements in place, use proper copyright markings throughout documents, and consider electronic fingerprinting/technological tools as evidence
4
Trademarks & Domain Names — Cybersquatting2 items
▾
1
Domain names = first-come, first-served via approved registrars— subject to the same trademark laws as corporate names. Owners must ACTIVELY police/protect trademarks or risk losing them.
2
Cybersquatting = unlawful trademark use— pre-emptively registering domain names containing well-known trademarks/trade names, then offering to sell to the rightful owner. Courts have found this (a) likely to confuse/mislead Internet users, AND (b) constitutes MISREPRESENTATION regarding the trademark owner's approval/authorization/endorsement.

Privacy & Securities Regulation — Governing Principles2 items
▾
1
Privacy (Ch.26 / PIPEDA) APPLIES to Internet activities— especially relevant for e-commerce (rapid electronic collection/transmission of personal info). "Click to agree" policies should be CLEAR and meet privacy legislation requirements, even though most users don't read them.
2
Securities laws (Ch.25) apply EQUALLY to the Internet as to any other medium— the governing principle: no special carve-out for online securities activity

Electronic Contracts — Key Rules5 items
▾
1
Canadian electronic commerce/evidence laws follow the Uniform Electronic Commerce Act— provide certainty for formation, performance, enforcement of e-contracts (don't solve EVERY issue, but give a framework)
2
"I Agree" contracts are valid and binding— the software making the offer acts as an ELECTRONIC AGENT for the offeror, treated as effective as a human agent
3
Timing rules for delivery/receipt— DELIVERY = instant the message enters a computer system BEYOND the sender's control; RECEIPT = instant it enters a system the receiver CAN access. Parties may set their OWN rules by contract.
4
"Notice and acceptance by conduct" agreements— users deemed to accept by engaging in conduct (e.g. using a site, downloading software) — but must be CLEAR and UNAMBIGUOUS to be enforceable
5
Unusual/onerous terms must be drawn to the user's attention— same rule as conventional unsigned standard-form contracts (Ch.6). Parties should also verify identity/capacity/authority and authenticity — via e-signatures, cryptography, passwords, PINs, or traditional backups (phone confirmation, signed paper copies, credit card verification). Residual risk → contractual risk allocation + insurance.

8 Email Risks for a Communications System Policy (CSP)8 items
▾
1
Casual/spontaneous tone— created with less care than formal communications; often contains ill-considered statements
2
Effortless mass reproduction/distribution— damaging communications spread quickly at virtually no cost
3
One click → irretrievable distribution to unintended recipients— including unintended forwarding
4
Easy to forge ("spoofing") or alter before forwarding
5
Can be lost or delayed beyond sender/recipient control
6
Detailed transmittal metadata— who sent it, when, to whom, sometimes when received/read
7
Almost always recoverable— harder to truly eliminate than paper; data forensics can recover deleted emails
8
Susceptible to unauthorized access— at every computer/filing location where a copy resides
CSP best practice: A written Communications System Policy should govern internet/intranet/extranet, email, and voicemail use; educate users on these risks; be drafted with LEGAL and TECHNICAL advice (consistent with applicable laws and any collective bargaining agreements); and have a SENIOR MANAGEMENT owner responsible for implementation. Vmail shares most of these risks but is generally easier to erase than email.
