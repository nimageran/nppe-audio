Sole Proprietorship
Click to flip
The Basics: A business owned and run by exactly one person.

The Trap: There is absolutely zero legal separation between you and the business. If the business goes bankrupt or gets sued, the court can seize your personal bank accounts and your house.
Unlimited Personal Liability
General Partnership
Click to flip
The Basics: Two or more people running a business together to make a profit.

The Trap: Every single partner is an agent of the other. If your partner signs a terrible contract behind your back, you are legally bound to pay for it.
You Bleed for Their Mistakes
Joint and Several Liability
Click to flip
The Story: Your firm gets sued for $1 Million. Your partner goes bankrupt and runs away.

The Rule: The plaintiff does not just take your 50%. "Joint and Several" means the plaintiff can demand the entire $1 Million directly from YOU.
100% on the Hook
Limited Partnership
Click to flip
The Basics: A business with at least one General Partner (who runs things) and Limited Partners (who just provide money).

The Trap: If a Limited Partner starts making management decisions or acting like a boss, they instantly lose their liability shield and become a General Partner.
Silent Investors Must Stay Silent
Limited Liability Partnership (LLP)
Click to flip
The Story: Used by professionals like accountants, lawyers, and sometimes engineers.

The Rule: It protects your personal assets from the professional negligence of your partners. BUT, you are still personally liable for your *own* negligence.
Protects You from YOUR Partner
Corporation (The Concept)
Click to flip
The Basics: A corporation is a fictional "legal person" created by the government.

The Rule: It is completely separate from its owners. It can own property, sign contracts, sue, and be sued in its own name. It does not die when the owners die.
A Separate Legal Person

Directors vs. Officers
Click to flip
Shareholders: The owners. They elect directors. They have limited liability.
Directors: The brains. They make high-level decisions and hire officers.
Officers: The hands (CEO, CFO). They run the daily operations.
Own -> Guide -> Operate
Piercing the Corporate Veil
Click to flip
The Story: You set up a fake corporation just to commit fraud or hide stolen money.

The Rule: A judge can "pierce the veil," ignoring the corporate shield completely, and go straight after your personal house and bank accounts.
Fraud Breaks the Shield
Director's Fiduciary Duty
Click to flip
The Trick: If you are a Director, who do you owe your ultimate loyalty to?

The Rule: You owe your fiduciary duty to the Corporation itself, NOT to the shareholders. If the shareholders want to do something that kills the company, you must protect the company.
Loyalty to the Company First
Personal Liability of Directors
Click to flip
The Threat: Being a director is dangerous. The corporate shield does NOT protect directors from everything.

The Rule: Directors can be personally sued or jailed for unpaid employee wages, unpaid taxes, and environmental damage caused by the company.
Directors Pay for Taxes & Spills
Director's Conflict of Interest
Click to flip
The Story: You are a director of TechCorp, and TechCorp wants to buy land that you personally own.

The Rule: You MUST declare your conflict of interest, and you are legally forbidden from voting on the decision.
Declare and Step Back
Insider Trading
Click to flip
The Story: You know your company is about to win a massive government contract, so you buy stock before the news hits the public.

The Rule: Using undisclosed, material information to trade shares is a criminal offense.
Trading on Secrets = Prison
Public vs. Private Corporations
Click to flip
Public: Shares are traded openly on the stock market. Highly regulated by securities commissions.

Private: Shares are closely held (often by founders/family). You cannot sell your shares to the public, and transfer is heavily restricted.
Public = Open Market | Private = Restricted
