2 Types of Defects (for Limitation Purposes)2 items
▾
1
Patent Defect— obvious; identifiable upon reasonable inspection. Limitation period runs from when it occurred / was reasonably discoverable.
2
Latent Defect— hidden (e.g. software bugs, foundation defects). Limitation period does NOT start until plaintiff knew or ought to have known — can stretch for the professional's lifetime, subject to ULTIMATE limitation periods.
2
4 Protections from Indefinite Liability4 items
▾
1
Contractual Time Period Agreement— parties can agree to a shorter/longer period than the statute (depends on wording — some provinces' Limitation Acts prevent shortening)
2
Agreed Liability Limits— defined financial limit (e.g. $250,000), insurance available at the time, re-performance of services, or a combination
3
Ultimate Limitation Period— statutory cap beyond which NO claims can be brought, even for latent defects. Ranges from 10 to 30 years. ULTIMATE LIMITS TRUMP DISCOVERABILITY.
4
Professional Liability Insurance— must be maintained throughout the professional's lifetime since it is issued on a CLAIMS-MADE basis
Key case (Howe Sound): A 6-year contractual limitation clause extinguished a claim for a latent defect even though the client only discovered it AFTER the 6 years expired. Contractual protections apply only between contracting parties — NOT to third parties.
3
Joint & Several Liability vs Several Liability2 items
▾
1
Joint and Several Liability— applies where the PLAINTIFF IS NOT AT FAULT AT ALL. Plaintiff can recover 100% from ANY at-fault party; that party then seeks contribution from the others. If one party is bankrupt, the others absorb their share.
2
Several Liability— applies where the PLAINTIFF IS PARTIALLY AT FAULT (contributory negligence). Each party responsible ONLY for their own portion. The plaintiff bears the bankrupt party's unrecoverable share.
Worked example: Contractor 70% / Engineer 30% at fault, owner 0% at fault → owner can recover 100% from engineer; engineer then chases contractor for 70% (engineer absorbs the loss if contractor is defunct). If instead owner is 1% at fault (contributory negligence) in a several-liability province → engineer pays only 30%, owner absorbs the defunct contractor's 69%.
4
Vicarious Liability — 2 Key Conditions2 items
▾
1
Employer–Employee Relationship Must Exist— does NOT apply to independent contractors
2
Employee Must Be Acting Within Course and Scope of Employment— after-hours activity, side consulting, personal actions = employee's own liability; employer's insurance likely will NOT cover
Vicarious liability addresses liability to THIRD PARTIES only — it does not determine whether the employer can later recover from the negligent employee (rare in practice).
5
3 Categories of Codes & Standards3 items
▾
1
Legislated Codes— mandatory for EVERYONE in the industry (e.g. building codes)
2
Contractual Codes— mandatory only BETWEEN the contracting parties
3
Voluntary / Recommendation Codes— not mandatory, BUT in the event of failure can become a de facto minimum standard in court. Violation is not conclusive of negligence but can be persuasive evidence.
Important: Violation of a legislated or contractual code is almost always a breach of contract. A design departing substantially from codes is prima facie faulty UNLESS it can be shown to conform to accepted engineering practice via rational analysis.
6
4 Circumstances Where Code Compliance Is NOT a Full Defence4 items
▾
1
Professional has ACTUAL knowledge the code is inadequate
2
Professional SHOULD REASONABLY have known the code was inadequate— e.g. common industry knowledge after a study/accident, even if the code revision hasn't caught up
3
Courts hold an entire industry standard to be careless— rare; industry cannot set its own (inadequate) standard
4
Design is leading-edge / unique— traditional codes may not be relevant; different standard applies
New approach / cost-saving method: Professional must fully inform the client of risks BEFORE proceeding. If client makes an informed decision, client assumes that risk — but this is NO defence against third parties who did not accept the risk.
7
Limitation Period Start Date — 2 Standards2 items
▾
1
Kamloops "Reasonable Discoverability" Standard— time begins when plaintiff knew OR ought to have known through reasonable diligence. Applies in most provinces.
2
Actual Discovery Standard— Ontario and Saskatchewan negligence statutes: time runs from when the cause of action arises, though plaintiff may show it should run from actual discovery
General rule: Two years from the breach in ON, AB, BC, SK. Other provinces vary by action type. Always check the applicable provincial statute — limitation periods are occasionally amended.
