4 Key Questions for Risk Assessment4 items · example: technology contract
▾
1
What losses would each party suffer for each day of delay?
2
Who should bear the risk of the technology not working?
3
What if technological challenges are more difficult than expected?
4
Who will complete the work if the technology provider defaults?
Once risks are identified, parties decide whether to: (a) pass risk to third parties (subcontractors, suppliers, bonding companies, insurers), or (b) allocate it between the contracting parties by mutual agreement — e.g. insurance, performance bonds, bonus/liquidated damages clauses.

3 Common Law Presumptions About Professional Contracts3 items
▾
1
Professionals are sophisticated parties— terms presumed to be negotiated fairly; courts rarely accept "economic duress/hardship" arguments; "I would have refused if I'd understood" rarely succeeds
2
If a risk is addressed in the contract, courts let the contract decide— courts are reluctant to substitute their own risk allocation for the parties' choice
3
No presumption that professionals guarantee results— default standard = competence/care of the average practitioner. BUT if a professional represents (in contract, email, or conversation) that results are guaranteed, courts may hold them to it.
Implied warranties: Despite no guarantee presumption, the law may still imply certain warranties — e.g. a residence must be fit for human habitation, and minimum standards of quality/workmanship/skill/care apply. These presumptions CAN be modified by contract terms (e.g. CCDC 2 GC 3.4.1 limits contractor's review obligations for design errors).

Risk Shifting — 3 Options (Case Study 15.1)3 items
▾
1
Install the cheaper unit— risk: if it fails, client may sue and engineer could be found liable
2
Install the more expensive unit— risk: client may complain about unnecessary cost once it becomes apparent
3
Inform the client in writing of the trade-off— BEST option: explain the problem, consequences, and costs, and let the CLIENT make an informed decision. Client can no longer claim they'd have accepted a different outcome.

2 Types of Client Dissatisfaction2 items
▾
1
Justified Dissatisfaction— may result in the client winning the case
2
Perceived Dissatisfaction— often a function of poor communication, unrelated to actual quality of service; may result in the opposite outcome (client loses)
Key principle: Explanations given BEFORE a result occurs are far more effective and credible than explanations given AFTER — especially after litigation begins, when statements carry much less weight.

Common Causes of Fee-Dispute Counterclaims4 examples
▾
1
Underestimating extent of architectural involvement during construction— changes arise from contractor error, owner/tenant requests, unforeseen conditions, designer error
2
Underestimating number of required site review visits— e.g. contract specifies 1–2 visits/week; extra visits billed as "extras" the client didn't budget for
3
Underestimating extent of geophysical/geotechnical investigation needed— initial investigation may need costly supplementation
4
Poorly drafted retainer agreements that don't specify scope of services— e.g. failing to distinguish "supervision" (daily presence) from "periodic review" (lesser involvement)
Typical scenario: Professional bills final fee instalment → client refuses, alleging errors/omissions exceeded budget → professional sues for fee → client counterclaims for negligence (often FAR larger than the original fee claim).

Where Disclaimers Can Appear5 forms
▾
1
Written into the contract itself— exclusion, limitation, or waiver clauses (consequential damages, no-damage-for-delay, pay-when-paid)
2
Posted in conspicuous places
3
Contained in letters
4
A note or paragraph in a geotechnical/geoscience report— commonly addresses report accuracy and reliance limitations
5
On drawings or specifications (or stamps on shop drawings)— e.g. "review limited to general conformity with design intent, not a detailed review"
2 Requirements for an Enforceable Disclaimer
1
Must be brought to the attention of the party against whom it is intended
2
Must be carefully drafted— if too broad, a court may find it unenforceable (see Zhu v. Merrill Lynch — disclaimer that effectively excused gross negligence was struck down)

2 Methods to Authenticate Documents in Court2 items
▾
1
Paper Documents— authentication tests developed through case law
2
Electronic Documents— federal/provincial Evidence Acts assess accuracy, genuineness, and authenticity; requires proving the integrity of the system that created/stored the document
Photographs/videos ARE considered "documents" for discovery purposes. Digital photos are less readily accepted due to ease of editing — a documented chain of custody is essential.

8 Best Practices for Digital Photo Evidence8 items
▾
1
Start a paper audit trail immediately— description, time, place, downloading, backups, and who performed each step
2
Check equipment routinely— internal settings, time/date settings, storage space, battery
3
Select image quality based on conditions, not storage capacity
4
Never delete an image— intentional or accidental deletion raises questions
5
Designate one computer for storing photos — with NO editing software
6
Create an immediate backup on non-editable media (e.g. WORM CD)— maintain multiple copies; keep master and working copies separate, with recorded access to the master
7
Maintain printed and labelled versions of the photographs
8
Maintain masters of the backup with potential evidential value
