This two-phase structure explains why "is there a contract" and "is the contract enforceable as written" are actually different questions that can have different answers.

Visualization Logic: The word "Trap" at the third stage is deliberate, because this is exactly the kind of result that catches people off guard — the developer probably FEELS like they're simply "going back to" the original deal, but legally, that original $100K/6-month offer has been dead since the moment the developer counter-offered with $110K in round one. A counter-offer doesn't pause or shelve the prior offer for later use, it KILLS it outright, the same as an explicit rejection would. This is precisely why the chapter's own chapter question 7 answer is "No, there is no contract" — what looks like the developer "accepting the original deal" is actually the developer making a brand-new offer that happens to have the same terms as the long-dead original, and the client is completely free to accept, reject, or counter it again, with zero obligation to honor it just because it matches something said earlier in the conversation.

3. The Consideration Flow

Consideration has to flow in BOTH directions for a contract to be enforceable — a one-way flow is just a gift, and gifts aren't contracts. This diagram makes that bidirectional requirement, and its single exception, visible.

Visualization Logic: The two-way arrow structure is the core image to internalize — consideration genuinely has to flow in BOTH directions, even if the value exchanged is wildly unequal (the chapter is explicit that courts rarely police whether the deal was a "good" one for either side, only whether SOMETHING of value moved each way). The "OneWay" branch captures the gift problem directly: a promise where nothing flows back is unenforceable no matter how sincerely it was made or how much the recipient relied on it emotionally, because without an exchange, courts treat it as a gift rather than a bargain. The seal exception at the bottom is worth remembering as a genuine legal workaround, not just historical trivia — it's literally a substitute mechanism the law created specifically to make ONE-directional promises enforceable when the formality of a seal (or, for individuals today, nominal consideration like one dollar) is used instead.

4. Past Consideration vs Fresh Consideration

This is the chapter's highest-yield trap, built directly from the window contractor example and Smith v. Dawson. A mid-contract demand for more money is only enforceable if something genuinely NEW is being given in exchange — and "I won't abandon what I already owed you anyway" doesn't count.

Visualization Logic: The single diamond at the top is the entire test, and it's worth phrasing as a question you can run on any "demand for more money mid-contract" fact pattern: is the other side giving up or adding something they DIDN'T already legally owe? The window contractor scenario nails the failing case perfectly — threatening to not finish on time, then "agreeing" to finish on time after getting paid more, isn't really giving anything new at all, since finishing on time was already the contractor's existing legal obligation under the original deal. The Smith v. Dawson case adds a second layer worth remembering: even something as dramatic-sounding as "giving up my right to abandon the project" failed as consideration, because Riddell J.'s reasoning treated that "right" skeptically — the contractor's underlying legal duty to perform never actually disappeared just because he threatened not to, so there was nothing genuinely NEW being forfeited. Contrast that against the diagram's right branch: an actual schedule acceleration is something concretely beyond the original deal, which is exactly why the chapter notes that specific change WOULD have supported the price increase.

A letter of intent is meant to be a safe, non-binding placeholder before the real contract gets negotiated. This diagram shows the three specific ways that safety assumption breaks down in practice.

Visualization Logic: Each of the three problem branches independently undermines the "safe placeholder" assumption people bring to letters of intent, which is why all three deserve separate attention rather than being treated as one vague risk. Problem 1 is purely about MUTUAL UNDERSTANDING — a document is only safely non-binding if BOTH sides actually share that understanding, and disputes often arise precisely because one side thought they had a deal. Problem 2 connects directly back to diagram 1's formation gauntlet — remember that performance of an unexecuted agreement can itself supply proof of contract formation, so starting work "just to keep momentum going" while detailed terms are still being hammered out can accidentally create exactly the binding contract the LOI was meant to defer. Problem 3 is the subtlest and most drafting-dependent: if a letter of intent is phrased in a way that looks like it's simply ACCEPTING an already-detailed offer or bid (rather than setting up a framework for future negotiation), a court may treat it as a real acceptance despite its label, then go on to infer whatever missing terms are needed to make that contract workable — turning a document meant to AVOID commitment into the very instrument that creates it.

Even a properly formed contract can later be voided through one of six recognized doors. This diagram lines them up together to show they're independent grounds — only ONE needs to apply, and each has its own specific trigger.

Visualization Logic: Treating these as independent doors (rather than a sequential checklist) reflects how they actually function — a contract doesn't need to fail multiple grounds simultaneously, ANY single door being proven is sufficient to make the whole contract voidable. The Outcome box at the bottom carries a detail worth remembering on its own: voidable does NOT mean automatically void — the INNOCENT party (the one not in breach or not responsible for the vitiating factor) gets to CHOOSE whether to walk away or continue performing, meaning even a textbook case of mistake or misrepresentation doesn't end a contract automatically; it just hands the wronged party an option they can exercise or decline. Mistake's three-part test deserves special attention as the most mechanically precise of the six doors — MATERIAL (not trivial, like the chapter's own $100-vs-$300,000 example in a $1 million deal), MUTUAL (both parties shared the same wrong assumption, not just one), and CONTEMPORANEOUS (existing at formation, not arising afterward) — all three conditions must hold together, and a unilateral mistake (only one party was wrong) generally gets no remedy unless the OTHER party knew or should have known about the error.

Visualization Logic: The threshold diamond at the top is the single most important gate, because it eliminates the most common misconception about duress — feeling PRESSURED into a deal, even significant business pressure, is explicitly NOT enough on its own (per Pao On v. Lau Yiu). This connects directly back to the Modular Windows case from diagram 4: the owner there clearly felt pressured to pay more to avoid a delay, but the REAL legal question wasn't "did pressure exist" so much as "was there a genuine consideration problem" — duress and lack-of-consideration are related but separate angles on the same kind of fact pattern. The four factors at the bottom aren't a rigid checklist requiring all four to point the same way, they're things courts WEIGH together — but notice they all point toward the same underlying question: did the pressured party have genuine freedom to say no and pursue an alternative, or were they truly boxed in with no real options, no time to get advice, and no follow-up effort to undo the deal once the pressure was off?

Visualization Logic: The structure shows this as a one-way ratchet with a specific RESET mechanism, which is the key insight — once a consistent pattern of leniency creates reasonable reliance, the strict right doesn't just temporarily pause, it becomes genuinely UNENFORCEABLE until the enforcing party deliberately and explicitly resets it through written notice. This explains the chapter's landlord/tenant example perfectly: years of accepting rent on the 3rd doesn't just mean "this one time is fine," it means the landlord has effectively rewritten the practical deal, and going back to strict enforcement requires an affirmative announcement, not just quietly reverting one month. The construction-specific branch at the bottom (Kei-Ron, Banister, Keen) extends this same mechanism to "written changes only" clauses — a clause requiring all changes in writing offers NO protection if both parties have a track record of casually ignoring it, because the clause itself can be waived by the very same conduct-based mechanism, regardless of how clearly it was originally drafted.

9. Quantum Meruit — When Contract Law Steps Aside

Quantum meruit isn't a backup contract theory available whenever someone feels underpaid — it only activates under one specific precondition. This diagram shows that gate and the two scenarios where it actually applies.

Visualization Logic: The gate diamond at the top is the rule most often missed: quantum meruit is NOT a general fairness remedy available any time someone feels work was undervalued — it's specifically a gap-filler for situations where NO valid contract actually governs the work in question. This is why ordinary change orders, even informally documented or delayed in paperwork (as discussed in Chapter 9), don't trigger quantum meruit at all — the contract's own change order machinery already covers that situation, so quantum meruit has nothing to fill in. The three scenario branches show the genuinely different ROUTES to that gap appearing: S1 is about scope (the change is so large it falls outside what change orders were ever meant to cover), S2 is about denial (the owner won't even admit a change happened, so there's no change order process to invoke in the first place), and S3 is about validity (the original contract itself has been voided entirely through one of diagram 6's doors, leaving no contractual framework standing at all). All three converge on the same remedy: pay for the actual value delivered, including a reasonable profit margin, which is what "quantum meruit" literally means.
