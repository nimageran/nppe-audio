7 Processes for Choosing Project Participants7 items
▾
i
Request for Qualifications (RFQ)— selection based solely on qualifications, not price; used for professionals, design-builders, alliance/P3 participants
ii
Invitation to Tender (Call for Tenders)— formal; clearly defined scope; lowest price wins without negotiation; strict rules apply
iii
Request for Quotation— informal; no formal closing time; used when scope not fully defined; negotiation may follow
iv
Request for Standing Offers— prearranges prices/terms for frequently ordered goods/services; activated by "call-up" or requisition
v
Request for Proposals (RFP)— invite proposed solutions; success based on quality of solution, not just price; buyer expects to negotiate with top proponent(s)
vi
Prequalification / Letter of Interest— screening before tender/RFP; limits field to qualified sellers only; spares underqualified sellers from wasted bidding effort
vii
Hybrid Methods— buyer-defined unique process; must follow its own terms once established; government contracts must still be fair

Contract A vs Contract B — 2 Contracts in Tendering2 items · from Ron Engineering
▾
A
Contract A — Bidding Contract— formed the moment a compliant bid is submitted; one Contract A per compliant bidder; contains implied terms (bid irrevocable, parties obligated to enter Contract B on acceptance, buyer must normally accept lowest tender)
B
Contract B — Construction/Project Contract— only one formed; between buyer and the SUCCESSFUL bidder; created upon acceptance of the bid
MJB modification: Contract A does NOT automatically arise on every bid. Depends on terms of the invitation. Buyer has 2 options: (1) define the relationship parameters, or (2) explicitly state no Contract A exists — though courts may ignore this.

6 Stages of the Bidding Process6 items
▾
1
Preparation of bid documents— drawings, specifications, general conditions, special conditions, instructions to bidders, invitation to bidders
2
Solicitation / invitation of bids— by invitation only, open invitation, or through a bid depository
3
Pricing by potential sellers
4
Submission of bids— must comply with timing, form, and content; late bids typically must be rejected
5
Evaluation by the buyer— with consultant assistance; only permitted alternatives considered; no post-tender negotiation unless defined in process
6
Award of the contract— constitutes acceptance of an offer; must follow criteria stated in tender documents

6 Rules for Avoiding Liability at Contract Award6 items
▾
1
Treat all bidders fairly, in good faith, consistent with instructions to bidders
2
Reject non-compliant bids; minimize mandatory requirements to limit rejections
3
Do not ask bidders to clarify ambiguities after tenders close— except in exceptional circumstances
4
No negotiations before award unless clearly defined in the bid process— never share one bidder's info with another
5
Make contract changes only after award and write them into the contract
6
Award in accordance with criteria stated in the tender documents— price is often most important but not the only criterion

Bid Mistakes — 2 Requirements to Withdraw Without Penalty2 items
▾
1
Error must be Obvious— apparent on the face of the bid
2
Error must be Material— significant enough to affect the substance of the bid
Risk: A bidder who revokes an irrevocable bid after closing but before acceptance forfeits the bid bond/deposit — unless the error is both obvious AND material. Courts are reluctant to let bidders withdraw.

3 Key International / Interprovincial Trade Agreements3 items
▾
1
NAFTA— North American Free Trade Agreement; 2014 threshold for services: $80,400
2
WTO Agreement on Government Procurement— World Trade Organization rules for government purchasing
3
AIT — Agreement on Internal Trade— interprovincial; threshold for services: $100,000. Only important interprovincial agreement.
Complaints about government procurement fairness go to the Canadian International Trade Tribunal (CITT). Filing deadline: 10 days from when the supplier knew or ought to have known about the breach.

Bid Shopping — 3 Legal Theories a Plaintiff Must Use3 items
▾
1
Breach of Contract— must show Contract A was created AND that non-shopping was an implied term of Contract A
2
Promissory Estoppel— prime contractor relied on subcontractor's price (detrimental reliance)
3
Unjust Enrichment— buyer or prime contractor gained benefit from the shopped price without proper compensation
Key point: Bid shopping is unethical but not automatically illegal — no remedy exists in many jurisdictions. Bidders who bid shop may lose the right to hold suppliers to their quoted price.
