Concurrent Liability
Click to flip
The Story: You sign a contract with a client, and then you negligently design a bad foundation.

The Rule: The client can sue you for Breach of Contract AND for Tort (Negligence) at the exact same time. They will use whichever area of law gives them the most money or the most time to sue.
Sued Twice for One Mistake
Vicarious Liability
Click to flip
The Story: As a junior engineer, you make a terrible calculation error that causes a collapse.

The Rule: The victim sues your company, not just you. Employers are held strictly, financially liable for any torts committed by their employees while on the job.
The Boss Pays for Your Sins
Joint and Several Liability
(In Tort)
Click to flip
The Trap: You (10% at fault) and the contractor (90% at fault) cause $1 Million in damage. The contractor goes completely bankrupt.

The Rule: The victim can legally force YOU to pay the entire $1 Million. You do not just pay your 10% share if the other guy is broke.
Deep Pockets Pay It All
Limitation Periods
Click to flip
The Basics: The strict legal ticking clock for lawsuits.

The Rule: If a plaintiff waits too long to file a lawsuit after being wronged, the limitation period expires and their right to sue is permanently, legally dead. (Often 2 years in most provinces).
Sue Fast or Lose the Right
The Discoverability Principle
Click to flip
The Story: You make a mistake in 2020. The pipe is buried, and it does not burst and cause damage until 2024.

The Rule: The 2-year limitation clock does NOT start when you made the mistake. It starts the exact day the victim actually *discovers* the damage (in 2024).
The Clock Starts at Discovery
Ultimate Limitation Period
Click to flip
The Shield: To prevent engineers from being sued 40 years into retirement, the law sets a hard deadline (often 15 years from the day the work was finished).

The Rule: Once this ultimate clock runs out, you cannot be sued, even if the victim discovers the damage the next day.
The Absolute Final Deadline
Contributory Negligence
Click to flip
The Story: You design a floor too steep (your fault), but the victim runs across it in socks while drunk and falls (their fault).

The Rule: If the plaintiff's own stupidity contributed to the injury, the judge will split the blame and reduce the amount of money you have to pay them.
Shared Blame = Reduced Payout
Codes and Standards
(As a Defense)
Click to flip
The Trap: You get sued in tort, but you followed the Building Code exactly.

The Rule: Following the code is excellent evidence you were not negligent, BUT it is not an absolute shield. If you knew the standard was outdated or dangerous, you must exceed the code to protect the public.
Code is Minimum, Not Maximum
Several Liability
(The Exception)
Click to flip
The Basics: Some provinces (like BC) or specific contracts replace "Joint and Several" liability with just "Several" (Proportionate) liability.

The Rule: Under "Several" liability, if you are 10% at fault, you ONLY pay 10% of the damages, period. If the other guy goes bankrupt, the victim eats the loss, not you.
Several = Pay Your Share Only
