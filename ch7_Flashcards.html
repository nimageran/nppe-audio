Breach of Contract
Click to flip
The Basics: Failing to do exactly what you promised in the contract.

The Rule: Liability in contract is strict. It does not matter if you tried your best or if it was an accident. If you failed to deliver, you breached.
No Excuses, Just Delivery
The Goal of Damages
Click to flip
The Goal: When someone breaches, the court forces them to pay money ("damages").

The Rule: The money must put the innocent party in the exact financial position they would be in if the contract had been performed perfectly.
Fix the Math, Not the Feelings
Duty to Mitigate
Click to flip
The Story: A contractor abandons the job, leaving a hole in your roof. It rains. You let the rain destroy your house for a month.

The Rule: You cannot sue for the water damage. You have a legal duty to "mitigate" (minimize) your own losses by throwing a tarp over it.
Stop the Bleeding
Remoteness
(Hadley v. Baxendale)
Click to flip
The Rule: You are only responsible for paying damages that were reasonably foreseeable at the time the contract was signed.

The Catch: You are not liable for a bizarre, unpredictable chain reaction of losses that you could never have guessed.
Predictable Pain Only
Repudiation
(Anticipatory Breach)
Click to flip
The Story: The contractor calls you on Tuesday and says "I am not showing up on Friday."

The Rule: You do not have to wait until Friday to sue. You can accept the breach immediately, fire them, and hire a replacement.
Quitting Early = Sued Early
Fundamental Breach
Click to flip
The Concept: A breach so massive that it destroys the entire purpose of the contract.

The Rule: It gives the innocent party the right to instantly cancel the entire contract and sue, rather than just asking for a small discount for a minor defect.
Destroy the Root, Kill the Contract
Liquidated Damages
Click to flip
The Rule: A clause where both parties agree in advance what the penalty will be if someone is late (e.g., "$1,000 per day").

The Catch: The court will only enforce this if it is a genuine, realistic estimate of the actual money lost by the delay.
Pre-Agreed Estimates are Legal
Penalty Clauses
Click to flip
The Story: The contract says "If you are one day late, you owe $10 Million."

The Rule: Courts will absolutely NOT enforce this. If a number is an arbitrary, massive amount designed purely to punish or scare someone, it is an illegal penalty.
Punishments are Not Legal
Specific Performance
Click to flip
The Concept: Sometimes money is not good enough to fix a breach.

The Rule: A court order forcing the breaching party to actually do the physical work they promised. Courts rarely use this, except for completely unique things like selling a specific piece of land.
Do What You Promised
Injunction
Click to flip
The Concept: The opposite of Specific Performance.

The Rule: A strict court order forcing someone to STOP doing something. (e.g., Stopping an ex-employee from sharing your trade secrets with a competitor).
The Legal Stop Sign
Quantum Meruit
Click to flip
The Story: You did the work, but the contract was accidentally voided or missing a price.

The Rule: Latin for "as much as is deserved." The court will force the client to pay you fair market value for the work you did. You do not work for free just because paperwork failed.
Pay for What You Got
Force Majeure
Click to flip
The Rule: A contract clause that forgives a breach if an unforeseeable "Act of God" (hurricane, war) makes the job physically impossible.

The Catch: It does NOT protect you if the job simply became more difficult or lost you money. It must be impossible.
Impossible, Not Just Expensive
