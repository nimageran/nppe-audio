CHAPTER 21: DEEP EMPLOYMENT LAW VISUALIZATION

1. The Implied Contractual Duties

Even if an employment contract is entirely oral, the common law automatically writes several strict obligations into the relationship[cite: 1].

Duty of Notice / Severance] D1 -.->|Loyalty & Confidentiality| Rule1(Cannot use trade secrets to compete) D2 -.->|Professional Standard| Rule2(Employer is vicariously liable for mistakes) D3 -.->|Employer Obligation| Rule3(Must provide reasonable notice to terminate) style Contract fill:#1e293b,color:#fff,stroke:none style D1 fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0284c7,stroke-width:2px style D2 fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0284c7,stroke-width:2px style D3 fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0284c7,stroke-width:2px style Rule1 fill:#f8fafc,stroke:#94a3b8 style Rule2 fill:#f8fafc,stroke:#94a3b8 style Rule3 fill:#f8fafc,stroke:#94a3b8

Visualization Logic: This maps the three primary implied duties of employment[cite: 1].

  • Duty of Fidelity (Loyalty): Employees are agents of the employer and must remain loyal[cite: 1]. This means they cannot use or disclose confidential information (trade secrets) to compete against a former employer, even after they quit[cite: 1]. However, an employee is completely free to use their general skills and experience at a new job[cite: 1].
  • Duty of Competence: Employees are expected to be competent[cite: 1]. If an employee makes a mistake causing a loss to a third party, the employer is usually sued under the doctrine of vicarious liability[cite: 1]. Employers rarely sue their own employees for mistakes because insurance usually covers both, and doing so would destroy their ability to hire future workers[cite: 1].
  • Notice of Termination: Unless the employee commits a fundamental breach (just cause), the employer has an implied duty to provide adequate notice or severance pay[cite: 1].

2. The Restrictive Covenant Gauntlet

Employers often try to prevent departing employees from competing against them by using "Restrictive Covenants" (non-compete clauses)[cite: 1]. Courts hate these clauses because they restrict a person's ability to earn a living[cite: 1].

Visualization Logic: This illustrates the legal tests a non-compete clause must pass to survive court scrutiny[cite: 1].

  • The Clarity Test: In the Shafron case, a lawyer drafted a geographic limit of "Metropolitan City of Vancouver"[cite: 1]. Because this term has no legal definition, the Supreme Court ruled it ambiguous, instantly destroying the entire covenant[cite: 1].
  • The Reasonableness Test: In the Terra Engineering case, an engineer was banned from working within 150 km of multiple cities, effectively banning him from practicing in BC[cite: 1]. The court struck it down as overly broad and contrary to the public interest (restraint of trade)[cite: 1].
  • The Rule: There is no "bright line" formula, but restricting a senior engineer for 1 year in a single city is far more likely to be enforced than restricting them for 15 years across a whole province[cite: 1].

3. The Termination & Dismissal Matrix

Unlike unionized environments, there is no such thing as a "layoff" in common law[cite: 1]. An employer terminating an employee must navigate a complex web of legal causes and notice periods[cite: 1].

Constructive Dismissal] Cause -.->|Theft, Dishonesty, Gross Incompetence| F1(Fired Immediately: ZERO Notice Required) NoCause -.->|Restructuring, Lack of Work| F2(Reasonable Notice OR Severance Required) Constructive -.->|Employer slashes salary by 10%| F3(Employee quits & sues for Severance) F2 --> Mitigate{Duty to Mitigate} F3 --> Mitigate Mitigate -->|Finds equivalent job in 1 week| Cut[Employer liability capped at 1 week] style Term fill:#1e293b,color:#fff style Cause fill:#fee2e2,stroke:#dc2626 style NoCause fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0284c7 style Constructive fill:#fef3c7,stroke:#d97706 style F1 fill:#f8fafc,stroke:#94a3b8 style F2 fill:#f8fafc,stroke:#94a3b8 style F3 fill:#f8fafc,stroke:#94a3b8 style Mitigate fill:#10b981,color:#fff style Cut fill:#dcfce7,stroke:#16a34a

Visualization Logic: This outlines the three ways an employment relationship ruptures[cite: 1].

  • Just Cause: A fundamental breach by the employee (e.g., sleeping on the job and lying about it, as in Richardson v. Davis Wire Industries)[cite: 1]. Minor absences only become "Just Cause" if repeated warnings are given[cite: 1]. Lack of business is never just cause[cite: 1].
  • Without Cause & Reasonable Notice: An employer can fire anyone without cause, but they must provide reasonable notice (or pay in lieu)[cite: 1]. Notice length depends on age, tenure, and position[cite: 1]. (e.g., A 20-year executive gets 18-24 months; a 1-year junior gets 1 month)[cite: 1].
  • Constructive Dismissal: If an employer unilaterally changes a contract to the employee's detriment (e.g., cutting salary), the employee can refuse, consider themselves fired, and sue for full severance[cite: 1].
  • Duty to Mitigate: A fired employee cannot just sit on the couch[cite: 1]. They must actively look for suitable alternate work[cite: 1]. If they find a new job quickly, the original employer's liability drops drastically[cite: 1].
  • Bad Faith (The Wallace Case): Employers must act fairly during dismissal[cite: 1]. If they terminate someone with bad faith or cause extreme personal damage, courts will punish the employer by increasing the required notice period[cite: 1].

4. The Worker Classification Spectrum (The Control Test)

Properly categorizing a worker dictates massive liabilities for taxes, insurance, holdbacks, and human rights[cite: 1]. You cannot just sign a paper calling someone an "Independent Contractor" and expect the government to believe you[cite: 1].

Visualization Logic: This shows the factors courts weigh to determine true employment status[cite: 1].

  • The Core Factors: The courts look at the level of control over means and methods, who provides the equipment, whether the worker hires their own helpers, the degree of financial risk, and the opportunity for profit[cite: 1].
  • The Consequences: If classified as an employee, the employer must withhold taxes, pay vicarious liability for errors, and follow statutory notice periods[cite: 1]. If classified as an independent contractor, the worker can deduct expenses, but the owner must legally deduct builders' lien holdbacks from their pay[cite: 1].

5. The Triad of Employee Remedies

When an employee is wrongfully terminated or abused, they have three distinct legal arenas to seek justice[cite: 1]. Each arena has completely different costs and rewards[cite: 1].

Human Rights Tribunal] R1 -.->|Expensive & High Damages| C1(No Reinstatement) R2 -.->|Free to enforce, very low payout limits| C2(No Reinstatement) R3 -.->|Must prove prohibited discrimination| C3(Can Order Physical Reinstatement) style Wrong fill:#1e293b,color:#fff style R1 fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0284c7 style R2 fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0284c7 style R3 fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0284c7 style C1 fill:#f8fafc,stroke:#94a3b8 style C2 fill:#f8fafc,stroke:#94a3b8 style C3 fill:#f8fafc,stroke:#94a3b8

Visualization Logic: This translates Table 21-1 into an actionable framework[cite: 1].

  • Common Law: Very expensive because you must hire a lawyer to sue, but it yields the highest financial damages (e.g., 6 months' pay)[cite: 1]. A judge cannot force the employer to give you your job back[cite: 1].
  • Employment Standards Legislation: Every province has minimum standards (wage, hours, notice)[cite: 1]. It is free to enforce because the government pursues the employer, but the statutory payouts are tiny (e.g., 3 weeks' pay instead of 6 months)[cite: 1]. Note: Many provinces explicitly exclude professionals like engineers from these protections[cite: 1].
  • Human Rights Legislation: Protects against prohibited discrimination (religion, race, disability, gender)[cite: 1]. Crucially, it does not protect against discrimination based on education or experience[cite: 1]. If a disability is involved, the employer must provide "reasonable accommodation" unless physical ability is a genuine job requirement[cite: 1]. The tribunal is cheap to access and has the extreme power to order the employer to reinstate the worker[cite: 1].
  • Note on The Charter: The Charter of Rights and Freedoms only applies to government actions, not private companies[cite: 1]. Therefore, a private company having a mandatory retirement policy does not violate the Charter[cite: 1].
