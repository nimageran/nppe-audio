The R4 box deserves attention because it corrects a common assumption: people intuitively think "owning land in fee simple" means owning everything below it too, but Canadian law specifically carves mines, minerals, oil, and gas OUT of the standard fee simple bundle, reserving most of those rights to government, which licenses them out separately.

Visualization Logic: The TWO-factor structure (not one) is the entire point of using the mobile home as the worked example — the chapter deliberately picks an item that's PHYSICALLY bolted down (satisfying a naive "is it attached" test) specifically to show that physical attachment ALONE doesn't resolve the question. You need BOTH factors: how permanent is the attachment, AND what was the underlying PURPOSE of attaching it. A mobile home bolted down just for stability while it's still essentially intended to remain movable stays personal property, but the same bolting-down, done with genuine permanence and intent to make the structure part of the land, flips it into real property — same physical act, different legal outcome, depending entirely on intent and permanence. The "Reversible" branch at the bottom captures a separate, important point using the chapter's nail example: this classification isn't a one-time, permanent label assigned at the moment of attachment — it tracks the CURRENT physical and intentional status of the item, meaning something can genuinely flip back and forth between chattel and fixture over its lifetime as it's installed and later removed.

Visualization Logic: Running the SAME fact pattern (Joe and Mary, Joe dies) through both branches side by side is what makes the practical stakes of this distinction unmistakable — under joint tenancy, Mary ends up owning the ENTIRE property outright and Joe's own family inherits nothing FROM THIS PROPERTY, while under tenancy in common, Joe's heirs step directly into his share and Mary is left co-owning the property with people who may be complete strangers to her. This is exactly why the distinction matters enormously for estate planning: joint tenancy is often chosen deliberately by couples specifically because they WANT automatic survivorship (no probate delay, no risk of the deceased's share going to unintended parties), while tenancy in common is often chosen by business partners or unrelated co-investors who specifically want their own share to pass to THEIR OWN heirs rather than automatically enriching their co-owner. The chapter's framing that joint tenancy is the law's DEFAULT assumption is also worth remembering — parties who actually want tenancy in common generally need to say so explicitly, or risk the default joint-tenancy rule applying without their full understanding of its survivorship consequences.

Visualization Logic: The top diamond is the single organizing question this entire diagram exists to answer, because it's the variable that determines whether buying a piece of property is a clean transaction or one where you inherit obligations you never personally negotiated. The restrictive covenant split deserves special attention as the chapter's subtlest distinction: most people assume "restrictive covenant" is a single category, but the chapter draws a sharp line between NEGATIVE covenants (a promise to refrain from something, like not building a structure) which DO run with the land, and POSITIVE covenants (a promise to actively DO something, like maintaining a shared fence) which generally do NOT run with the land. This asymmetry has a practical real-estate consequence worth remembering: a buyer doing due diligence needs to check not just WHETHER a restrictive covenant exists on title, but WHICH TYPE it is, since a positive covenant that looked binding on paper might not actually bind them as the new owner at all.

5. Torrens vs Registry System

Canada doesn't use one uniform land registration system — it uses two structurally different ones, and the practical task of "find out what interests exist in this property" is dramatically easier under one than the other.

Visualization Logic: The structural difference between these two systems isn't cosmetic — it changes the actual WORK required to determine what interests exist in a property, which is exactly why the diagram traces each system through to its practical SEARCH experience rather than stopping at the definitional level. Under torrens, the certificate of title functions like a single, continuously-updated snapshot — everything relevant to that specific property lives in one place, and a buyer's lawyer can rely on that single document being accurate and current. Under a registry system, there's no equivalent single snapshot — the registry is fundamentally CHRONOLOGICAL (organized by when documents were filed, not by which property they relate to), meaning determining current title status requires actively reconstructing the property's history from potentially many separate historical entries. This explains why due diligence costs and complexity genuinely differ by jurisdiction in Canada — it's not just a regional quirk, it reflects a real difference in how much investigative work a title search actually requires.

Visualization Logic: Rather than treating these six interests as an alphabetical list to memorize independently, arranging them on a spectrum reveals genuine FAMILY RELATIONSHIPS between them, which is a much more durable way to retain the distinctions. A lease and an easement both grant rights to USE property, but a lease is exclusive occupation while an easement is typically just a right of passage or limited use — a meaningfully narrower right. A restrictive covenant is conceptually different from an easement because it can restrict almost ANYTHING (paint colour, building height, business types) rather than just granting a specific use right, which is exactly why the chapter flags covenant conditions as potentially "very broad." The profit à prendre sitting at the end as a deliberate HYBRID is the perfect capstone for this spectrum — it explicitly borrows the land-binding permanence of an easement (running with the land) while ALSO carrying the specific-activity flavor of a licence (harvesting trees, removing gravel), making it the conceptual bridge connecting the two ends of this spectrum together in a single right.

Visualization Logic: This tree exists to answer the chapter's own "best mousetrap" question completely, by routing the same invention through MULTIPLE applicable categories rather than assuming only one IP regime could ever apply to a single creation. The TradeSecret box at the very end captures the chapter's foundational principle stated at the top of section 4.3: IP law protects EXPRESSIONS of ideas, never bare ideas themselves — so anything that doesn't fit cleanly into one of the five named categories (copyright, patent, trademark, industrial design, integrated circuit topography) falls back to the much weaker, contract/confidentiality-based protection of trade secrets, which only works as long as the secret actually stays secret.

Visualization Logic: Lining these up side by side surfaces two distinctions easy to lose when each term is studied in isolation. First, the STARTING POINT varies meaningfully across types — copyright's clock doesn't start at creation or publication, it starts at the AUTHOR'S DEATH (making its effective duration highly variable and often far longer than 50 years from the work's creation), while patents and integrated circuit protection both start from the APPLICATION FILING DATE, and industrial design starts from REGISTRATION — three genuinely different trigger events that are easy to confuse under exam pressure. Second, and more importantly, trademark is structurally UNIQUE on this list — it's the only IP type without an absolute, hard ceiling, since it can be renewed indefinitely every 15 years forever, but that apparent advantage comes with a real string attached: unlike the other four types (which simply expire passively after their fixed term regardless of what the owner does), trademark protection can be LOST EARLY through inaction — failure to actively use or protect/police the mark against infringement destroys the right even within an otherwise-valid renewal period, making trademark simultaneously the most potentially permanent AND the most actively demanding of the five protections to maintain.
