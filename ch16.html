Visualization Logic: Both halves of this diagram converge on the same lesson from two different angles — there is no reliable shortcut around carrying real insurance. The timing diamond is the single highest-yield fact in this section: the EXACT same act (transferring assets to a spouse) is either completely legal or completely reversible fraud depending entirely on whether the person already knew a claim was coming when they did it. The corporate veil branch adds a second, independent trap — even if the corporate shell itself survives a piercing attempt, that protects the COMPANY's assets, not the individual professional, who remains personally exposed to a negligence claim regardless of their corporate role. Two separate doors into liability, and incorporating only closes one of them.

2. Duty to Defend vs Duty to Indemnify

These two duties are usually bundled together, which is exactly why it's easy to miss that they're legally SEPARATE obligations that can split apart. This diagram shows the moment they diverge.

Visualization Logic: The key insight this diagram isolates is that the duty to defend is triggered by the ALLEGATION, not by the eventual finding — the insurer has to defend based on what the claim SAYS, even if it later turns out the insured isn't liable, or is liable for something the policy doesn't cover. This is exactly why "DefendOnly" is a real, common outcome and not an edge case: the chapter explicitly notes the duty to defend can be MORE valuable than the duty to indemnify when a claim is weak, because defence costs alone (lawyers, experts, discovery) can be enormous even for a claim that ultimately goes nowhere. It also explains the strategic note buried in the text: a sharp plaintiff's lawyer will deliberately frame a claim to touch on covered conduct, specifically to drag the insurer into the defend box even if the core allegation (like fraud) wouldn't otherwise qualify.

Visualization Logic: Follow the chain top to bottom and you can see subrogation is really just the insurer recovering what it already paid out, by temporarily "becoming" the party it insured for the purpose of a NEW lawsuit against whoever actually caused the loss. The two diamonds at the bottom are the two ways this otherwise-automatic right gets cut off, and they're conceptually different: a waiver of subrogation clause is a CONTRACTUAL choice the parties made (and one professionals should watch for, since agreeing to it can unintentionally let the insurer walk away from its indemnity obligation); the "same insured" block is a fundamental LEGAL RULE that can't be waived around — confirmed in Commonwealth v. Imperial Oil, where the court refused to let an insurer pay one insured and then turn around and sue another insured under the SAME policy, because that would make the policy's protection illusory. This is also exactly why naming all project participants under one project-specific policy (a wrap-up policy, covered in diagram 9's neighbourhood) is such an effective way to eliminate subrogation fights between contractors and subs.

4. Insurable Interest — Why a Subcontractor Insures the Whole Project

It seems counterintuitive that a subcontractor doing a small fraction of a project's total work could have an insurable interest in the ENTIRE project. This diagram walks through the Commonwealth v. Imperial Oil reasoning that makes this make sense.

Visualization Logic: The diagram is built around a single rejected hypothesis to make the actual rule memorable — courts COULD have limited insurable interest to "your own work only," but explicitly chose not to, because doing so would have left massive gaps in project insurance every time one trade's mistake damaged another trade's work. The deeper logic in the "Why" box is the real exam-relevant principle: insurable interest isn't really about OWNERSHIP, it's about whether you'd be PREJUDICED by a loss — and a subcontractor working inside a complex structure is obviously prejudiced if that structure burns down or collapses, regardless of how small their own contract value is. The bottom box also quietly connects back to diagram 3 — this is precisely the mechanism (everyone sharing the same project policy) that makes the "same insured" subrogation block in diagram 3 so commonly applicable in construction.

5. Claims-Made vs Occurrence — The Timing Puzzle

This is the chapter's single most testable mechanical concept. Using the chapter's own geoscientist example (Ace → Deuce), this diagram plots BOTH the work date and the claim date on a timeline to show exactly which policy ends up on the hook — and why occurrence-style thinking gets the wrong answer.

Visualization Logic: The whole point of laying this out as a timeline rather than a definition is to make the counterintuitive result impossible to miss: the policy that was actually in force when the NEGLIGENT WORK happened (ABC, 1995) ends up providing ZERO coverage, while a policy that didn't even exist yet at the time of the work (DEF, which the geoscientist wasn't insured under until 2001) is the one that pays. This only makes sense once you fully internalize that "claims-made" tracks the CLAIM DATE, not the WORK DATE — which is the opposite of how an occurrence policy would behave. This single mechanic explains why retiring professionals are told to keep paying for "tail" coverage after they stop practicing (diagram 6 picks this thread up directly) — without it, work done decades ago can become completely uninsured the moment the original policy lapses, regardless of how good the work actually was.

6. The Notice Trap When Changing Insurers

Claims-made coverage creates a notice trap that's easy to fall into completely by accident — simply by changing jobs, changing insurers, or retiring without taking one specific extra step. This diagram shows the trap and the single action that avoids it.

Visualization Logic: All three triggers create the exact same underlying danger — a gap opens up where past work exists but no current policy will cover a future claim about it — even though each trigger looks like a routine, unremarkable life event. The Reid Crowther v. Simcoe & Erie case (not shown as its own box, but the reasoning behind "Fix") sharpens this further: you don't need the words "I am suing you" to trigger a notice obligation — if a REASONABLE professional in your position would have suspected a claim might be coming (e.g. a client expressing quality concerns), you're expected to report that as a potential claim, not wait for a formal demand letter. Reid Crowther actually won that case on unusual facts, but the chapter is explicit that this is the exception, not something to rely on — the safe move is always to over-disclose rather than wait and hope a brewing problem quietly goes away.

7. Material Non-Disclosure — Even Unrelated Facts Can Void Coverage

This diagram targets a result that feels deeply unfair at first: an insurer can deny an UNRELATED claim because of a non-disclosure that had nothing to do with that specific claim. Walking through why exposes the actual logic insurers rely on.

Visualization Logic: The surprising result sits entirely in the WhyYes box, and it's worth sitting with: the test for material non-disclosure is not "did this specific fact cause this specific claim" — it's "would the insurer have priced or accepted the risk DIFFERENTLY had it known everything." Once you frame it that way, the result stops feeling arbitrary: the insurer was deprived of the chance to evaluate the firm's TRUE overall risk profile (a firm with one prior claim is statistically a different risk than one with none), and the duty of utmost good faith means the insured has to volunteer that picture even where it seems unrelated to whatever eventually goes wrong. Compare this against chapter question 7 (the "foreign citizen" non-disclosure) for contrast — THAT non-disclosure was found immaterial because immigration status has no plausible bearing on theft/fire risk, while a prior negligence claim plausibly does bear on future E&O risk. The test is always "could this fact have changed the insurer's pricing or acceptance decision," not "is this fact embarrassing or unrelated-sounding."

Visualization Logic: The five conflict triggers all share one underlying mechanism worth memorizing as a single sentence: whenever the INSURER's incentive (resolve this as cheaply as possible) might diverge from the INSURED's incentive (protect reputation, avoid personal exposure, preserve a fee claim, or avoid a result that bleeds into uninsured territory), that's the signal to get independent advice — sometimes paid for by the insurer, sometimes not, but valuable either way. The policy-limits branch gets extra visual weight because it's the one situation where the insured's OWN MONEY (not just the insurer's) is genuinely on the line — a $1M policy facing a $2M claim means the insured is personally exposed for the gap, which flips the insured's incentive to settle quickly even if the insurer (who's only on the hook for $1M regardless of outcome) might be willing to gamble on a trial. The bad-faith branch at the bottom is the insured's actual leverage in that exact scenario — formally requesting a within-limits settlement creates a paper trail that can support a bad-faith claim if the insurer refuses and then loses, exposing the insured to the excess.

9. The Faulty-Design Exclusion + Resultant Damage Carve-Out

This is the most layered mechanic in the chapter, built from the wall-collapse and CNR tunnel cases. A builders' risk claim isn't a single yes/no — it runs through a chain of tests, and the final answer can split a SINGLE incident into covered and uncovered pieces simultaneously.

Visualization Logic: This chain deliberately has TWO separate diamonds that each independently decide the outcome, and missing either one leads to the wrong answer. The first diamond (state-of-the-art) decides whether "faulty design" even applies in the first place — CNR's tunnelling machine failure was NOT automatically "faulty" just because it failed; the test asks whether it met the engineering knowledge available AT THE TIME, which is a deliberately HIGHER bar than ordinary negligence but still short of demanding perfection or omniscience. The second diamond only matters once you've already concluded the work WAS faulty — and it asks the completely different question of whether damage that spread to other property counts as genuinely separate "resultant damage" or whether courts will treat the whole project as one integrated obligation (the trend the chapter flags: courts increasingly say the latter, narrowing the resultant-damage exception over time). The practical exam lesson buried in this chain: a single incident — like the chapter's own switchgear/sports car example — can split into covered and uncovered pieces simultaneously (the car covered under a separate LIABILITY policy entirely, the wall excluded under the PROPERTY policy, the switchgear genuinely uncertain) — you have to run EACH damaged item through this chain separately rather than assuming one verdict covers the whole loss.
