Contra Proferentem
Click to flip
The Story: You drafted a sloppy contract clause that could be interpreted two completely different ways. You end up in court.

The Rule: The judge will automatically choose the meaning that goes against the person who wrote it.
Ambiguity Hurts the Drafter
The Parol Evidence Rule
Click to flip
The Story: You sign a final contract. Later, you claim, "But on the phone, you promised to do it for cheaper!"

The Rule: The court will not listen. You cannot use outside verbal evidence (parol evidence) to contradict a clear, written contract.
Ink Beats Talk
Express vs. Implied Terms
Click to flip
Express: Words physically written down in the document.

Implied: Unwritten rules the court assumes exist (like acting in good faith or doing professional-level work).

The Rule: Implied terms can NEVER contradict Express terms.
Written Beats Assumed
Condition Precedent
Click to flip
The Story: A contract says, "I will buy this land IF the city approves the rezoning."

The Rule: It is a strict trigger. If the city says no, the contract never comes into effect. Nobody breached; the deal just legally dies.

The Golden Rule
(Plain Meaning Rule)
Click to flip
The Rule: Courts read the words in a contract using their normal, everyday dictionary meaning.

The Exception: The judge will only look for hidden or technical meanings if reading it normally creates a completely absurd or impossible result.
Read it Like a Normal Person
Custom and Trade Usage
Click to flip
The Story: A contract calls for a "2x4" piece of lumber. It measures 1.5 x 3.5 inches.

The Rule: The court will accept the smaller size because it is a universally known industry standard. The trade custom overrides the literal dictionary meaning.
Industry Slang Counts
Order of Precedence Clause
Click to flip
The Trap: The engineering drawings show one thing, but the written specs demand something else. Which wins?

The Rule: Contracts use this specific clause to rank documents. Typically, specific written specs outrank general drawings.
The Tie-Breaker List
Drafting: Consistency is King
Click to flip
The Trap: You use "Engineer," "Consultant," and "Designer" interchangeably in a contract so it doesn't sound repetitive.

The Rule: If you use three different words, the court will legally assume you meant three completely different entities. Pick one word and stick to it.
Boring is Legally Safe
Ambiguity vs. Vagueness
Click to flip
Ambiguous: A clause that can be read two totally different ways. (Courts hate this and use Contra Proferentem).

Vague: A broad, undefined concept, like "do the work in a reasonable time." (Courts allow this, but it leaves the exact timing up to the judge).
Vague is Broad | Ambiguous is Broken
Strict Interpretation of
Exclusion Clauses
Click to flip
The Story: You write a clause to escape liability if the building leaks.

The Rule: Courts despise exclusion clauses. They will read it as narrowly as physically possible. If there is even a tiny loophole in how you wrote the escape hatch, the court will use it against you.
