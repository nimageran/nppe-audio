▾
1
Disclosure of all MATERIAL CHANGES— an ongoing process (NI 51-102 — Continuous Disclosure Obligations)
2
Filing audited and unaudited financial statements
3
Press releases and insider trading reports
Disclosure does NOT end with the prospectus — it is a continuing obligation for the life of the public company.

Key National Instruments (NI) for Engineers/Geoscientists3 items
▾
NI 43-101
Standards of Disclosure for Mineral Projects— developed largely in response to mining disclosure scandals (notably BRE-X); has companion policy 43-101CP. Additional applicable standards: CIM Best Practices Guidelines, CIM Standards on Mineral Resources/Reserves, CIM Diamond Exploration Reporting Guidelines, GSC Paper 88-21 (coal).
NI 51-101
Standards of Disclosure for Oil and Gas Activities
NI 51-102
Continuous Disclosure Obligations— governs ongoing reporting of material changes
CSA = Canadian Securities Administrators — a forum of provincial securities commissions that creates National Instruments for cross-provincial consistency. Provinces CAN choose not to adopt them, but they still represent a common MINIMUM STANDARD OF PRACTICE. Always confirm you're using the LATEST version — these documents change.

The "Qualified Person" — Qualifications & Duties2+4 items
▾
Qualifications (e.g. mineral projects)
1
Geoscientist or engineer with ≥5 years' RELEVANT experience— relevant to the subject matter of the project AND the technical report
2
Member in good standing with a professional association
Required Duties
1
Personally inspect the property
2
Sign and seal the technical report
3
Sign a consent document— stipulating the technical disclosure contains NO misrepresentations
4
Act independently and follow CSA guidelines
Who chooses the QP? Responsibility for SELECTING the qualified person and for PROPER USE of their information rests with the company's OFFICERS AND DIRECTORS. The QP should also review the prospectus/other disclosure to confirm their information was properly interpreted.

Liability for Technical Reports — Key Points4 items
▾
1
Misrepresentation in a report, consent letter, or prospectus— creates a right of action for the company and its investors
2
Negligent preparation (e.g. not following NI 43-101/51-101)— also creates a right of action. The NIs themselves don't CREATE a cause of action, but as MINIMUM PRACTICE STANDARDS they become highly relevant in court.
3
DISCLAIMER CLAUSES are UNLIKELY to succeed— in this context, disclaimers have little chance of limiting liability
4
Statutory liability (use in a prospectus or takeover attempt) CANNOT be disclaimed— separate cause of action under securities legislation; direct officer/director liability also possible (Ch.5)
7
Insider TradingCriminal Code s.382.1
▾
1
"Insider" definition— a shareholder, professional advisor, director, or employee who POSSESSES inside knowledge AND uses it to (directly or indirectly) buy or sell a security
2
Any professional providing advice can be considered an "insider"— engineers, geoscientists, and architects must be careful not to violate the prohibition
Practical scenario: A geoscientist learning of extremely positive (not-yet-public) test results CANNOT discuss them with anyone outside the confidentiality obligation (e.g. friends/family), and CANNOT have associates trade on it. Even AFTER public release, she must discuss ONLY what was actually disclosed publicly — adding extra color, tone ("it's REALLY good"), or non-verbal hints ("wink and a nudge") about non-disclosed details remains illegal.
