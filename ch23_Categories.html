3 Phases of an Environmental Site Assessment (ESA)3 items · CSA Z768
▾
I
Phase I — Information Gathering— determines LIKELIHOOD of contamination; little/no drilling or sampling. Sources: regulatory authorities, present/former owners, historical title searches, aerial photos, regulatory databases, fire insurance maps. Includes SURROUNDING properties (migration risk).
II
Phase II — Sampling & Delineation— conducted if recommended by Phase I; samples groundwater/soil at probable contamination locations and compares to regulatory/industry standards. INTRUSIVE testing begins here.
III
Phase III — Remediation— detailed description of contaminants, recommended remediation process, confirmatory sampling during remediation, and measurement of remediation success
2
ESA vs Environmental Audit2 items
▾
1
Environmental Site Assessment (ESA)— determines whether a SPECIFIC SITE is contaminated (CSA Z768)
2
Environmental Audit (Compliance Audit)— broader review of a CORPORATION'S systems/policies and environmental risk (CSA Z773); "systematic process of objectively obtaining and evaluating evidence regarding a verifiable assertion." May include ESAs.
Neither is legally mandatory, but following CSA guidelines represents the MINIMUM PRACTICE STANDARD courts will hold professionals to.

4
Confidentiality vs Reporting — Key Conflictrule + exception
▾
Default rule: Legislative obligations to report contamination AND professional codes of ethics (overriding duty to safeguard the environment) generally OVERRIDE contractual confidentiality clauses.
Possible exception: A tightly worded confidentiality clause, OR an assessment prepared at a LAWYER'S request (potential privilege), may give the client grounds to argue the report is exempt from the reporting obligation. Seek SPECIFIC legal advice before reporting in such cases.
Vendor disclosure duty: Even a seller who commissions an ESA without buyer involvement cannot withhold contamination findings — the law implies an obligation to disclose all relevant facts about a property to the purchaser, unless patently obvious.
5
2 Categories of Contamination — Remedies for Landowners2 items
▾
1
Contamination Wholly on the Property— claim against the PRIOR landowner for breach of purchase/sale contract or innocent/negligent/fraudulent misrepresentation; realtors may also be liable depending on what they knew
2
Contamination from a Neighbouring Property— common law actions: trespass, negligence, misrepresentation, STRICT LIABILITY (nuisance — no need to prove negligence). All property owners are responsible for contaminants flowing FROM their property.

4 Elements of Strict Liability (Rylands v. Fletcher)4 items · ALL required
▾
1
Non-natural use of land
2
Brought onto land something likely to do mischief if it escaped
3
The substance escaped
4
Damage caused to plaintiff's property or person
No need to prove negligence — same 4-part test as Ch.12 nuisance, applied here to environmental contamination migrating between properties.
7
CN Railway 2-Stage Compensation Test2 items
▾
1
Did the claimant act REASONABLY in remediating the property?
2
Were the REMEDIATION COSTS reasonable?
From CN Railway v. A.B.C. Recycling (BC, under the Environmental Management Act) — trial court awarded virtually all remediation costs PLUS full legal costs (vs the usual ~30%); on appeal, the legal-costs portion was reduced to the ordinary award.

Levels of Government & Key Federal Statutes3 levels
▾
1
Federal— focus: fisheries, oceans, interprovincial/international issues, toxic substances (e.g. PCBs). Key statutes: Fisheries Act, Canadian Environmental Protection Act (CEPA), Transportation of Dangerous Goods Act, Canadian Environmental Assessment Act. International treaties (NAFTA, Kyoto) also relevant.
2
Provincial— air quality, boilers, parks, hazardous goods transport/disposal, drainage, environmental emergencies, permits, public health, pesticides, land use. Most provinces (ON, AB, NS, BC) have comprehensive environmental statutes.
3
Municipal/Local— derive authority from the PROVINCE; historically controlled environment via ZONING, now increasingly pass dedicated environmental bylaws
Conflict rule: Where federal and provincial/local legislation directly conflict operationally, FEDERAL legislation governs.

Environmental Offences — Strict Liability + Due Diligence1 rule + 5 methods
▾
Rule: Except for Criminal Code provisions, environmental offences are generally STRICT LIABILITY — once the prohibited act is proven, the ONLY defence is DUE DILIGENCE (all reasonable steps taken to satisfy statutory obligations). Individuals require some element of personal fault — cannot be guilty solely because their corporation is guilty. Since 2004 Criminal Code amendments, some offences now carry JAIL terms (not just fines), and liability can attach to corporations, societies, partnerships, unions, and associations for acts of directors/officers/employees/agents/contractors.
5 Practical Methods to Demonstrate Due Diligence
1
Maintain a program of environmental auditing
2
Ensure policies/systems exist to PREVENT statutory breaches
3
Ensure policies/systems exist to FULFILL reporting requirements
4
Ensure everyone (directors, employees, agents, contractors) knows their responsibilities
5
ACTIVELY MONITOR and regularly review all of the above— simply having a policy "on paper" without active monitoring is NOT due diligence
11
Parties Potentially Liable for Environmental Cleanup7 items
▾
1
The polluter— general principle: "polluter pays"
2
Owner of the offending substance, process, or land
3
Party with charge, custody, or control of the substance/process/land
4
Party holding the required statutory licence
5
Party who caused or contributed to the damage
6
Successors (receivers, assignees, trustees, principals, agents)
7
Officers/directors of the CURRENT owner, and parties involved in cleanup
"Orphan site" liability extends back in time: Even a CURRENT owner with no connection to the original contamination can be liable. Former owners can be held to TODAY'S standards even if their actions were legal/acceptable at the time (e.g. 1930s oil dumping). When setting standards for new projects, professionals should anticipate FUTURE standards, not just meet current minimums.

4 Types of Federal Environmental Assessment4 items
▾
1
Screening— used for modest projects
2
Comprehensive Study— used for modest projects
3
Mediation— used for large/controversial projects
4
Panel Review— used for large/controversial projects; involves public input
Federal assessment required if a project has federal implications (e.g. interprovincial pipeline, navigable waterways/oceans, or meets Canadian Environmental Assessment Agency criteria). A project COULD face both federal and provincial assessments, but jurisdictions generally coordinate to avoid duplication. Outcomes may include conditions, extra project stages (possibly off-site), or in some cases the project cannot proceed at all.
