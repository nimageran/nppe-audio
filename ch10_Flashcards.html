Contract A vs. Contract B
Click to flip
The Basics: Tendering in Canada creates two contracts.

Contract A: The "Bidding Contract." Formed the exact second a contractor submits a valid bid. It contains the rules of the game.
Contract B: The "Construction Contract." Formed only with the winner to actually build the project.
A = The Game | B = The Job
The "Ron Engineering" Case
Click to flip
The Story: A contractor made a $750k mistake in their bid, tried to cancel it after the deadline, and lost in the Supreme Court.

The Rule: This case invented "Contract A." It proved that once the tender deadline passes, bids are legally locked and irrevocable.
Deadline Passes = Locked In
Compliant Bids
Click to flip
The Story: You submit the cheapest bid, but you forgot to include the required schedule.

The Rule: The owner MUST reject your bid. Contract A is only formed if the bid perfectly complies with the tender rules. Accepting a non-compliant bid allows all other bidders to sue the owner.
Strict Rules, Zero Exceptions
The Privilege Clause
Click to flip
The Basics: A line in the tender stating: "Lowest or any bid not necessarily accepted."

The Trap: It lets the owner pick a higher bid based on disclosed criteria (like better experience). It does NOT let the owner pick a non-compliant bid or invent secret judging criteria later.
Not a License to Cheat
Bid Shopping
Click to flip
The Story: A General Contractor shows Subcontractor X's low bid to Subcontractor Y and says, "If you beat this price, you get the job."

The Rule: This is completely unethical and often illegal. It poisons the bidding process and violates the owner's Duty of Fairness.
Price Peddling is Prohibited
Duty of Fairness
Click to flip
The Rule: The owner legally promises to treat every bidder exactly the same.

The Catch: You cannot give one bidder extra time, share secret site data with only one guy, or let someone fix a typo after the deadline. A level playing field is legally required.
Equal Treatment for All
Bid Bonds
Click to flip
The Basics: A financial guarantee submitted with the bid.

The Rule: If you win the tender but refuse to sign Contract B, the owner cashes your bond. The money covers the price difference between your bid and the second-place bidder.
Put Your Money Where Your Mouth Is
Letters of Intent (LOI)
Click to flip
The Story: The owner writes, "We intend to hire you. Start mobilizing your gear," but you haven't signed the final contract.

The Rule: It is usually just an "agreement to agree." If the deal collapses, you get paid for the work you already did (Quantum Meruit), but you cannot sue for lost future profits.
Start Working, But No Promises
Bid Depositories
Click to flip
The Concept: A secure facility where subcontractors submit sealed bids before the main deadline.

The Goal: It physically stops General Contractors from "Bid Shopping" at the last second, forcing them to use the legitimate sealed numbers.
The Anti-Bid Shopping Vault
The "Obvious Error" Exception
Click to flip
The Story: A contractor meant to bid $1,000,000 but typed $1,000.

The Rule: The owner cannot legally "snap up" an obviously broken bid. If the mistake is glaringly obvious on its face, trying to hold them to it breaches the owner's Duty of Fairness.
Cannot Exploit an Obvious Typo
