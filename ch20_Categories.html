Chapter 20 — Labour Law
Lists, types & categories only

1
Labour Law vs Employment Law2 items
▾
1
Labour Law— governs UNION–MANAGEMENT and employee–union relationships; mostly provincial legislation (federal sector: Canada Labour Code)
2
Employment Law— governs employee–employer relationships where there is NO union representation (see Ch.21)
2
2 Basic Purposes of Labour Legislation2 items
▾
1
Promote industrial peace
2
Allow employees to organize and bargain as a group— right to form a union is protected under Charter s.2(d) (freedom of association), recognized for collective bargaining since 2007
Charter limit: s.2(d) protects the right to associate and engage in collective action on workplace issues — it does NOT guarantee a particular outcome, a specific statutory regime, or cover all aspects of "collective bargaining."
3
3 Categories of Worker3 items
▾
1
Employee— does NOT usually determine the means and methods of the work; entitled to organize/bargain collectively
2
Independent Contractor— determines the means/methods to achieve results. PROHIBITED by law from colluding to fix prices (cannot unionize).
3
Dependent Contractor— performs work "for compensation," may supply own tools/vehicles/equipment, need not have an employment contract, but is in a position of ECONOMIC DEPENDENCE — relationship resembles an employee more than an independent contractor
The line between these categories "is never easy" — courts examine an economic spectrum from "true entrepreneur" to "individual worker," looking primarily at degree of CONTROL over methods. Same general test used to distinguish employee vs independent contractor under tax law (Ch.21).
4
Categories Often EXCLUDED from "Employee" Definition2 items
▾
1
Supervisory / Managerial Employees— duties may conflict with union interests; many statutes exclude those who "exercise managerial functions" or work in a confidential labour-relations capacity
2
Certain Regulated Professions (Ontario-specific)— Ontario excludes members of the architectural, dental, land surveying, legal, or medical profession employed in a PROFESSIONAL capacity. NOT typical of other provinces — engineers elsewhere may be included in a bargaining unit.
Professional duty prevails: If a professional is in a union and a strike occurs, but refusing to work would endanger public safety, the professional's duty to public safety PREVAILS over union solidarity (see Ch.3 conflicts of interest).
5
Establishing Union Representation — Processkey steps
▾
1
Union obtains majority employee support— either employees approach a union, or a union initiates an organizing drive
2
Union demands employer recognition as bargaining agent
3
Employer agrees OR demands an election— a democratic vote of employees on certification. Some provinces allow certification WITHOUT a vote if a large enough majority (e.g. 55%) has joined.
Construction industry challenge: Project-based hiring means employment often ends before a union can secure majority support on any one project.
6
Unfair Labour Practices — Examplesexamples
▾
1
Threats, coercion, intimidation— e.g. threatening to fire a union organizer or close the business if certified
2
Increased pay/benefits, transfers, firings aimed at discouraging unionization
3
Discriminatory discharge/layoff/transfer of union members— employer bears the burden to prove the action was NOT related to union membership (Manitoba example)
Wal-Mart Jonquière case: SCC found Wal-Mart liable for closing a store during a union organizing drive — it failed to prove it would have closed anyway. NOT unfair: closing a business for reasons genuinely UNRELATED to union activity.
7
4 Union Security Arrangements4 items
▾
1
Closed Shop— union membership is a PRECONDITION of hiring. Legal in Canada (struck down in the US as freedom-of-association violation, but NOT in Canada).
2
Union Shop— employees must become union members AFTER being hired
3
Open Shop— no union membership requirement; often synonymous with "non-union shop"
4
Rand Formula— employees must pay union DUES but need NOT join the union. Rationale: non-members still benefit from union-negotiated wages/benefits, so they shouldn't get a "free ride." Held CONSTITUTIONAL by SCC (Lavigne v. OPSEU).
8
Work Stoppages — Key Rules3 rules
▾
1
Strikes/lockouts illegal while a collective agreement is in force— legitimate only at an impasse in negotiations (typically after expiry)
2
Replacement workers— some provinces (e.g. BC) PROHIBIT hiring replacement workers hired after bargaining notice/commencement to perform striking/locked-out work. Workers themselves face NO legal impediment to seeking alternate employment during a stoppage.
3
Picketing— protected free speech if PEACEFUL and non-intimidating. Public has the right to respect OR ignore a picket line. Courts can issue injunctions limiting numbers/location if access is effectively blocked.
Construction-specific tension: Other trades on the same site who have no relationship with the picketing union still have the RIGHT TO WORK — competing rights between the picketing union and disinterested third parties.
9
Successor Employer vs Common Employer2 items
▾
1
Successor Employer— buys a business or substantial assets of a business; INHERITS the union bargaining obligation. A mere sale cannot eliminate this obligation.
2
Common/Related Employer— two or more entities carrying on associated/related activities under COMMON CONTROL OR DIRECTION may be treated as ONE employer for labour purposes (Ontario s.1(4))
Vulcan Packaging principle: Restructuring through bankruptcy + asset sale to related parties (same key people, shareholders, unchanged business) was found to be "corporate sleight-of-hand" — the new entities were bound by the original collective agreement. Purpose: prevent corporate transactions designed purely to eliminate a union.
10
Grievance Arbitration vs Interest Arbitration2 items
▾
1
Interest Arbitration— used to ARRIVE AT a collective agreement (sets future terms)
2
Grievance Arbitration— determines RIGHTS under an EXISTING collective agreement. A grievance = complaint that the other party breached the agreement. Most collective agreements require all grievances to be finally resolved through this process.
11
Layoff vs Termination + Seniority Rule3 items
▾
1
Layoff— TEMPORARY suspension of employment (concept exists only under collective agreements, not common law)
2
Termination— PERMANENT end of the employment relationship
3
Seniority Rule— most senior employees are LAST laid off and FIRST rehired; employer cannot choose to lay off a senior employee ahead of a junior one
Common law vs collective agreement on termination: Common law allows termination for just cause OR with reasonable notice (no cause needed — see Ch.21). Most collective agreements REMOVE the "without cause" option — termination requires just cause, typically with progressive warnings unless conduct is extreme. Arbitrators (unlike courts) CAN order reinstatement of a terminated employee.
