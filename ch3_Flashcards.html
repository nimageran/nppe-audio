The Prime Directive
Click to flip
The Rule: Your absolute highest loyalty is to the safety, health, and welfare of the public.

The Catch: The public outranks your boss, your client, and your own bank account. If a client tells you to hide a safety risk to save money, you must refuse.
Public Safety > Everything
Conflict of Interest
Click to flip
The Story: You are designing a factory, and you specify a brand of steel that is secretly sold by your wife's company.

The Rule: A conflict exists anytime your personal financial interests could interfere with your unbiased professional judgment.
Divided Loyalties
How to handle a Conflict of Interest
Click to flip
The Action: First, try to avoid it completely.

The Backup: If you cannot avoid it, you MUST disclose it immediately, in writing, to all affected parties. You can only proceed if everyone explicitly agrees. Silence is an ethical breach.
Disclose and Get Consent
Moonlighting (Side Jobs)
Click to flip
The Story: Doing engineering work on the weekends while holding a full-time job.

The Rule: It is only legal if: 1. You tell your main boss. 2. You tell the side-client about your main job. 3. There is no conflict of interest. 4. You do not use company time or software.
No Secrets, No Stolen Pens
Whistleblowing
Click to flip
The Story: Reporting your own company to the authorities for doing something dangerous.

The Rule: You must escalate the problem internally first (talk to your boss, then the CEO). You only blow the whistle to the government if the company ignores you AND public safety is at risk.
Internal First, External Last
Reviewing Another Engineer's Work
Click to flip
The Catch: It is considered highly unethical to review or double-check another engineer's work behind their back.

The Rule: You must notify them first. You do not need their permission, but you must give them the courtesy of knowing they are being audited.
Warn Before You Criticize
Confidentiality
Click to flip
The Rule: You must keep your employer's and client's business secrets strictly confidential.

The Exception: You are legally forced to break confidentiality ONLY if ordered by a judge, or if keeping the secret puts the public in immediate physical danger.
Secrets Stay Secret (Unless Danger)
Duty to Report (Policing the Profession)
Click to flip
The Story: You discover another engineer at a different firm is falsifying test results.

The Rule: You cannot look the other way. You have an ethical duty to report unethical or incompetent engineers to the provincial association.
Police Your Own
Secret Commissions (Bribery)
Click to flip
The Story: A contractor offers you a 5% "finder's fee" if you recommend them for your client's project.

The Rule: Taking secret money to influence a professional decision is bribery. It is grounds for immediate loss of your license.
Secret Money = Bribery
Ethics vs. Law
Click to flip
The Catch: The exam will trick you by describing an action that is perfectly legal, but highly unethical.

The Rule: The law is just the bare minimum standard. The Code of Ethics holds you to a much higher standard of moral conduct.
Law = Minimum | Ethics = Maximum
Working Outside Your Expertise
Click to flip
The Story: You need money, so you accept a job you do not actually know how to do.

The Rule: This is an ethical breach. You can only take the job if you clearly warn the client you are not an expert AND you hire a qualified specialist to supervise you.
Stick To What You Know
Environmental Ethics
Click to flip
The Rule: You must consider the environmental impact of every project and seek to minimize harm.

The Catch: If a client demands you ignore environmental regulations to speed up a project, your duty to the public and the environment overrides their demand.
Protect the Planet
