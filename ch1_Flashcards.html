The Canadian Constitution
Click to flip
The Story: It is the ultimate master rulebook for the entire country.

The Rule: Its main job is to divide power between the Federal government and the Provincial governments so they do not fight over who controls what. Any law that breaks the Constitution is automatically cancelled.
The Supreme Rulebook
Federal Government Powers
Click to flip
The Concept: The Federal government is only allowed to make laws about things that affect the whole country on a large scale.

Examples: The military, criminal law, postal service, currency, and patents.
National Scope = Federal
Provincial Government Powers
Click to flip
The Concept: The Provinces make laws for local, day-to-day life inside their borders.

The Exam Trick: Provinces control property, civil rights, and the regulation of professions. This means engineering is regulated by the province (like APEGM), not the national government.
Local Rules = Provincial
Charter of Rights and Freedoms
Click to flip
The Story: It is a shield that protects regular people from the government stepping on their basic rights.

The Rule: It guarantees things like freedom of speech and religion. It only applies to government actions, not fights between private companies.
People vs. The Government
What is "Statute Law"?
Click to flip
The Basics: Laws that are officially written and passed by elected politicians (like Parliament or the Provincial Legislature).

The Rule: Statutes are incredibly powerful. A new statute can instantly override an old judge-made law.
Written by Politicians
What is "Common Law"?
Click to flip
The Basics: It is the system used in 9 out of 10 Canadian provinces.

The Rule: It is law created by judges in courtrooms. When a judge solves a problem, future judges look back at that decision and follow the same pattern to keep the rules fair and predictable.
Rule of Past Precedent
What is "Civil Law" (Quebec)?
Click to flip
The Exception: Quebec does not use Common Law. They use the French "Civil Law" system.

The Rule: Instead of looking at past cases like other provinces, Quebec judges look at one massive, written book of rules (the Civil Code) to find the answer.
Quebec = The Rule Book
What is "Stare Decisis"?
Click to flip
The Story: A Latin term that is the beating heart of the Common Law system. It means "let the decision stand."

The Rule: Lower courts MUST follow the legal decisions made by higher courts in their province. They are not allowed to ignore the higher judges.
Follow the Leader
The Supreme Court of Canada
Click to flip
The Hierarchy: It is the absolute highest court in the country.

The Rule: Because of *stare decisis*, if the Supreme Court of Canada makes a decision, every single lower court in every single province must follow it forever. Their word is final.
The Final Boss
Public Law vs. Private Law
Click to flip
Public Law: The Government vs. A Citizen (e.g., Criminal Law, Tax Law). The state is enforcing the rules.

Private Law: A Citizen vs. A Citizen (e.g., Contracts, Torts). The government is just providing the courtroom so two people can settle a fight.
State Fight vs. Citizen Fight
Do International Treaties apply in Canada?
Click to flip
The Story: The government signs a trade deal like NAFTA/USMCA with another country.

The Catch: It does NOT instantly become law inside Canada. To make it legally binding on Canadians, the politicians must first vote to pass a new domestic "Statute."
Treaties Need Statutes First
