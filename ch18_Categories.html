2 Purposes of Construction Lien Statutes2 items
▾
1
Provide security of payment— for suppliers of labour, materials, and equipment rentals on construction projects
2
Facilitate credit in the construction industry— unlike cars/houses, materials like drywall/paint cannot be repossessed once installed
A lien = a charge or claim against real property improved by construction, registered against title. Registration makes the property hard to sell/finance until removed. Note: A lien claim is NOT the only remedy — the unpaid party can also sue for breach of contract (against whoever hired them), though that's a hollow remedy against an insolvent/absent party.

4 Steps to Making and Proving a Lien Claim4 items
▾
1
File the Claim of Lien— register documents at the land registry within statutory time limits (e.g. relative to substantial completion, last day worked, or notice to owner)
2
Perfect the Lien— commence legal action within the limitation period (commonly ~1 year) to prove the work/material was supplied to that specific improvement and all statutory requirements were met. Some provinces also require filing a lis pendens (certificate of pending litigation) within a set time, or the lien may be dismissed.
3
Defendant Raises Defences— deficiencies in work, set-offs, proof of payment, limitation defences
4
Enforce the Proven Lien— if substitute security (e.g. lien bond) is in place, claimant is paid from it; otherwise the claimant becomes a judgment creditor and may force a sale of the property (rarely happens in practice)
Always get legal advice: limitation periods vary by province and may be ABRIDGED (e.g. Alberta: 30 days via notice to commence action). Lien claims are frequently lost on technical/procedural grounds even when the underlying claim is valid.

Who May Claim a Lien — BC Builders Lien Act Examplekey definitions
▾
1
Workers, Contractors, Subcontractors, Material Suppliers— typical claimants. No privity with owner/GC required — claimant can be 2+ tiers removed.
2
"Subcontractor" definition EXCLUDES subconsultants— a person engaged by an architect, engineer, or material supplier is NOT a "subcontractor" and has no lien right
3
Prime architect/engineer CAN claim a lien— "services" includes architect/engineer services even before construction begins, AND equipment rental. Must relate to an actual "improvement" — drawings for a project never built ≠ lien right.
Material supplier challenge: Must often PROVE DELIVERY to the specific site. Suppliers serving multiple jobsites should designate separate orders per site and keep accurate delivery records, or risk being unable to prove the lien.

Substitute security replaces the land as security for the lien — allows the lien to be removed from title so funds can keep flowing. Security value = lien amount + security for costs (Ontario: lesser of $50,000 or 25% of the lien; BC has removed this requirement via case law). Held in trust/court until the claim is resolved or abandoned.
5
Trust Provisions — Trustee/Beneficiary Chaincascading structure
▾
1
Owner pays General Contractor— funds become impressed with a trust immediately upon receipt; GC = trustee, all subs/workers/suppliers directly below = beneficiaries
2
GC pays Subcontractor— the cycle repeats: subcontractor becomes trustee of those funds for ITS workers/suppliers
3
Rule: pay beneficiaries before using funds for any other purpose— e.g. cannot use progress payment funds to pay down a bank loan or buy equipment if subs remain unpaid
Breach of trust consequences: Quasi-criminal penalties (fines/jail under the lien statute, not the Criminal Code). Directors/officers of a corporate breaching party may be PERSONALLY liable to repay trust funds + face sanctions. Anyone knowingly participating in the breach may also be liable. Best practice: maintain SEPARATE bank accounts per project — never co-mingle funds, even temporarily.
Mackenzie Redi-Mix principle: A contractor who has paid a subcontractor IN FULL has discharged its trust obligation to those further down the chain (e.g. the sub's suppliers) — the contractor is NOT a guarantor of the subcontractor's debts to others.
6
2 Holdback Systems2 items
▾
1
Multiple Holdback System— ON, SK, BC. Owner, GC, AND every subcontractor each retain 10% from the party below them.
2
Single Holdback System— Alberta. Only the OWNER retains the holdback from the GC. (Often trickles down contractually anyway — GC requires subs to accept the same % so it doesn't have to finance it.)
Release trigger: Substantial completion (determined by consultant's certificate, who must act impartially), then a fixed period after (e.g. 45–55 days). Defence value: The party retaining holdback can discharge ALL liens against it by paying the holdback amount — its maximum liability to lien claimants is capped at the holdback, regardless of total lien value (claimants share pro rata).
Shimco principle (BC): A lien against the LAND and a lien against the HOLDBACK are now treated as two SEPARATE liens. If a claimant misses the land-lien deadline, it may still assert a lien against the holdback — but must do so via legal proceedings BEFORE the holdback is paid out.

Lien Priority — Key Rules3 items
▾
1
Registration order generally governs priority vs other charges— mortgages/judgments registered AFTER a lien rank below it; in some jurisdictions, lien priority "relates back" to the last date work was performed
2
Construction lenders— in some provinces, rank ahead of liens only for funds advanced BEFORE the lien was filed. Lenders must search title before each progress draw; refuse to advance if a lien has been filed.
3
CRA "Requirement to Pay" vs unpaid subcontractors (PCL v. Norex)— if the contractor has a valid RIGHT OF SET-OFF against the defaulting subcontractor, unpaid suppliers' claims take PRIORITY over the CRA
Critical drafting point: Always include a right of set-off in owner-contractor contracts — otherwise the owner may end up paying the holdback amount TWICE (once to CRA, once to unpaid claimants).
8
2 Precautions Against Paying Twice (GC/Owner)2 items
▾
1
Require performance bonds and L&M payment bonds from subcontractors— if a sub defaults on paying its workers/suppliers, the sub's surety bears the responsibility instead of the GC
2
Require monthly statutory declarations as a precondition to payment— sworn statement that all financial obligations (workers, subs, suppliers, WCB premiums, taxes) have been met. Knowingly swearing a FALSE declaration = criminal liability for perjury.
Even if a GC has properly disbursed 100% of received funds, it can still face liens if a SUBCONTRACTOR breaches trust or becomes insolvent — the GC is typically still contractually responsible for removing all liens at its own expense.
