4 Elements to Prove Negligence4 items · ALL must be proven
▾
1
Duty of Care— defendant owed a duty to the plaintiff (based on reasonable foreseeability)
2
Breach of Duty— defendant did not meet the standard of care of a reasonably competent professional in the same field
3
Loss or Damage Suffered— plaintiff actually suffered a loss; if no loss, claim fails even if negligence occurred
4
Proximate Cause— the breach was the proximate (legal) cause of the loss; must pass the "but for" test
Defence: Defendant needs to disprove only ONE of the four elements to defeat the claim.
3
2 Categories of Breach of Standard of Care2 items
▾
1
Incompetence (Lack of Skill)— relatively rare; professionals generally avoid work they are unqualified to do; entering a new field without adequate training is a risk
2
Lack of Care— most common; preventable through checklists, peer review, independent checking of calculations, and continuing education
Standard: NOT perfection — a reasonably competent member of the profession. Specialists are held to the specialist standard, not the average professional standard.

3 Types of Misrepresentation3 items · remedies differ
▾
TypeDefinitionRemedy
InnocentFalse statement, no intent to deceive, no carelessnessRescission only (cancellation of contract). No damages unless fraud or negligence.
NegligentFalse statement made without due care or skill; plaintiff reasonably relied on itRescission + damages. Pure economic loss IS claimable (recognized exception).
FraudulentStatement known to be false OR made not caring if true or false; intended to induce action; plaintiff suffers damageRescission + damages (most powerful remedy). Can also result in criminal fraud charges.
Misrepresentation by omission: Failing to disclose information can also constitute misrepresentation — e.g. owner withholding soils report from bidders.

4 Elements to Prove Fraud4 items · ALL required
▾
1
Representation is untrue in fact
2
Defendant knows it is untrue OR is indifferent as to its truth
3
Intended or calculated to induce the plaintiff to act upon it
4
Plaintiff acts upon it and suffers damage

4 Hallmarks of a Fiduciary Relationship4 items
▾
1
One party (fiduciary) has discretion or power
2
Fiduciary can unilaterally exercise that power to affect the beneficiary's interests
3
Beneficiary is peculiarly vulnerable or at the mercy of the fiduciary
4
Does NOT normally arise between arm's length commercial parties
Key point: The A/E/G relationship with clients is NOT inherently fiduciary — courts look for these specific hallmarks. Breach of fiduciary duty is described as "carrying a stench of dishonesty."

4 Elements to Prove Nuisance (Rylands v. Fletcher)4 items · ALL required
▾
1
Defendant made a non-natural use of land
2
Defendant brought onto land something likely to do mischief if it escaped
3
The substance escaped
4
Damage caused to plaintiff's property or person
Nuisance is a strict liability tort — negligence does NOT need to be proven. Also applies to vibrations (e.g. pile driving). Trespass is also strict liability.

2 Categories of Product Defects2 items
▾
1
Product not manufactured according to specifications
2
Product was manufactured to spec, but the specifications were inadequate
Canada vs USA: In Canada, consumer must prove NEGLIGENCE (all 4 elements). In the USA, strict liability applies — no negligence needed. Once plaintiff proves defect in Canada, burden shifts to manufacturer to disprove standard of care breach.

4 Methods to Reduce Negligence Claim Risk4 items
▾
1
Checklists and standard procedures— professional associations and liability insurers often provide standard checklists
2
Independent checking of calculations— hire someone else to verify
3
Peer review— cost more than offset by claim reductions; some associations require it after a claim
4
Continuing education— failure to keep up with changing standards = negligence; standard is measured at time of the act, not time of trial

Pure Economic Loss — Rule + 2 Key Exceptions3 items
▾
The Rule
–
Pure economic loss is NOT claimable in negligence— loss of money only, no physical injury or property damage; to prevent "floodgates" problem
2 Key Exceptions
1
Negligent Misrepresentation— pure economic loss IS claimable (Hedley Byrne); plaintiff must have reasonably relied on the statement
2
Substantial Danger to Health and Safety— Winnipeg Condominium: pure economic loss recoverable if there is a substantial danger to occupant health and safety
