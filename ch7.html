Visualization Logic: The "JustDo" branch is placed deliberately as the intuitive-but-wrong choice, because most people would assume quietly performing disputed work and sorting out payment afterward is the safe, cooperative path — Kiewit proves the opposite: the Supreme Court held that simply DOING the work was itself treated as conceding the work was already covered by the contract, destroying the very claim for extra payment the contractor was trying to preserve. The "Fix" box shows the legislative response this harsh result triggered, and the key word in that fix is "WITHOUT PREJUDICE" — that specific phrase is what breaks the Kiewit logic, because it converts silent, ambiguous performance into an explicit, on-the-record statement that performing now does NOT concede the scope dispute. The Availability diamond at the bottom is a critical practical caveat: this protection is NOT automatically available everywhere in Canada — the chapter specifically flags Alberta as a province without a statutory right, meaning parties operating there (or anywhere a relevant statute doesn't exist) need to negotiate equivalent protest language directly into their contract, or risk falling right back into the original Kiewit trap.

Visualization Logic: Treating these as three SEQUENTIAL filters (rather than three unrelated rules) shows that a damages claim has to survive ALL three independently — passing one filter says nothing about whether the next one will let the claim through. Filter 3's remoteness test deserves the most attention because it contains the chapter's most counterintuitive and exam-relevant distinction: a delay causing lost REVENUE is the kind of garden-variety consequence any reasonable party would foresee, but that SAME delay causing the other party's complete BANKRUPTCY is typically too remote — not because bankruptcy is impossible, but because it's not the kind of consequence parties would ordinarily anticipate when making the deal. The Fix box at the bottom turns this filter into actionable drafting advice: foreseeability is measured AT THE TIME OF CONTRACTING, which means a party with an unusually severe exposure (e.g., "if you're late, I lose a once-in-a-decade business opportunity worth millions") needs to actually SAY SO before signing — silence about unusual risk isn't neutral, it's what gets that risk excluded from recovery later as too remote.

Visualization Logic: Running the actual dollar figures rather than discussing the concept abstractly is what makes this economic argument land — a $5 component absorbing the FULL risk of a $1.5 million consequence would have to be priced at roughly $100,000 to make that bet rational for the manufacturer, and that inflated price would apply to EVERY seal sold, including the overwhelming majority that never fail at all. The diagram's structure shows WHY consequential damages exclusions are a genuinely efficient market solution rather than just a one-sided manufacturer protection: it's cheaper, in aggregate, for buyers to pay $5 and accept the (small, monitored) risk than for everyone to pay $100,000 to fully insure against a rare catastrophic failure. The Limits branch at the bottom is the necessary caveat — these clauses are powerful but not absolute, and the chapter specifically flags that ambiguous or overly broad consequential-damages language gets read narrowly and against whoever drafted it, which is exactly why the chapter insists these clauses should be drafted by a lawyer rather than copied casually from a template.

5. Fundamental Breach vs Simple Breach — The High-Stakes Fork

Visualization Logic: The "Danger" branch is the entire reason this classification matters so much — it's not a neutral labeling exercise, it's a high-stakes bet, because guessing wrong doesn't just mean "the termination doesn't work," it actively FLIPS legal fault onto the party who was originally the innocent one. This is precisely why the chapter frames unilateral termination as "a very serious step that may have potentially disastrous consequences" — a party convinced they're righteously ending a contract over what they perceive as a major breach can, if a court later disagrees with that characterization, end up as the DEFENDANT in the resulting lawsuit instead of the plaintiff. The "Lesson" box at the bottom reinforces a practical takeaway worth internalizing: because COURTS, not the parties themselves, ultimately decide whether a breach was fundamental, no party should ever treat their own internal judgment call on this question as final or safe without first getting legal advice.

Visualization Logic: The two starting boxes ("Before" vs "AnyTime") show the actual relationship between these concepts: anticipatory breach is specifically about timing — it can ONLY happen BEFORE the performance deadline, by definition, since it's about announcing a future breach in advance. The CheckSeverity diamond captures the overlap precisely: an anticipatory breach automatically becomes repudiation too, but ONLY if the threatened future breach would itself be fundamental in nature — an anticipated SIMPLE breach (say, an owner indicating they might be a day late on a minor administrative item) is still an anticipatory breach in the technical sense, but doesn't rise to repudiation, which carries the more serious automatic fundamental-breach classification and the corresponding termination rights that come with it.

The Q2 diamond carries the heaviest visual weight because it captures a trap distinct from the fundamental/simple distinction in diagrams 5 and 6: even where a party has an ABSOLUTELY VALID underlying reason to terminate, getting the PROCEDURAL mechanics wrong — missing a required notice period, skipping a cure window the breaching party was entitled to — can independently make the termination wrongful, regardless of how clearly the other side was actually in breach. The convenience-clause branch is structurally different from the other two paths since it doesn't depend on breach analysis at all — it's a pure contractual right to walk away, usually priced through a required payment to the other side, existing specifically so parties can exit cleanly without the messy, risky business of arguing about fault.

Visualization Logic: The "Eliminated" box is placed first and given strong visual weight because it disposes of the most intuitive-feeling option immediately — it might seem natural to ask "well how much did the contractor save by cutting corners," but the chapter is explicit this measure is IRRELEVANT, since damages exist to compensate the OWNER's actual loss, not to confiscate the contractor's ill-gotten savings (those are two completely different legal questions). The real decision then happens in the three F-boxes, all converging on a SINGLE underlying concern explicit in the Strata Corp NW 1714 reasoning: courts are fundamentally worried about handing the injured party a WINDFALL — an amount of money roughly equal to the repair cost when there's no genuine intention to actually spend it on repairs would leave the owner with cash in hand AND a still-functional (if technically deficient) building, which the court explicitly calls "not a reasonable result." This is why genuine intention and reasonableness of repair, not just the existence of a quantifiable deficiency, end up driving which of the two remaining measures (M2 or M3) actually gets applied.

This demonstrates a genuinely important practical lesson buried in this question: responding to a missed deadline with anything other than immediate, decisive termination can inadvertently waive your own enforcement rights, even while you're actively in the middle of trying to assert them.
