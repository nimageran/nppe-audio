4 Causes of Breach of Contract4 items
▾
1
Inability— wants to perform but cannot (e.g. lack of financial resources)
2
Inadvertence— unintentionally failed to carry out a promise or obligation
3
Disagreement— parties interpret the contract differently (e.g. dispute over scope of work)
4
Anticipated Financial Losses— party calculates it is cheaper to breach than to perform (e.g. go bankrupt rather than continue)
2
4 Remedies for Breach of Contract4 items · damages is most common
▾
1
Damages— most common; money to put innocent party in same position as if contract performed properly
2
Specific Performance— court orders party to perform specific acts; rarely used in A/E/G contracts; typical for land sale refusals
3
Injunction— court prohibits a party from doing something
4
Declaratory Order— court determines rights of parties without ordering payment or action
3
3 Limitations on Recovery of Damages3 items
▾
1
Mitigation— innocent party must take reasonable steps to reduce their loss; can only recover the difference after mitigating
2
Not Speculative— must prove on balance of probabilities that the loss would have occurred AND the amount of that loss
3
Remoteness— loss must have been reasonably foreseeable at time of contract; unusual risks must be disclosed to the other party or they cannot be claimed
4
2 Types of Breach2 items
▾
1
Fundamental Breach— goes to the root of the contract; deprives innocent party of substantially all benefit. Options: (a) continue and sue for damages, OR (b) terminate and sue for damages.
2
Simple Breach— does NOT entitle innocent party to terminate or stop performing. Only remedy: sue for damages. Treating a simple breach as fundamental = the innocent party commits a fundamental breach themselves.
Critical: Never terminate a contract unilaterally without legal advice. If wrongly treated as fundamental, the terminating party becomes liable for damages.
5
2 Options for the Innocent Party After a Fundamental Breach2 items
▾
1
Continue to perform and sue for damages
2
Declare contract terminated and sue for damages
6
3 Types of Damages Clauses3 items
▾
1
Consequential Damages Exclusion— excludes indirect/consequential losses (e.g. lost business). Common in supplier contracts. Interpreted narrowly; ambiguity construed against the drafter (contra proferentem).
2
Liquidated Damages— genuine pre-agreed estimate of loss for a specific breach (e.g. $1,000/day delay). Must be a real estimate, not a penalty. Common in construction contracts.
3
Penalty Clause— sum set as punishment rather than compensation. Courts have discretion NOT to enforce penalty clauses. Distinguished from liquidated damages by intent.
Bonus clause: Entitles a party to extra payment for exceeding performance (e.g. early completion). Not required alongside liquidated damages to make them enforceable.
7
2 Ways to Measure Damages for Deficient Work2 items
▾
1
Cost of Repair / Performance— standard measure; used when repairs have been or will be carried out and it is reasonable to do so
2
Diminution in Value— used when repairs are unreasonable or the owner has no genuine intention to repair; avoids giving the innocent party a windfall
Key factor: whether the innocent party has already done repairs or genuinely intends to. Courts will not award a windfall.
8
Ways a Contract Can Be Terminated4 items
▾
1
Completion of Performance— most common; all obligations performed and warranty periods expired
2
Frustration / Impossibility— unforeseen event makes performance impossible or of no value
3
Mutual Agreement— both parties agree to end before full performance; should set out final payment terms
4
Unilateral Termination— one party terminates; either provoked by a breach OR is itself a breach. Requires strict compliance with notice and limitation periods.
9
Repudiation vs Anticipatory Breach — 2 Distinctions2 items
▾
1
Repudiation— party communicates by words or conduct that it will NOT perform its obligations. Without justification = fundamental breach.
2
Anticipatory Breach— party communicates BEFORE the time for performance that it intends to breach. If fundamental in nature, it is also repudiation.
