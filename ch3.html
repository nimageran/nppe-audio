Visualization Logic: The three tracks fanning out from a single act is the core image worth remembering, because it corrects a common intuition that "facing multiple proceedings for one act" must somehow be unfair or legally blocked. The DoubleJeopardy diamond exists specifically to dismantle that intuition — double jeopardy is a NARROW protection limited to the criminal track alone (you cannot be criminally tried twice for the identical offence), and it says NOTHING about civil or disciplinary consequences running in parallel. Each track's DIFFERENT standard of proof matters practically: a criminal acquittal (failing to meet the tough beyond-a-reasonable-doubt bar) does NOT automatically mean the civil or disciplinary cases fail too, since those only need the much lower balance-of-probabilities standard — meaning someone could walk free from criminal court yet still lose a civil case or their licence over the exact same underlying conduct.

Visualization Logic: This is the single most important diagram in the chapter because nearly every ethics scenario in the chapter questions reduces to "which of these two duties wins" — the formwork/superior pressure question (public safety over employer convenience), the geoscientist expert witness confidentiality question (public/profession over client confidentiality), and the no-damages-for-delay claim question (fairness/impartiality regardless of who's paying) all resolve by locating the conflicting duties on this hierarchy and letting the higher one win. The chapter is explicit that public safety is PARAMOUNT — that specific word matters, because it signals this isn't just "first among equals," it's a duty that overrides ALL others even where the professional risks real consequences like job loss for upholding it. Notice "Duty to Oneself" sits at the very bottom — self-interest is acknowledged as a real, legitimate consideration, but it's explicitly subordinate to every other duty on this list, meaning "but I might get fired" is never, on its own, a valid ethical justification for compromising any of the four duties ranked above it.

Visualization Logic: This diagram exists to extend diagram 1's "three tracks running in parallel" insight into its most important PRACTICAL consequence — the tracks don't just coexist independently, they can actively interfere with each other if not managed carefully. The Risk box captures the real danger: a professional defending themselves in a disciplinary hearing might feel pressure to explain their side of the story, but doing so creates a transcript or record that could later be used as evidence against them in the SEPARATE criminal proceeding, effectively undermining the constitutional right to silence through a side door. The recommended fix — applying to suspend the non-criminal proceeding — isn't about avoiding accountability, it's about SEQUENCING the proceedings properly so the accused isn't forced to choose between defending their licence and protecting their criminal defence at the same time.

Visualization Logic: The I3 box is given the heaviest visual weight because it's the genuinely surprising element of this conflict — most people analyzing a claim dispute would naturally see it as a simple TWO-PARTY tension (owner wants to pay less, contractor wants to be paid more), and miss that the person evaluating the dispute has their OWN stake in the outcome that's completely separate from either party's interest. The WhySelf branch traces exactly why: approving an extra for, say, a design error effectively means the consultant is grading their OWN homework and concluding they made a mistake — an inherently uncomfortable position that creates real pressure to side with the owner regardless of the claim's actual merits. The Resolution branch resolves this with a deceptively simple instruction (approve if honestly warranted, regardless of cause) paired with a practical self-protective step (loop in the insurer early) — the ethical answer and the practical risk-management answer turn out to be compatible, not in tension, as long as the consultant is honest about WHY they're approving something AND careful about how that approval is communicated.

Visualization Logic: The key conceptual point this diagram protects is that a constructive trust is fundamentally different from an ordinary damages award — it's not the employer proving they LOST money and being compensated for that loss, it's the law treating the employee's WRONGFUL GAINS as never having truly belonged to the employee in the first place, instead deeming them to have been held in trust for the employer all along. This distinction matters practically: an employer doesn't need to prove they suffered a quantifiable loss from the competing work (which might be hard to establish, especially if the employer's business wasn't actually harmed) — they only need to show the employee earned profits through improper competition, and those profits themselves become recoverable regardless of whether the employer can point to its own corresponding loss. The Permission diamond at the top is the simple, practical escape valve — this entire mechanism is triggered only by SECRECY and lack of consent, meaning an employee who simply asks first and gets a green light avoids the entire problem.

Visualization Logic: The two failure branches (TooLittle and TooMuch) deliberately bracket the correct path to show this isn't a simple "always report" rule — it's a genuine double bind where BOTH under-reporting and over-reporting carelessly create separate ethics problems. The chapter explicitly notes some governing bodies go further and require self-policing so strict that members can be expelled for failing to report claims against THEMSELVES, which raises the stakes on the "TooLittle" side considerably. But the "TooMuch" side is just as real a trap, especially for expert witnesses working from an incomplete record (since it's the LAWYER's job to introduce supporting facts into evidence, not the expert's) — an opinion that impugns another professional's competence, based on facts that later turn out to be incomplete or wrong, can boomerang into an ethics complaint against the ACCUSER. The Right branch resolves this tension elegantly: rather than choosing between silence and confident accusation, the safe path is TRANSPARENT CONDITIONALITY — state your assumptions, flag that your conclusion could change with new facts, and let that qualification do the protective work.

Visualization Logic: The "Normal" box at the top exists specifically to make the CFPOA's unusual extraterritorial reach stand out as an exception rather than the rule — most Canadian criminal statutes simply don't apply once you've left Canadian soil, which makes the CFPOA's two jurisdictional triggers (T1 and T2) worth remembering precisely because they're atypical. T2 deserves particular attention because "Canadian nationality" is defined more broadly than people often assume — it explicitly includes PERMANENT RESIDENTS, not just citizens, meaning a permanent resident engineer working entirely outside Canada can still fall within CFPOA's reach purely based on residency status. The US2 box at the bottom carries the most serious practical warning in the whole diagram: even a Canadian who fully resolves their CFPOA exposure (conviction, sentence served, case closed) is NOT protected from a completely separate US prosecution for the identical underlying conduct, because the US doesn't recognize double jeopardy across these two countries' legal systems — meaning "I already dealt with this in Canada" provides zero protection if the US decides to pursue the same facts independently, especially given how minimal a US jurisdictional trigger can be (even routing through the US Postal Service once).
