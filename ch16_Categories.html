2 Categories of Insurance Policies2 items
▾
1
Liability Insurance— protects insured against claims by THIRD PARTIES (e.g. professional E&O claims). A third-party claim IS a prerequisite for recovery.
2
Property Insurance— protects insured against loss/damage to property (e.g. fire, theft). A third-party claim is NOT required to recover.

4 Basic Elements of Every Insurance Policy4 items
▾
1
Deductible (Self-Retention)— portion of claim/defence costs the insured must pay
2
Coverage Period— the length of time coverage is in place
3
Coverage Description— either (a) listed PERILS that trigger the duty to indemnify/defend, or (b) an ALL-RISKS policy covering everything except specific exclusions
4
Limitation Period— contractual/statutory time limit to give notice of claim or commence legal action

5
Duty to Defend vs Duty to Indemnify2 items
▾
1
Duty to Defend— obligation to pay legal fees/defence costs (incl. expert witnesses). Insurer appoints counsel, subject to deductible. May exist even if duty to indemnify is never triggered — especially valuable for unlikely-to-succeed claims.
2
Duty to Indemnify— obligation to pay the claim itself
Out-of-scope conduct: If a claim alleges ONLY conduct outside policy scope (e.g. fraud), insurer likely has NEITHER duty. Skilled plaintiff's lawyers frame claims so at least part falls within typical coverage.
6
Subrogation — Definition + 2 Ways to Eliminate It2 items
▾
Subrogation: After paying a claim, the insurer can "step into the shoes" of the insured to recover the loss from a responsible third party (in the insured's name).
1
Waiver of Subrogation Clause— contract clause that eliminates the insurer's subrogation rights. Watch for unauthorized waivers — they can let the insurer escape its indemnity obligation.
2
Naming All Project Participants as Insureds Under the Same Policy— basic rule: subrogation CANNOT be obtained against an insured (Commonwealth v. Imperial Oil). Common for project-specific policies.
Insurable interest in construction: A subcontractor has an insurable interest in the ENTIRE PROJECT, not just its own scope (Commonwealth v. Imperial Oil).
7
Claims-Made vs Occurrence Policies2 items
▾
1
Claims-Made Policy— covers claims MADE during the policy period, regardless of when the work was done. Almost all professional liability / E&O policies are this type.
2
Occurrence Policy— covers events that OCCURRED during the policy period, regardless of when the claim is made
Gap risks: Corporate changes (renaming, new employer), retirement without maintaining coverage, and switching insurers without disclosing potential claims can ALL leave a professional uninsured for past work. Hybrid policies (occurrence-based trigger + claims-made reporting window) add further complexity — report any potential claim immediately.
8
5 Situations Where Insurer & Insured Interests May Diverge5 items
▾
1
Denial of coverage by the insurer
2
Claims alleging conduct outside scope of coverage (e.g. fraud)
3
Insured wants to defend despite a reasonable settlement offer
4
Claims exceeding policy limits
5
Settlement offers requiring compromise of a counterclaim— e.g. professional's claim for unpaid fees
Separate counsel: When no conflict exists, one lawyer represents both insurer and insured. Where a conflict/potential conflict exists, the insured should retain SEPARATE counsel — sometimes paid by the insurer. If a claim exceeds policy limits and a settlement offer falls within limits, the insured should put the insurer on notice — refusal to settle could support a bad-faith claim.

Builders' Risk: The "Resultant Damage" Exception — 2 Key Tests2 items
▾
1
State-of-the-Art Test (CN Rail v. Royal Sun Alliance)— a design is NOT "faulty" merely because it fails to meet a standard of perfection for ALL foreseeable risks. Must comply with the then-current state of the art. Higher bar than negligence/industry standard, but does not require perfection.
2
"Resultant Damage" — Courts Reject Splitting One Project Into Separate Components— (B.C. Rail v. American Home; Sayers) Damage to the faulty component itself is excluded; courts have generally treated surrounding/integrated work as part of the SAME faulty performance, NOT as separate "other property" — narrowing the resultant-damage exception
11
2 "Judgment Proofing" Methods + Why Risky2 items
▾
1
Transferring assets (e.g. to a spouse)— transferring assets AFTER a lawsuit is imminent = fraudulent transfer; courts will reverse it
2
Incorporating and keeping minimal assets in the company— ineffective because: (a) courts CAN pierce the corporate veil for improper purposes (though difficult), and (b) an individual can still be personally sued for negligence even while acting as an employee/officer
Asset planning done BEFORE notice of a potential claim is legal. After notice of a potential claim, transferring assets to avoid creditors is prohibited.
