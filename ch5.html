Visualization Logic: Reading top to bottom as a cascade from MAXIMUM exposure to MOST contained exposure clarifies what's actually changing across these structures — it's not really about profit-sharing or management style, those are secondary; the structural backbone of the whole chapter is risk containment. The partnership row deserves the most attention because it contains the chapter's sharpest, most counterintuitive fact: joint liability means a partner who had ZERO involvement in causing a debt or a negligence claim can still be personally sued for the FULL amount, not capped at their proportionate share — the computer-store example makes this concrete (the store can sue BOTH Smith and Jones for the full unpaid balance, even if only Smith bought the computer, as long as it was for partnership business). The LLP row exists specifically as professional services' answer to that partnership risk — it preserves partnership-style collaboration while ring-fencing each partner's PERSONAL liability to their own conduct, which is precisely why it's commonly used by accounting and law firms and increasingly by other professional practices.

Visualization Logic: The ConductTest diamond is the heart of this diagram because it captures something easy to miss: partnership status isn't purely a matter of CHOICE or PAPERWORK — it can be IMPOSED by conduct, regardless of whether the parties ever sat down and decided "let's be partners." This matters enormously for unsuspecting professionals, since two engineers sharing office space, regularly collaborating on projects, and splitting certain costs on an ongoing basis could find themselves legally exposed to JOINT, UNLIMITED liability for each other's business debts without ever having intended to form a formal partnership. The musicians example at the bottom crystallizes the key distinguishing factor across all three conduct indicators: CONTINUITY. A single one-off collaboration looks more like an isolated joint project, but a recurring, ongoing pattern of joint activity starts to look exactly like what Partnership Acts are designed to catch, which is precisely why the chapter recommends reviewing the provincial Partnership Act carefully before entering ANY ongoing collaborative arrangement without a written agreement.

Visualization Logic: The single boundary diamond at the top is doing all the work in this diagram because the whole limited-partnership structure rests on ONE simple, brittle distinction — money in, hands off. The moment a "limited" partner starts genuinely controlling decisions or actively contributing services (even something that might feel minor, like regularly weighing in on operational choices or doing hands-on work), they've crossed from passive INVESTOR territory into active MANAGEMENT territory, and the law responds by stripping away the entire protective benefit they were relying on. This connects to a practical structuring lesson worth remembering: a limited partnership only delivers genuine "corporation-like" protection (as the chapter describes it) if the limited partners genuinely STAY limited in practice, not just on paper — a limited partner who can't resist getting hands-on involved is, functionally, choosing to become a general partner without realizing the liability consequences of that choice.

Visualization Logic: The Q1 diamond is given the heaviest visual weight because it's a NARROW gate, not a broad fairness test — many people assume courts will pierce the veil whenever the result of NOT piercing it feels unfair (e.g., a thinly-capitalized company that genuinely can't pay its debts), but the chapter restricts this to circumstances "usually limited to acts of fraud" specifically. A company that's simply a bad investment, poorly managed, or insolvent through ordinary business misfortune does NOT trigger veil-piercing — the protection holds regardless of how sympathetic the unpaid creditor's situation might be. The "RealWorldFix" branch is included specifically to prevent confusing two genuinely different protective mechanisms: a personal guarantee is a PROACTIVE, CONTRACTUAL workaround banks and suppliers use BEFORE a problem arises (essentially refusing to rely on limited liability protection in the first place for a thinly-capitalized company), which is structurally completely different from a court RETROACTIVELY piercing the veil after the fact due to fraud — one is a private contractual choice, the other is a judicial remedy with a much higher bar to clear.

Visualization Logic: This diagram traces a logical chain that exposes a genuine structural flaw, not just a definitional fact — the chain runs from "who is the duty owed to" through "who can sue" to "but what if the wrongdoer controls that decision," and each step builds on the last to reveal WHY a special remedy (the derivative action) had to be invented at all. The key insight is the circularity at the "Problem" diamond: the fiduciary duty exists to protect the corporation FROM its own officers and directors, but enforcement of that duty is ALSO controlled by those same officers and directors, creating an obvious conflict where the wrongdoers are effectively in charge of deciding whether to pursue themselves. The derivative action exists specifically to break that circularity by giving harmed MINORITY SHAREHOLDERS — who have a real stake in the outcome but no DIRECT legal claim of their own (since the duty was never owed to them personally) — a path around the corporation's own reluctance to sue its own leadership.

Visualization Logic: Organizing these statutory exposures by CATEGORY (wages, environment, tax, liens, safety, criminal) rather than as a flat list of named statutes makes a pattern visible: nearly all of these categories share the SAME defence mechanism — due diligence — which means understanding due diligence deeply (covered in depth in Chapter 23's environmental context) gives you transferable protection across most of this web at once, rather than needing a separate defence theory for each statute. The C6 branch is deliberately isolated with its own red "NoShield" box because criminal liability is categorically different from the others — due diligence can still apply as a defence in some criminal contexts, but the chapter's separate, blunt warning is that NEITHER a corporate indemnity NOR D&O liability insurance can ever protect against actual IMPRISONMENT, since insurance and indemnities are financial instruments that simply have no mechanism to address a custodial criminal sentence. This is precisely why the chapter frames accepting an officer/director role as "a serious undertaking" deserving real reflection, not a routine resume addition.

Visualization Logic: Showing both shields side by side, each with its OWN distinct failure mode, prevents the common mistake of treating "get an indemnity AND insurance" as a complete solution — the two tools fail in genuinely DIFFERENT ways, so stacking them covers more ground but still leaves real gaps. The indemnity's weakness is fundamentally about CREDITWORTHINESS — it's only as good as the company's ability to actually pay when the bill comes due, and the exact scenario where a director most needs protection (the company is in serious trouble, facing claims or bankruptcy) is precisely the scenario where the company is LEAST able to honor that promise. The insurance's weakness is categorically different and connects directly to diagram 6's criminal liability point — insurance is a financial product addressing financial exposure, and it has no mechanism whatsoever to address imprisonment, meaning a director facing potential criminal charges under something like the Aeronautics Act gets zero protection from either shield on that specific front, regardless of how comprehensive their coverage looks on paper.

Visualization Logic: The single Timing diamond is the entire mechanism behind the chapter's Ted chapter question, and it's worth treating as a standalone, memorizable rule: insider trading isn't about WHETHER you have inside knowledge at all (Ted clearly does, as an employee-shareholder), it's about whether you traded BEFORE or AFTER that knowledge became available to everyone else. Ted's sale happened DAYS AFTER the public announcement, meaning by the time he traded, he had no informational advantage over any other investor reading the news — the information that was once "inside" had already crossed over into public knowledge, which is precisely why the chapter's answer is "not guilty." The StockTipping branch adds an important extension worth remembering separately: insider trading liability doesn't stop at the person who actually executes the trade — anyone who PASSES ALONG privileged non-public information, even without trading themselves, faces independent liability, meaning the geoscientist mentioning a confidential gold-strike result to a friend who then trades on it exposes BOTH people, not just the actual trader.
