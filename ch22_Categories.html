3 Fundamental Worker Rights Under OH&S Legislation3 items
▾
1
Right to be Informed— of known or foreseeable safety/health hazards in the workplace
2
Right to Participate— in accident/disease prevention, as a member of a joint health and safety committee or as a health and safety representative
3
Right to Refuse Dangerous Work— and be protected against dismissal or discipline for a LEGITIMATE refusal
Design safety vs workplace safety: This chapter covers WORKPLACE safety (OH&S). DESIGN safety (code compliance, choice of safety factors) is covered in Ch.13 — though temporary works like scaffolding involve BOTH.

4 Sources of Health & Safety Law in Canada4 items
▾
1
Criminal Code (s.217.1)— federal; creates a legal duty on anyone directing work to take reasonable steps to prevent bodily harm. Breach = criminal negligence causing bodily harm or death (can result in imprisonment).
2
Canada Labour Code (CLC) Part II + Canada Health and Safety Regulations (CHSR)— applies to FEDERALLY regulated workplaces (federal property, Aboriginal reserves, federal buildings)
3
Provincial OH&S Statutes— most provinces have separate OH&S legislation; BC combines OH&S with WCB legislation. Enforcement varies (dedicated body like WorkSafeBC/WorkSafe NB vs Ministry of Labour).
4
Other Industry-Specific Safety Statutes— e.g. Canada Transportation Act, maritime/airline regulations, provincial mining-specific OH&S regulations
3
Criminal Code s.217.1 — Key Points3 items
▾
1
Applies to anyone who UNDERTAKES or has AUTHORITY to direct how work is done— not just employers; supervisors and individuals can be personally charged
2
Duty: take reasonable steps to prevent bodily harm to THAT person or ANY OTHER person
3
A company can be found negligent through COMBINED acts/omissions of multiple representatives— not just one individual's conduct
Key precedents: R. v. Transpavé (2008) — first corporate conviction under s.217.1; $110,000 fine after a worker was fatally crushed (safety mechanism deactivated + inadequate training). Domenico Fantini (Ontario) — construction supervisor who failed to direct sloping/shoring before a fatal trench collapse had criminal charges withdrawn in exchange for guilty pleas under OH&S legislation + $50,000 fine. Only ~10 prosecutions under s.217.1 to date.

8 Accident Prevention Measures8 items
▾
1
Develop, implement, enforce, and audit safety plans— organizational and project-specific
2
Provide proper training and supervision of all employees
3
Maintain clear records of safety-related activities (including training)
4
Enforce appropriate discipline for OH&S violations
5
Clearly delineate authority and responsibility— including identification of the prime contractor/constructor and supervisory roles
6
Implement proper communication procedures and protocols
7
Provide appropriate resources and proper goal-setting
8
Take appropriate steps upon identifying an unsafe condition to prevent an accident
OH&S compliance = MINIMUM, not maximum: Other codes (e.g. National Building Code) must ALSO be followed. If following OH&S code would still leave an unsafe condition, the professional must apply a HIGHER standard using judgment. (BC case: an engineering inspector who should have noticed inadequate shoring cages was penalized alongside the owner/GC/developer — supervisory responsibility extends to anyone positioned to identify hazards.)
5
Prime Contractor / Constructor — Key Rules4 items
▾
1
Default = the OWNER— unless designated otherwise
2
Owner may designate ONE (and only one) prime contractor/constructor by agreement— designation can change at milestones (e.g. substantial completion)
3
Owners CANNOT contract out of their own OH&S obligations— but CAN transfer the prime contractor/constructor ROLE to a qualified party
4
No designation OR multiple simultaneous designations → defaults back to the OWNER— designating TWO prime contractors at once makes the owner responsible for any OH&S breaches
6
5 Steps When an Accident Occurs5 items
▾
1
Minimize the effects— arrange medical attention, eliminate hazards immediately
2
Fulfill reporting obligations— statutory AND contractual, per the applicable safety plan
3
Preserve evidence— including photographs
4
Seek legal/professional advice BEFORE giving statements to investigators— where possible. Witnesses immediately after an accident may volunteer guilt/fear-driven statements that become foundational to regulator findings.
5
Conduct internal investigation— determine cause (with legal counsel if appropriate) and take steps to prevent recurrence
Investigation rights: In some jurisdictions, people interviewed about an accident may have the right to have another person present. Companies should cooperate with regulators but balance this with employees' rights/responsibilities.
7
OH&S Regulator Enforcement Tools3 items
▾
1
Stop-work orders
2
Compliance orders requiring immediate remedial action
3
Fines and penalties— a company's OH&S RECORD affects future fines (repeat offences increase dramatically). Examples: Alberta companies fined $350,000 (2007); BC company fined $297,000+ (2007).
8
Workers' Compensation — The "Historic Trade-Off"2 sides
▾
1
Workers GIVE UP— the right to sue their employer (and other registered employers/employees) for workplace injuries
2
Workers GAIN— no-fault compensation regardless of employer's fault OR ability to pay
1
Employers GIVE UP— must contribute to mandatory insurance scheme (per-employee, risk-rated; safety record can affect premiums in some provinces)
2
Employers GAIN— immunity from potentially crippling lawsuits — applies to ALL registered employers/employees in a multi-employer workplace, not just the direct employer
Scope limit: WCB protection does NOT apply OUTSIDE Canada — employees working abroad CAN sue their Canadian employer (separate insurance should be obtained). Tort claims remain available for people injured while NOT working (Ch.12 applies).

Ethical Resolution of Safety vs Schedule Conflicts1 rule
▾
Rule: If a professional discovers a safety issue (e.g. inadequate cross-bracing during a formwork inspection before a concrete pour) and the client pressures them to proceed despite cost/delay concerns, codes of ethics REQUIRE the dilemma to be resolved in favour of PROTECTING SAFETY — never relax standards to avoid delay costs (see Ch.3).
