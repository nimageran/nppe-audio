3 Principles Applied in Contract Interpretation3 items
▾
1
Overriding Principle — Objective Intention of the Parties— what a reasonable person looking at the contract from the outside would have intended; NOT what the actual parties subjectively thought
2
Canons of Contract Construction— 8 specific principles courts use to determine objective intention (see below)
3
Introduction of Additional Evidence— extrinsic evidence may be admitted in limited circumstances (e.g. factual matrix, subsequent conduct)
2
8 Canons of Contract Construction8 items
▾
i
Plain and Ordinary Meaning— the "golden rule"; use natural/literal meaning unless it creates absurdity, inconsistency, or repugnancy
ii
Special Meaning— where words have a customary/technical meaning (common in construction), that special meaning prevails; dictionaries, headings, marginal notations, or expert evidence may be used
iii
Reading the Contract as a Whole— each clause read in context of the whole document, not in isolation; conflicting clauses resolved to reflect true intentions
iv
Give Effect to All Parts— no part of a contract should be treated as inoperative, surplus, or meaningless
v
Restriction by Express Provisions— special/supplemental conditions added to standard forms take precedence over general conditions on conflict
vi
Commercial Purpose— interpret to reflect the most commercially reasonable result; courts do not imply terms unless necessary to give effect to the parties' intentions
vii
Context— broad/general words are not interpreted as broadly as they appear in isolation; meaning based on the context in which they are used
viii
Contra Proferentem— where ambiguous with two interpretations, courts choose the one that FAVOURS the party who did NOT write the contract
3
Parol Evidence Rule — 1 Rule + 2 Exceptions3 items
▾
The Rule
–
Where a contract is entirely written and clear, extrinsic evidence is NOT admissible— to add to, vary, or contradict the written words
2 Key Exceptions
1
Factual Matrix— evidence of the factual background known to the parties at or before the date of the contract is allowed
2
Subsequent Conduct— parties' conduct after signing may help interpret ambiguous contractual language
Also: parol evidence rule does not apply when fraud or mistake is alleged.
