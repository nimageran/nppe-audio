3
Bond vs Insurance — Key Differences4 items
▾
1
Indemnity from the principal— sureties require the principal (and often its shareholders) to indemnify the surety; insurers generally do not require this from the insured
2
Must be in writing— surety contracts must be in writing to be enforceable; not all contracts generally require this
3
Original document required— to enforce a bond, the obligee must have the ORIGINAL; a photocopy is worthless. Keep it secure.
4
Three-party structure— a bond always involves principal, surety, and obligee; insurance is typically two-party
Both share the right of subrogation — after paying out, the surety/insurer can assume the obligee's/insured's rights to recover from a responsible third party.

3 Types of Bonds Used on Construction Projects3 items · with CCDC forms
▾
CCDC 220
Bid Bond— guarantees the contractor will enter into the contract if its bid is accepted; typically 10% of bid amount
CCDC 221
Performance Bond— guarantees the contractor's performance of the construction contract; face value typically 50% of contract value
CCDC 222
Labour and Material (L&M) Payment Bond— guarantees payment to subcontractors/suppliers/workers; one-tier (claimants = those with a direct contract with the contractor)
Outside construction: fidelity bonds and performance bonds are also common (e.g. environmental clean-up work).
5
Bid Bond — Key Mechanics3 items
▾
1
Primary obligation guaranteed— that the contractor will ENTER INTO THE CONTRACT if its bid is accepted
2
Payout if contractor refuses— surety pays the difference between the defaulting contractor's bid and the bid actually accepted, up to the FACE VALUE of the bond
3
Surety's position ≤ principal's position— the surety can never be in a WORSE position than the principal. Any defence available to the contractor (e.g. valid bid revocation) is available to the surety too.
Owner's duty to mitigate: The owner cannot simply accept any bid after a default — it must take the next lowest bid from a qualified contractor (mitigation is implied even where not explicit, as in the CCDC bond). CCDC bid bond claims must be made within 7 months of the bond date.
6
6 Options for the Surety Once the Principal Is in Default6 items
▾
1
Require the principal to remedy the default— principal performs its obligations
2
Complete the contract itself— surety risk: NO financial limit (open-ended), plus takes on warranty obligations
3
Solicit bids for completion— pay obligee the difference between the new bid and amount remaining owed, capped at face value of bond
4
Pay the obligee the face value of the bond
5
Assert a defence and refuse to act
6
"Wait and see"— used when there's genuine dispute over whether the principal was actually in default; avoids becoming a "volunteer"
Volunteer risk: If a surety pays out believing the principal is in default, but it turns out the OBLIGEE was actually in default, the surety becomes a "volunteer" — it cannot recover from the principal under its indemnity. Canadian case law tends to find sureties NOT liable for delay damages (Lac La Ronge), though the issue remains unsettled.
7
5 Defences Available to a Surety Under a Performance Bond5 items
▾
1
Any defence available to the principal— mistake, economic duress, misrepresentation, frustration, lack of consideration, etc.
2
No default has actually occurred— it is a condition precedent that the principal be in default; consultant's "default" finding can be challenged
3
Obligee failed to perform its own obligations (e.g. non-payment)— condition precedent: obligee must have performed ITS obligations first
4
Late notice of claim— if notice deprives the surety of its ability to mitigate or act
5
Significant change in risk— material change in scope, type of work, schedule, or substantial overpayment to the principal can discharge the surety's obligations. Minor changes (normal in construction) do NOT count.
Time limitation: CCDC 222-2002 requires suit within 2 years of the date final payment under the bond is due. Prudent practice: obtain the surety's prior approval before making significant project changes.

Payment Bonds — 2 Common Situations2 items
▾
1
Owner requires a payment bond from the contractor— protects against liens by unpaid subcontractors/suppliers/workers
2
General contractor requires a payment bond from major subcontractors
Trust structure: The obligee is named as TRUSTEE for claimants. A claimant under a one-tier CCDC bond = anyone with a DIRECT contract with the contractor (sub-subcontractors are excluded). Surety can raise defences such as deficiencies/set-offs, the bond's face-value cap, and any applicable limitation period.
9
Lien Bond vs L&M Payment Bond2 items
▾
1
L&M Payment Bond— put in place at the START of the contract; protects against future non-payment claims
2
Lien Bond— put in place AFTER a lien has already been filed; substitutes for the land as security so the lien can be removed from title. Surety pays the lien claimant only if the lien is valid AND the principal doesn't pay.
