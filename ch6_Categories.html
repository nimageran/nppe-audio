5 Requirements for an Enforceable Contract5 items
▾
1
Offer and Acceptance— a valid offer capable of acceptance, and an unequivocal acceptance of that offer
2
Consideration— something of value exchanged by each party; must flow both ways
3
Legal Capacity— parties must have the legal ability to contract
4
Intention to Create Legal Relations— parties must intend to be legally bound
5
Lawful Purpose— contract cannot be for an illegal purpose

2
Implied Terms— never discussed but taken for granted; implied by law or custom

Price
3
Time for performance
4
Scope of work

6 Grounds for Voiding / Making a Contract Voidable6 items
▾
1
Mistake— must be material, mutual, and existing at time of agreement
2
Misrepresentation— untrue factual statement that induced the other party to contract (innocent, negligent, or fraudulent)
3
Duress— improper pressure or coercion; party had no real choice but to comply
4
Unconscionability— contract so one-sided that enforcing it would be offensive; requires extreme inequality of bargaining power
5
Frustration— unforeseen event makes performance impossible or of no value; risk must not have been originally allocated to one party
6
Impossibility— same doctrine as frustration; often used interchangeably
6
3 Types of Misrepresentation3 items
▾
1
Innocent Misrepresentation— false statement, no intent to deceive, not careless. Hardest to get damages for.
2
Negligent Misrepresentation— false statement made without due care or skill. Damages generally available.
3
Fraudulent Misrepresentation— party knows the statement is false. Easiest to rescind contract and get damages.
Also: Misrepresentation by omission — misrepresentation created by failure to disclose information (e.g. owner withholding subsurface data from bidders).
7
3 Requirements for a Mistake to Void a Contract3 items · ALL must be present
▾
1
Material— significant, not trivial
2
Mutual— made by BOTH parties (not just one)
3
Existing at time of agreement— a mistake made AFTER the agreement is insufficient
8
2 Types of Consideration That Do NOT Work2 items
▾
1
Past Consideration— promising to do something you were already legally obligated to do. NOT valid (e.g. window installer demanding extra money to do work already contracted)
2
A Promise Without Consideration (Gift)— courts do not enforce a promise to give a gift. Exception: seal on a written document can act as consideration.
9
3 Problems with Letters of Intent / Agreements to Agree3 items
▾
1
Not all parties may know it is unenforceable
2
Both parties may commence performance without a formal contract
3
If worded as an acceptance, it may be treated as one by a court— even though many contract terms are still unclear
Rule: An agreement to agree in the future is NOT an enforceable contract.
10
Quantum Meruit — 2 Key Conditions2 items
▾
1
The other party received the benefit of the work
2
No valid contract exists that applies to the work— if a change order provision covers it, quantum meruit does NOT apply
Quantum meruit = "the amount it is worth." Payment approximating the value of work performed including reasonable profit. Also applies to constructive changes — changes the owner refuses to acknowledge.
11
Offer & Acceptance — 4 Key Rules4 items
▾
1
Counter-offer = Rejection— a counter-offer terminates the previous offer; offeree cannot later accept the original offer unless revived
2
Revocation must be communicated— offeror can revoke any time before acceptance, but must communicate the revocation
3
Postal Acceptance Rule— when regular mail is used, acceptance is deemed communicated when placed into the mail system
4
Acceptance by conduct— beginning performance of the work constitutes acceptance (e.g. consultant starting site inspection)
