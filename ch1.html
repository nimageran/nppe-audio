Visualization Logic: The Provincial box is given the heaviest visual weight deliberately, because the chapter is explicit that this textbook's content overwhelmingly falls on the PROVINCIAL side — contracts, negligence, liens, and professional regulation are all provincial matters, which is exactly why provinces can and do have genuinely different rules from each other on these topics (Alberta lien legislation differs from Ontario's, as the chapter notes directly). The Exceptions box deserves special attention precisely because it's counterintuitive — labour law and competition law FEEL like classic private-law subjects that should be provincial, but they're explicitly carved out as federal in specific circumstances (labour law only for federally regulated industries like airlines/railroads; competition law as a blanket federal matter regardless of industry). Recognizing these two exceptions prevents the common mistake of assuming "private law topic" always means "automatically provincial."

Visualization Logic: The NotPrivate branch is the single most important fact in this diagram because it's the one most commonly misunderstood — people frequently invoke "the Charter" when complaining about discrimination by an employer, a landlord, or another private business, but the Charter has zero application to those purely private relationships; it only restrains GOVERNMENT action. The Gap diamond completes the picture by showing this isn't actually a hole in Canadian rights protection overall, it's a deliberate DIVISION OF LABOUR between two different legal instruments — the Charter constrains the state, while separate provincial human rights legislation independently constrains private parties, meaning a person facing discrimination from a private employer needs to look to their PROVINCIAL human rights statute, not the Charter, for a remedy. This distinction connects directly back to Chapter 21's employment law content, where the same point resurfaces in the specific context of workplace discrimination claims.

Visualization Logic: The Theory diamond captures a subtle but genuinely important point the chapter makes explicitly — there's an official, constitutional FICTION that judges only "interpret" statutes rather than "create" law, but the chapter is candid that this distinction breaks down in practice, since the very act of resolving an ambiguous statutory phrase unavoidably generates new rights and remedies that didn't exist in a clearly articulated form before that interpretation. The Subordinate branch deserves attention because it's directly relevant to professional practice: a provincial engineering association's BYLAWS aren't created by the legislature itself, but they still carry real legal weight (breach can create liability) because the ENABLING STATUTE granted the association the authority to create them — this is exactly the same mechanism by which municipalities create binding bylaws. The NoStatute box at the bottom is worth connecting directly to Chapter 6's content: basic contract formation concepts like consideration have NO governing statute defining them at all in most common law provinces — the entire framework comes from centuries of accumulated case law, which is precisely why Chapter 6 had to rely so heavily on named cases (Smith v. Dawson, etc.) rather than citing a Consideration Act.

Visualization Logic: The StillSame diamond is placed deliberately in the middle of the diagram to prevent an overcorrection — once someone learns Quebec uses a "totally different legal system," it's easy to wrongly assume EVERYTHING is different there, but the chapter is explicit that federal-jurisdiction matters (criminal law, patent law, and other federal areas) are governed by the SAME common law throughout Quebec as everywhere else in Canada. The difference is narrower and more specific than "Quebec's whole legal system": it's specifically PRIVATE LAW relationships — contracts, torts, employment, business organizations, insurance, bonds — that fall under the Civil Code instead of common law. The final Practical diamond delivers the chapter's own reassuring, practically-oriented conclusion: despite the structural difference in HOW the law is organized (comprehensive code versus accumulated case law), the actual SUBSTANCE of basic legal principles ends up fairly similar in practice — different enough to need separate diligence when practising in Quebec specifically, but not so different that everything learned elsewhere in this textbook becomes useless there.

Visualization Logic: The two-gate structure exists specifically to correct a natural but incorrect assumption — that getting legal authorization to WORK in a country (the TN visa) is the only hurdle to actually DOING professional work there. This same two-gate pattern is worth recognizing as a transferable concept: it mirrors the chapter's own point elsewhere about distinguishing the right to USE a title from the right to PRACTISE a profession (Chapter 2's two-pillars structure) — immigration status and professional licensing are simply two more independent gates layered on top of that same general principle when working across an international border.

Visualization Logic: Breaking down both citation formats side by side, piece by piece, turns what looks like an arbitrary string of numbers and abbreviations into a fully decodable reference system — useful not just for this exam but for actually being able to look up any case cited throughout this entire textbook series. The key practical difference between the two styles is what they tell you to search for: the older style points you to a PHYSICAL location (a specific volume and page number of a specific printed reporting series like the S.C.R.), while the newer "neutral citation" style points you to a court's own sequential decision numbering for that year, which works regardless of which printed or online reporting service you're using to actually access the text. Recognizing this format also has a practical safety benefit the chapter stresses separately: a non-lawyer reading a single cited case (as you'll encounter dozens of times throughout this textbook series) should never assume that one case's outcome perfectly predicts how an unrelated, even superficially similar, fact pattern will be decided — case law is fact-dependent, and these citations exist precisely so a lawyer can pull the FULL case and check exactly which specific facts drove that particular result.
