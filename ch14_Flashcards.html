Litigation
Click to flip
The Story: You sue someone in the official, public court system.

The Rule: It is the default system, but it is incredibly slow, entirely public, and your case will be decided by a judge who likely knows absolutely nothing about engineering.
Public, Slow, Expensive
Arbitration
Click to flip
The Story: You want a binding decision, but you do not want to go to public court.

The Rule: A private trial where you hire an expert (like a senior engineer) to be the judge. Their decision is legally binding and extremely difficult to appeal.
Private, Binding, Expert Judge
Mediation
Click to flip
The Story: Two parties are fighting but want to find a compromise.

The Rule: You hire a neutral third party to help you negotiate. The mediator has zero power to force a decision. If you do not agree, you just walk away.
Assisted Negotiation (No Power)
Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR)
Click to flip
The Concept: Any method used to resolve a fight without going to formal Litigation.

The Rule: This includes Negotiation, Mediation, and Arbitration. Modern contracts usually force you to try ADR before you are legally allowed to sue.
Anything But Court
Dispute Resolution Board (DRB)
Click to flip
The Story: You are building a massive $500M dam. If you stop construction to fight in court, everyone goes bankrupt.

The Rule: A pre-selected panel of neutral experts visits the site regularly and solves disputes immediately so the work never stops.
Solve It Fast, Keep Building
The Expert Witness (Duty)
Click to flip
The Trap: Your client pays you $500/hour to testify in court about a bridge collapse.

The Rule: Your absolute, strict legal duty is to tell the objective truth to the court. You are legally forbidden from acting as a "hired gun" or advocate for your client.
Loyalty to the Court, Not the Client
Fact vs. Expert Witness
Click to flip
The Rule: A regular Fact Witness can only testify about what they physically saw or heard (e.g., "The beam bent").

The Catch: An Expert Witness is the *only* person in the courtroom legally allowed to give an opinion (e.g., "The beam bent because the design was negligent").
Only Experts Give Opinions
"Without Prejudice"
Click to flip
The Story: You email the client and offer $10,000 to drop the lawsuit, marking it "Without Prejudice."

The Rule: If they reject it and go to court, they cannot show that email to the judge as proof you admitted fault. It protects settlement negotiations.
Settlement Offers are Secret
Document Discovery
Click to flip
The Story: You are sued, and you decide to delete an embarrassing email where you admitted a mistake.

The Rule: This is highly illegal. In litigation, both sides are legally forced to share all relevant documents, even the ones that completely destroy their own case.
