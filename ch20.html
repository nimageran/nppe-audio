CHAPTER 20: DEEP LABOUR LAW VISUALIZATION

1. Employment Law vs. Labour Law (The Agency Model)

The law explicitly divides the workplace into two distinct legal realms based on union representation[cite: 1].

Visualization Logic: This illustrates the fundamental shift in power and communication when a union is formed[cite: 1].

  • Employment Law: Governs employee-employer relationships where there is no union representation[cite: 1].
  • Labour Law: Governs union-management and employee-union relationships, primarily under provincial legislation[cite: 1]. Section 2(d) of the Charter of Rights and Freedoms guarantees freedom of association, protecting the right to collectively bargain[cite: 1].
  • The Agency Shift: Once a union is certified, it becomes the sole agent for the employees[cite: 1]. The employer is legally prohibited from bypassing the union to negotiate directly with a represented employee[cite: 1].

2. The Worker Classification Spectrum

Labour boards must categorize every worker to determine if they are legally allowed to join a union[cite: 1].

Visualization Logic: This maps out who is protected by labour legislation[cite: 1].

  • The Control Test: An independent contractor determines their own means and methods, while an employee does not[cite: 1]. Independent contractors are legally prohibited from colluding to fix prices, meaning they cannot unionize[cite: 1].
  • The Dependent Contractor: A shaded middle ground. They may provide their own vehicles or tools, but they rely on one source for their income (economic dependence), making them eligible for unionization[cite: 1].
  • Professional Ethics Override: In some provinces, professionals (like engineers) may find themselves in a union[cite: 1]. However, if a strike is called that endangers public safety, the professional's Code of Ethics strictly prevails over the union's strike rules[cite: 1].

3. Union Security & The Rand Formula

To survive, unions need financial security. The law permits various models to ensure unions are funded and membership is maintained[cite: 1].

Visualization Logic: This compares the structures of workplace union enforcement[cite: 1].

  • The Free Rider Problem: When a union secures a raise, even non-union employees benefit from it[cite: 1]. The Rand Formula forces non-members to pay union dues as a compromise so they do not financially exploit the union's bargaining efforts[cite: 1]. The Supreme Court ruled this does not violate the Charter[cite: 1].
  • Closed Shops in Canada: Unlike the United States, where "Closed Shops" are heavily restricted as an infringement on freedom of association, Canadian courts still permit them[cite: 1].

4. Multi-Union Construction & Stoppages

Unlike a factory with one giant union, construction sites have dozens of separate "trade" or "craft" unions operating simultaneously[cite: 1]. This creates massive coordination risks[cite: 1].

Visualization Logic: This maps out how conflicts manifest on a job site[cite: 1].

  • Jurisdictional Disputes: A conflict where two or more unions claim that a specific piece of work belongs exclusively to their members[cite: 1]. Because one small trade striking can shut down the entire site, labour legislation explicitly prohibits work stoppages caused by jurisdictional disputes[cite: 1].
  • Strikes & Lockouts: These are the ultimate weapons in a labour dispute, but they are strictly illegal as long as an active collective agreement is in force[cite: 1].
  • Replacement Workers: Some provinces (like British Columbia) legally prohibit employers from hiring replacement workers (scabs) to do the jobs of employees who are on a legal strike or locked out[cite: 1].

5. The "Corporate Charade" Shield

Employers cannot simply shuffle paperwork or change their company name to destroy a union[cite: 1]. The law pierces through corporate structures to protect bargaining rights[cite: 1].

Successor Employer Rule: Did they buy the business?] Test --> T2[2. Common Employer Rule: Same Directors & Control?] T1 -->|Yes| Bind[Union Agreement SURVIVES & Binds New Company] T2 -->|Yes| Bind style Old fill:#f1f5f9,stroke:#64748b style New fill:#fee2e2,stroke:#dc2626 style Test fill:#fef3c7,stroke:#d97706 style Bind fill:#166534,color:#fff,stroke-width:3px

Visualization Logic: This diagrams the outcome of the Vulcan Packaging Case Study[cite: 1].

  • Successor Employers: If a corporation purchases a business or its substantial assets, they inherit the obligation to bargain with the existing union[cite: 1].
  • Common Employers: If separate companies are running related activities under common control or direction (same key officers and directors), the board will treat them as a single employer[cite: 1].
  • The Vulcan Case: In Vulcan Packaging, the owners pushed the company into bankruptcy, then a group controlled by the exact same people bought the assets and tried to operate non-union[cite: 1]. The board ruled this a "corporate charade" designed to get rid of the union, and legally bound the new companies to the old collective agreement[cite: 1].

6. Grievances, Layoffs, and Arbitrator Power

The rules of discipline and termination under a collective agreement are drastically different from standard common law[cite: 1].

Visualization Logic: This contrasts collective agreement protections against normal employment law[cite: 1].

  • Grievance vs. Interest Arbitration: Grievance Arbitration resolves disputes over the interpretation of an existing agreement[cite: 1]. Interest Arbitration is used to formulate a brand new collective agreement when negotiations break down[cite: 1].
  • Layoffs & Seniority: A layoff is temporary[cite: 1]. Employers cannot pick and choose who stays; they are legally forced to lay off the most junior employees first, and re-hire them in reverse order[cite: 1].
  • Arbitrator Supremacy: Under normal common law, an employer can fire anyone without cause as long as they pay severance (reasonable notice)[cite: 1]. Under a collective agreement, firing without "just cause" is essentially banned[cite: 1]. Furthermore, a labour arbitrator has the power to order an employer to physically reinstate a fired worker—a power that standard common law courts do not have[cite: 1].
