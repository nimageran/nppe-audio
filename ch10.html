2. The Contract A / Contract B Formation Mechanism

This is the single most important mechanical concept in Canadian tendering law. Submitting a compliant bid doesn't just put you "in the running" — it instantly creates a small, binding contract of its own. This diagram traces exactly how many contracts actually form in a typical tender.

Visualization Logic: The key number to internalize from this diagram is MANY versus ONE — every single compliant bid creates its OWN separate Contract A (so three compliant bidders means three separate Contract A's exist simultaneously), but only ONE Contract B ever forms in the entire process, between the buyer and whichever bidder is ultimately chosen. This explains why a buyer who improperly handles ANY bidder's submission — not just the eventual winner's — can face liability: even an unsuccessful bidder had a real, binding Contract A with specific implied terms, and breaching THAT contract (e.g., by accepting a non-compliant bid over theirs, per diagram 3) is an independent wrong, separate entirely from who eventually won Contract B. Bidder 5's box deliberately shows the other critical fact: non-compliance (even something as simple as being late) means Contract A never forms at all — that bidder has zero contractual standing in the process.

The danger box under Option 2 is worth sitting with because it's a genuine trap for poorly advised buyers: simply writing "no contract is formed by submitting a bid" into your documents does NOT guarantee a court will respect that disclaimer if the REST of the document's structure (mandatory criteria, fixed deadlines, binding evaluation process) behaves exactly like a real Contract A relationship anyway.

4. The Fairness Paradox — Process vs Application of Process

This is a subtle distinction that the chapter explicitly warns is "often misunderstood." Canadian courts require fairness in ONE specific place and explicitly refuse to require it in another, adjacent place — and getting this backwards leads to a wrong answer on the exam.

Visualization Logic: The diagram deliberately places these two diamonds side by side with OPPOSITE answers, because that contrast IS the entire lesson — fairness law in this area isn't a single blanket rule, it's a rule that applies asymmetrically depending on WHICH stage of the process you're asking about. The "DesignFree" box might feel counterintuitive at first (surely a buyer favoring contractors they already know is "unfair"?), but the WhyFree box resolves that tension: any possible set of criteria advantages SOME bidders and disadvantages others (favoring history is unfair to newcomers; ignoring history is unfair to established relationships), so courts have simply declined to police that impossible-to-satisfy standard. The ApplyBound box is where actual legal exposure lives — once the rules are published, deviating from them (even slightly, even with good intentions) is the real source of buyer liability, which is precisely why Figure 10-3's tip about using subjective AND objective criteria matters: the criteria themselves can be whatever the buyer wants, but once chosen, they become binding.

5. The Privilege Clause Boundary

A privilege clause ("the lowest or any bid may not be accepted") sounds like it gives a buyer total discretion. This diagram shows the actual boundary of that discretion, which is much narrower than the clause's wording implies on its face.

Visualization Logic: The "Looks" box at the top is placed deliberately as the WRONG intuition, because the chapter is explicit that this statement "cannot always be taken at face value" — the clause's broad wording doesn't actually grant unlimited discretion, it just protects the buyer from liability for NOT picking the lowest bidder, provided the real reason was disclosed somewhere in the tender documents. The boundary diamond is the crux of the whole concept: a buyer with an undisclosed preference (say, favoring local contractors) who relies SOLELY on the privilege clause's broad wording to justify that choice is still exposed to a breach claim, because the privilege clause doesn't retroactively excuse using criteria nobody was told about. The practical drafting lesson living inside this diagram: if a buyer has ANY policy that will influence bid selection beyond pure price, that policy needs to be written into the tender documents explicitly — the privilege clause is a shield for disclosed discretion, not a blank check.

6. Bid Shopping — Improper but Not Illegal

This diagram exists to resolve a genuine paradox: bid shopping is widely considered unethical and "improper," yet the chapter is explicit that in many cases it gives rise to NO legal remedy at all. Seeing why requires tracing the actual legal theories available.

Visualization Logic: The split at the very top between "Ethics" and "Legal" is the entire resolution to the paradox — something can be simultaneously WIDELY condemned as improper AND legally unenforceable, because Canadian courts require any claim to be slotted into an EXISTING legal theory (contract, estoppel, unjust enrichment), and bid shopping doesn't naturally fit cleanly into any of them. The Ron Brown branch traces exactly why the most intuitive theory (an implied term against shopping) failed: to be "custom and practice," a practice has to be nearly universal, and the plaintiff's own expert witnesses undermined that argument by confirming bid shopping happens regularly — ironically, the WIDESPREAD nature of the unethical practice is precisely what defeated the legal argument against it. The "ButStill" box at the bottom is worth remembering as a separate, narrower consequence: even without a clean cause of action against the SHOPPING party, a bidder who shops a SUPPLIER'S price can lose the ability to legally hold that supplier to their original quote — a smaller but real legal teeth attached to the practice.

Visualization Logic: The two-gate AND structure at the top (obvious AND material, not obvious OR material) is the foundational rule — failing either gate alone defeats the whole withdrawal claim, regardless of how the bidder feels about it. The context diamond branching off the materiality gate captures something genuinely tricky for exam purposes: there is NO fixed percentage threshold that universally defines "material" — the SAME 10% spread that would be glaringly unusual in concrete work might be completely unremarkable in painting, meaning materiality has to be assessed relative to normal variance within that specific trade, not against some universal numeric rule. The JudgmentCheck diamond at the bottom captures the chapter's geotechnical drilling example and is the distinction most likely to trip someone up: choosing a cheaper METHOD that then turns out to be inadequate or more expensive than expected isn't a "mistake" in the legal sense at all — it's a risk the bidder knowingly chose to take by selecting that approach, and the law won't rescue a bidder from a bad judgment call the way it might rescue them from a genuine calculation error.

Visualization Logic: The very first diamond (was the sub NAMED) is the single biggest fork in this entire area of law, and the Ron Brown branch shows exactly why: a subcontractor whose PRICE was used but who was never actually IDENTIFIED has no leverage at all — there's no "mutuality of obligation," meaning neither side can force the other into a deal, which is why the prime contractor in that case was free to simply do the mechanical work itself using Ron Brown's price as a benchmark without ever being bound to actually hire Ron Brown. The TimingIssue diamond beneath the Crystallize branch adds a second layer most people miss: even once a subcontract HAS crystallized via naming and unconditional acceptance, an EXPIRED price (on either the prime's side or the sub's side) breaks that crystallization entirely, converting what would have been an acceptance into a fresh, unbound offer needing new agreement all around. The StillBound box is the practical safety valve in normal circumstances — sub contractors don't need to be notified the INSTANT a buyer accepts a bid for the subcontract to be valid, "reasonable time" gives some breathing room, but the prime contractor can't sit on that acceptance indefinitely and still expect instant mobilization from a subcontractor who was never told anything happened.

Visualization Logic: Every single mechanical feature in this diagram exists specifically to close off a different bid-shopping tactic, which is worth noticing as a pattern rather than memorizing as isolated rules. The sealed envelope physically prevents a GC from peeking at one sub's price and walking it over to a competing sub for a lower counter-offer. The minimum-deposit-period rule (e.g., 1 day before the GC's own deadline) directly counters the "everyone waits until the last possible hour" dynamic the chapter describes elsewhere, which otherwise leaves GCs no time to evaluate prices and creates exactly the chaotic, frantic last-minute scrambling depositories were built to eliminate. The SelfBid box addresses a sneaky workaround some GCs would otherwise use — wanting to perform work with their own crew specifically because it's cheaper than any outside sub — by forcing the GC to submit ITS OWN number into the same sealed, blind competition, so self-performance only wins if it's genuinely the most competitive price, not just a default option the GC reaches for whenever it's convenient.

The "Window" box deserves the heaviest visual weight in the whole diagram because the 10-day deadline is brutally short compared to ordinary limitation periods elsewhere in this course (commonly 2 years for general contract/tort claims) — a supplier who suspects something is wrong cannot afford to sit on that instinct and investigate slowly, they have to either act almost immediately or lose their right to complain through this specific tribunal route entirely. The bottom box reinforces why that urgency matters so much: this isn't simply ONE OF several options, it's largely a REPLACEMENT for ordinary court litigation in this specific context, so missing the 10-day window doesn't just lose one avenue, it can close off the primary remedy altogether.
