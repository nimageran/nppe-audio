The Constitution — 3 Primary Features
Source of all legal authority in Canada
▾
3 Primary Features of the Constitution
Division of Powers
Assigns legislative rights between federal Parliament and provincial legislatures. Federal law prevails on direct conflicts.
Creation of the Courts
Establishes the hierarchy of superior courts across Canada.
Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms
Individual rights for all Canadians and many non-citizens. Applies to government acts only — NOT private parties.
Federal Jurisdiction (examples)
National defence · Postal service · Broadcasting · Criminal law · Patent law
Also: Competition Act (bid rigging, unfair trade practices). Labour law for federal industries (airlines, railways, post office).
Provincial Jurisdiction (examples)
Property rights · Contracts · Negligence · Construction liens · A/E/G practice laws
Most private law. Laws differ province to province (e.g. lien legislation differs between AB and ON).
Charter — Prohibited Grounds of Discrimination
Race · Ethnic origin · Religion · Disability · Age
Charter applies to government acts only. Human rights statutes in each province cover private interactions.
Key RuleWherever there is a direct conflict between a federal and a provincial statute — federal law prevails.

4 levels from Provincial Court to Supreme Court of Canada

Court Hierarchy (top to bottom)
LevelName / ExamplesKey Notes
1 — Supreme Court of CanadaSupreme Court of CanadaUltimate appeal. Civil cases need LEAVE — must show national importance. Rarely granted for A/E/G cases.
2 — Courts of AppealBC Court of Appeal, Alta. Court of Appeal, etc.Each province + territory has one. No cross-provincial jurisdiction.
3 — Superior Trial CourtsBC Supreme Court / Alta. Court of Queen's Bench / Ont. Superior CourtJudges appointed by Governor General. Cannot be removed except for misconduct. Have "inherent jurisdiction."

Key RuleNo court in any province has jurisdiction over courts in other provinces. Federal Courts have no jurisdiction over provincial courts.

Creation of Law — 2 Main Sources
Statute law and common law
▾
2 Types of Law
Statute Law
Enacted by federal/provincial legislatures. Must be compatible with Constitution. Judges interpret statutes — in practice this creates new rights. Subordinate legislation = regulations by agencies (engineering associations, municipalities).
Examples: lien rights, joint & several liability, workers' compensation, contract formalities
Common Law
Judge-made law based on precedent. Roots in English equity. All Canadian provinces except Quebec. Judges technically interpret — but in practice create new rights and remedies.
Exception: Quebec and Louisiana use civil law (Napoleonic Code)
Watch OutLaw is dynamic — not static. What is settled law today may not be the rule tomorrow. Case law is fact-specific; a slightly different set of facts can produce a very different result.

Claims, Disputes & Risk Allocation
Types of claims and risk-shifting mechanisms
▾
Common Types of Claims
Negligence
Failure to take reasonable care. No contract needed between parties.
Breach of Contract
Violation of a voluntary agreement. Can sue simultaneously for both negligence AND breach of contract.
Refusal to Pay / Insolvency
File a lien or claim against a labour and material payment bond. Must act quickly — limitation periods apply.
4 Risk-Shifting Mechanisms
Contract Design
Allocate risks between parties through contract terms.
Insurance
Shift risk to a third party — the insurer.
Bonds
Protect against risks of non-performance and non-payment.
Contractual Disclaimers
Attempt to limit or shift liability through contract language.
Watch OutLimitation periods and procedural requirements must be met to preserve legal rights. Missing a deadline can permanently extinguish a claim.

3 Elements to Prove Negligence
1. Foreseeable Harm
Defendant could reasonably have foreseen that the plaintiff (or someone in their position) would suffer harm.
2. Standard of Care Not Met
Defendant did not meet the standard of care expected of someone in their position.
3. Causation
The failure to meet the standard of care was the legal cause of the loss suffered.
Intentional Torts (less common for professionals)

International Law — Key Points
NAFTA, TN visa, tax treaties
▾
Key Distinctions
Right to Work ≠ Right to Practise
TN visa (NAFTA) allows working legally in USA. Still need state licence to practise engineering (e.g. Oregon Board of Registration licence required to practise consulting engineering in Oregon).
Tax Treaties
Prevent double taxation. Canadian income tax may attach even if resident abroad for years. Always consult a tax specialist.
Treaties Only Enforceable if Adopted Domestically
International treaties enforceable in Canada only to the extent adopted through domestic legislation. May also help interpret ambiguous domestic law.

Case Citations — How to Read Them
Format for locating cases in law libraries
▾
Citation Format 1 — Volume-based
Backman v. Canada, [2001] 1 S.C.R. 367
Case name: Backman v. Canada  |  Year: 2001  |  Volume: 1  |  Series: S.C.R. (Supreme Court Reports)  |  Page: 367
Citation Format 2 — Decision number
Martel Building Ltd. v. Canada, 2000 SCC 60
Year: 2000  |  Court: SCC (Supreme Court of Canada)  |  Decision #: 60th decision of that year by that court
Watch OutNever read a single case and assume the same outcome in different circumstances. A slightly different fact can produce a very different result. Always check for the most current case law.
